# Elon Musk - Sandy Munro Interview (2021)

**Date:** February 11, 2021  
**Source:** Munro Live (YouTube)  
**Location:** Tesla Fremont Factory / Virtual  
**Interviewer:** Sandy Munro (Munro & Associates)  
**Guest:** Elon Musk (CEO of Tesla)

---

## About This Interview

Sandy Munro is an automotive manufacturing expert famous for his detailed teardowns of vehicles. His teardowns of Tesla's Model 3 and Model Y revealed both Tesla's innovations and their early manufacturing challenges. This interview was a manufacturing-focused conversation between a legendary automotive engineer and the CEO of the company transforming the industry.

This dialogue was unique because:
- Munro is a manufacturing expert, not a journalist
- The questions were deeply technical
- It was a conversation between equals in engineering knowledge
- Musk opened up about manufacturing philosophy

---

## Manufacturing Philosophy

### Engineering vs. Manufacturing

**Sandy Munro:** Everyone talks about your engineering, but nobody talks about your manufacturing. I think that's a mistake.

**Elon Musk:** The factory is the product. People think we design cars. But just as important is designing the factory that makes the cars.

**Munro:** You've said that before. The machine that builds the machine.

**Musk:** Exactly. If you want to make a million units of something, the factory is more important than the product design. Anyone can design a great car if they only have to make ten of them. Making a million—that's hard.

### Cost Down Philosophy

**Munro:** How do you think about cost reduction?

**Musk:** We obsess about it. Every component, every process—we're always asking, is this necessary? Can we simplify it?

**Munro:** Give me an example.

**Musk:** Look at the wiring in a car. Traditional automakers have thousands of different wire harnesses—different lengths, different connectors, for every vehicle configuration. It's a nightmare. We designed a completely different architecture. Instead of 3,000 unique parts, we have 100.

**Munro:** The zonal architecture. I've seen it. That's brilliant.

**Musk:** And it's necessary. We're going to need 20 million vehicles per year. You can't have that complexity at that scale.

---

## Castings and Gigapress

### Single Piece Castings

**Munro:** The rear casting on the Model Y. That's a game changer.

**Musk:** Yeah, it replaces 70 parts with one.

**Munro:** I've never seen anything like it. The whole rear floor, everything goes into this one aluminum casting.

**Musk:** The tolerances are tough. You're casting something that big, and if the die has any warp, you're in trouble. We worked with Idra on the gigapress. They thought we were crazy.

**Munro:** How big is that machine?

**Musk:** It's a 6,000-ton press. We have even bigger ones now for the front casting—8,000 tons. The Cybertruck will have a single-piece exoskeleton. Everything is a casting.

**Munro:** The industry is going to copy this. Everyone is going to have to.

**Musk:** They should. It's obvious in retrospect. Why weld 70 parts together when you can cast one?

### Quality Control

**Munro:** But what about quality? What happens if there's a defect in that massive casting?

**Musk:** You have to get the process right. The die has to be perfect. The thermal management has to be perfect. Every casting is tested. We know immediately if there's an issue.

**Munro:** And if there is?

**Musk:** You refine the process. But once you get it right, it's actually more consistent than welding 70 different parts from five different suppliers.

## The Tesla Production System

### First Principles Manufacturing

**Munro:** How do you approach manufacturing differently?

**Musk:** Traditional automakers have this whole supply chain of tier 1 and tier 2 suppliers. They don't really know how anything is made. They assemble components other people make.

**Munro:** And you?

**Musk:** We bring everything in-house. We want to understand every process. The battery cell, the battery pack, the motor, the controller—we design and build everything.

**Munro:** That gives you control.

**Musk:** It gives us speed. When something isn't working, we fix it immediately. We don't call a supplier and wait six months for a replacement component.

### The Toyota Influence

**Munro:** You've studied Toyota Production System.

**Musk:** Of course. Toyota is the best in the world at manufacturing. We have enormous respect for what they've built. But we also see where we can improve.

**Munro:** Like what?

**Musk:** Toyota is amazing at eliminating waste. They're less good at embracing technology that dramatically changes the process. They're incremental. We're willing to start over.

**Munro:** The gigapress is an example.

**Musk:** Exactly. No one in automotive was casting parts that size. It was unthinkable. We just asked, why not?

## Battery Technology

### The 4680 Cell

**Munro:** The 4680 battery. You're making your own cells now.

**Musk:** We have to. The battery is the constraint. If we want to scale, we need to control battery production.

**Munro:** But that's a huge investment.

**Musk:** It is. But it's also where all the innovation happens. The cell chemistry, the dry electrode process, the manufacturing—it's all connected.

**Munro:** What about the tabless design?

**Musk:** It solves so many problems. The tabs create bottlenecks. They create heat. They make manufacturing slower. Tabless removes all of that.

### Cell Manufacturing

**Munro:** How far along are you with the 4680 production?

**Musk:** We're in pilot production. It's working, but ramping is hard. Manufacturing cells at scale is maybe the hardest thing we've ever done.

**Munro:** Harder than rockets?

**Musk:** Different. Rockets are hard because of physics. Cells are hard because of chemistry and manufacturing complexity combined. You need to be perfect at a billion units a year.

## Manufacturing Challenges

### The Model 3 Ramp

**Munro:** The Model 3 ramp was brutal. I saw it.

**Musk:** Yeah. We almost died. We were building parts of the factory while we were trying to build cars. The automation didn't work. We had to rip it out and redo it.

**Munro:** I was surprised you didn't give up.

**Musk:** We don't give up. But I will say—I wouldn't recommend that experience to anyone. I aged ten years that year.

**Munro:** What did you learn?

**Musk:** Don't automate too fast. Don't skip steps. The hard way is the shortcut.

### The Fremont Experience

**Munro:** You've got NUMMI history there—the old GM-Toyota plant.

**Musk:** NUMMI was the best manufacturing training in the world. Toyota teaching GM how to build cars. And we inherited that.

**Munro:** But you did things very differently.

**Musk:** We had to. Traditional automotive is designed around combustion engines and transmissions. Those go away in an EV. We could rethink everything.

---

## Design for Manufacturing

### Designing Cars Differently

**Munro:** The Cybertruck. That's a manufacturing play, not just design.

**Mus:** Exactly. People think it's about the look. It's about: can we stamp this from stainless steel? A flat panel is much easier than complex curvature. The exoskeleton architecture lets us simplify manufacturing massively.

**Munro:** But what about crumple zones?

**Musk:** They put the crumple zones in the front and rear. The passenger cell is a rigid shell. It's actually safer than conventional designs.

### The 3D Architecture

**Munro:** The zonal wiring with the CAN bus communication. That's where everyone is going.

**Musk:** Yeah. Distributed architecture. Each zone has its own controller. It eliminates all that wiring complexity.

**Munro:** And over-the-air updates make it all software-defined.

**Musk:** Exactly. The car improves over time. Traditional vehicles peak the day they leave the factory. Tesla vehicles get better.

## Competition and Industry

### On Other Automakers

**Munro:** What do you think about other EVs?

**Musk:** I'm glad they exist. The goal is sustainable energy. We need everyone building electric cars.

**Munro:** But they don't have your manufacturing approach.

**Musk:** They will. It takes time. The industry will evolve. What we've proven is that vertical integration and obsessive manufacturing matters.

**Munro:** Will they catch up?

**Musk:** Some will. But the ones that truly understand manufacturing will succeed. The ones that just want to buy components and assemble them will struggle.

### The Future

**Munro:** Where are you in five years?

**Musk:** We need to be at 20 million vehicles per year. That's our target.

**Munro:** That's massive.

**Musk:** It has to be. The world needs that many sustainable vehicles. And we're going to need terawatt-hours of batteries to do it.

## Key Insights from Munro Interview

### 1. The Factory is the Product
Musk's philosophy: The factory design is as important as the vehicle design. At 1M+ units, manufacturing becomes the constraint.

### 2. Complexity Kills Scale
Tesla's wiring: 100 parts vs. 3,000 for traditional OEMs. Complexity doesn't scale linearly—it compounds exponentially.

### 3. The Gigapress Innovation
70 parts → 1 part. 6,000-ton press. The industry said impossible. Tesla did it first. Now everyone must follow.

### 4. Vertical Integration Philosophy
"The hard way is the shortcut." Owning every process eliminates supplier dependencies and accelerates iteration.

### 5. Cell Manufacturing as Moat
4680 cells: hardest thing Tesla's done. Controlling battery supply is strategic necessity for 20M vehicle target.

### 6. Don't Automate Too Fast
Model 3 ramp lesson: Learn the process, then automate. Automating broken processes amplifies problems.

### 7. Over-the-Air Advantage
Traditional cars peak at manufacture. Tesla cars improve continuously—software-defined vehicles change the product lifecycle.
