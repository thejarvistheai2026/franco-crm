# Elon Musk - Tesla Battery Day Presentation (2020)

**Date:** September 22, 2020  
**Source:** Tesla Battery Day, shareholder meeting  
**Location:** Fremont, California (outdoor event)  
**Speakers:** Elon Musk, Drew Baglino  
**Event:** Annual Tesla Shareholder Meeting and Battery Day

---

## About This Presentation

Tesla Battery Day was one of the most anticipated tech events of 2020. Elon Musk and Drew Baglino unveiled Tesla's plans to revolutionize battery technology and manufacturing. The presentation revealed Tesla's complete vertical integration strategy—designing and manufacturing their own cells, electrodes, and even mining processes.

The event took place six months into the COVID-19 pandemic, with shareholders sitting in socially-distanced Tesla vehicles instead of traditional seating.

---

## Opening: The Scale Problem

### The Problem with Batteries

**Elon Musk:** We want to accelerate the transition to sustainable energy. But to do that, we need to solve the cell production constraint. We need massive factories producing cells at scale.

**Drew Baglino:** The challenge is that just making the same form factor cells won't work. We need to fundamentally change how batteries are made.

**Musk:** Long term, we want to produce about 20 million vehicles a year. To do that, we need 3 terawatt-hours of battery production per year. Current global cell production is about 100 gigawatt-hours. We need to increase global cell production by 30x.

### The Price Point

**Musk:** To make electric vehicles truly accessible to everyone, we need to get the cost per kilowatt-hour down dramatically. At $100/kWh at the pack level, EVs reach price parity with gasoline cars without subsidies.

---

## The New Cell: 4680 Form Factor

### Unveiling the Cell

**Drew Baglino:** This is our new tabless cell, the 4680. It's 46 millimeters in diameter, 80 millimeters tall. That's why we call it 4680.

**Musk:** The key innovation here is the tabless electrode. Traditional cells have tabs that create thermal bottlenecks and manufacturing complexity. The tabless design removes those issues.

### Technical Improvements

**Baglino:** The 4680 gives us five times more energy, six times more power, and enables 16% more range just from the cell form factor change.

**Musk:** And it's coming in about three years. Next year we'll be doing pilot production, but large-scale production in about three years.

### Manufacturing Innovation

**Baglino:** We're rewriting cell manufacturing from scratch. Traditional wet electrode coating works like spreading cheese on bread—it's slow, wasteful, and energy-intensive.

**Musk:** We're moving to a dry electrode process. No ovens, no solvent recovery, much faster, much smaller factory footprint.

---

## Vertical Integration

### Cathode and Anode

**Baglino:** We're designing our own cathodes. The cathode is where most of the cost is. We're moving to a high-nickel chemistry with no cobalt.

**Musk:** Cobalt is expensive, problematic to mine, and supply-constrained. We want to eliminate cobalt entirely.

**Baglino:** For the anode, we're using silicon instead of graphite. Silicon stores 9x more lithium, but it expands. We've solved the expansion problem.

### Full Vertical Integration

**Musk:** We're taking the entire battery value chain in-house. From mining the ore, to the finished pack in the vehicle, we own the whole thing.

**Baglino:** This lets us innovate at every step. The battery pack isn't just something we buy—it becomes a fundamental part of Tesla's technology.

---

## The Million-Mile Battery

### Durability

**Musk:** These cells are designed for longevity. We believe they can achieve a million-mile lifespan.

**Baglino:** Most vehicles are driven about 200,000 miles before they're scrapped. But batteries often degrade before that. With a million-mile battery, the battery will outlast multiple vehicle bodies.

**Musk:** This has huge implications for robotaxis. A robotaxi might drive 200,000 miles a year. A million-mile battery means the battery lasts five years even under extreme commercial use.

### Secondary Use

**Musk:** After the vehicle, the battery can go into stationary storage. Then, after that, the materials can be recycled into new batteries.

**Baglino:** We're building battery recycling into our factories. We can recover 92% of the battery materials.

---

## The $25,000 Tesla

### Cost Reduction Path

**Musk:** Through these innovations—cell form factor, manufacturing process, material chemistry—we believe we can reduce the cost per kilowatt-hour by 56%.

**Baglino:** That enables us to produce a fully autonomous $25,000 Tesla.

**Musk:** This isn't some far-off future car. We're talking about something we could produce in about three years. About three years from now, we think we can do this.

### The Vision

**Musk:** A $25,000 Tesla that's fully autonomous. That changes everything. Electric vehicles become truly mass-market. Combine that with FSD, and transportation costs drop to almost nothing.

---

## Manufacturing: Terafactories

### Not Just Gigafactories

**Musk:** We need terawatt-hours of production, which means terafactories. The Nevada Gigafactory is about 35 GWh. We need 100x that.

**Baglino:** Battery Day is about solving production at scale. The cell is the constraint on EV growth. We need to remove that constraint.

### The Texas Factory

**Musk:** We're building in Austin, Texas. This will be a massive Terafactory producing Model Y, Cybertruck, and Semi, plus the batteries for all of them.

**Baglino:** Battery production will be co-located with vehicle production. The battery comes off one line and goes straight into the vehicle.

---

## Plaid Model S

### Performance Unveil

**Musk:** We're also announcing Plaid. This will be the fastest production car ever made. Zero to sixty in under 2 seconds.

**Baglino:** We're targeting under 2 seconds with the rollout, and we'll get it under 2 seconds with the production version.

**Musk:** This is what happens when you don't care about cost. This is for people who want the absolute best. The Model S Plaid goes into production next year.

---

## Challenges and Realism

### Timeline Reality

**Musk:** We tend to be optimistic on timelines. These battery innovations are real, and we're building the factories. But it takes time to scale to millions of vehicles.

**Baglino:** We're doing pilot production of the 4680 cells right now. Next year we'll ramp. Large-scale production is about three years out.

**Musk:** I want to be clear—we're confident this will work, but it's not going to happen overnight.

### The Hard Part

**Musk:** The hard part is manufacturing at scale. Prototype cells in a lab is one thing. Ten million cells a day is another.

## Key Insights from Battery Day

### 1. 56% Cost Reduction
Goal: $56/kWh reduction through form factor, chemistry, and manufacturing. Path to $100/kWh battery packs.

### 2. Tabless Cell Design
The 4680 removes tabs that slow manufacturing and create hot spots. Simpler, faster, cooler.

### 3. Dry Electrode Coating
Eliminate wet coating ovens (football field sized). Move to dry process—faster, cheaper, smaller factories.

### 4. Full Vertical Integration
Tesla owns: mining → cathode → anode → cell → pack → vehicle. No supplier bottlenecks.

### 5. High-Nickel, Zero Cobalt
Cathode chemistry shift: nickel is abundant, cobalt is problematic. Eliminate cobalt.

### 6. Silicon Anode
Graphite replaced with silicon (9x capacity). Innovation: solve expansion problem.

### 7. Million-Mile Lifespan
Outlasts vehicle, then goes to storage, then gets recycled. 92% material recovery.

### 8. The $25K Tesla
With 56% cost reduction, Tesla can make a $25,000 EV. Target: ~3 years from 2020.

## Context and Impact

Battery Day was met with mixed reactions:
- **Bulls:** Revolutionary, creates insurmountable moat
- **Skeptics:** Too ambitious, 3-year timelines always slip
- **Stock:** Dropped after event (expectations too high)

**Reality check on timelines:**
- 4680 cells: Pilot production started 2021, still scaling in 2024
- Dry electrode: Transitioned to wet electrode for speed, still pursuing dry
- $25K Tesla: Hasn't happened yet
- Terafactory: Texas Gigafactory operational, 4680 production ongoing

**What worked:**
- 4680 cells are real and in vehicles today (Model Y Texas)
- Vertical integration proceeded (Lithium refinery in Texas)
- High-nickel chemistry achieved (Model Y and 3 use it)

**What evolved:**
- Timelines extended (always)
- Some technical challenges required iteration (dry electrode)
- The vision became bigger (more factories, more vehicles)

Battery Day established Tesla as a battery technology company, not just a car company. The market cap responded accordingly—Tesla became worth more than the next 10 automakers combined.
