# Elon Musk - Code Conference Interview (2016)

**Date:** June 1, 2016  
**Source:** Recode Code Conference  
**Location:** Rancho Palos Verdes, California  
**Interviewers:** Kara Swisher and Walt Mossberg  
**Guest:** Elon Musk (CEO of Tesla and SpaceX)

---

## About This Interview

Elon Musk appeared at the 2016 Code Conference fresh off Tesla's Model 3 unveiling and in the middle of significant SpaceX developments. This wide-ranging interview covered Mars colonization, Tesla's future, artificial intelligence, and Musk's relationship with Apple.

This conversation captured Musk during a pivotal year when both of his companies were scaling dramatically.

---

## Mars Colonization

### The Vision

**Kara Swisher:** You've talked a lot about Mars. You want to get there by 2024?

**Elon Musk:** The Mars shots are coming up in 2018. The first cargo missions will go in 2018. Then 2020 is more cargo missions. 2022 we send the first actual Mars colonial transport ship with equipment.

**Walt Mossberg:** And when do humans go?

**Musk:** Humans go in 2024. That's the first opportunity.

**Swisher:** Why are you so focused on Mars?

**Musk:** I think it's important for humanity to have a future that's inspiring and appealing. Just look at the future. What would you like the future to be? I think you want it to involve humanity out there among the stars, exploring multiple planets, expanding the scope and scale of consciousness.

**Swisher:** But why Mars specifically?

**Musk:** Mars is the only planet that we have any chance of making self-sustaining. It's the closest one. It's got a day that's almost the same as Earth's. It's got water ice, CO2 atmosphere. It's possible over time to warm it up and make it like Earth.

### The Economics

**Mossberg:** This is going to cost a lot. How do you pay for it?

**Musk:** I think the key breakthrough is to make the cost per person to Mars something that people could afford. If it's $100 billion per person, we're done. But if it's under $100,000, that means the cost of Mars travel is less than the median home price in the US. Then I think people would be willing to sell their stuff and move to Mars.

**Swisher:** You're assuming a lot of people want to go. Is there really that much demand?

**Musk:** Absolutely. If it's affordable, there will be plenty of volunteers. Especially if there's infrastructure there—jobs, ways to contribute, things to do.

## Tesla and Model 3

### The Model 3 Launch

**Swisher:** You just unveiled the Model 3. You got 325,000 pre-orders in a week.

**Musk:** Actually, I think it's 373,000 now. 

**Swisher:** That's $14 billion in implied sales. Did you expect that?

**Musk:** No, we didn't expect that at all. We thought maybe we'd get 50,000 to 100,000 pre-orders over the first year.

**Mossberg:** So you're now on the hook to deliver 400,000 cars.

**Musk:** Yeah, we have to build 400,000 to 500,000 cars a year just to satisfy Model 3 demand.

### Production Hell

**Swisher:** You've talked about "production hell" before with the Model S and Model X.

**Musk:** Manufacturing is really hard. It's underappreciated how difficult manufacturing is. Everyone thinks the hard part is the design and the software. But actually building things at scale with high quality is super difficult.

**Mossberg:** Can you actually make this many cars?

**Musk:** We have to. We will. It's not going to be easy. But we need to build an order of magnitude more cars than we're building now.

### Autopilot and Autonomy

**Swisher:** Let's talk about Autopilot. There's been some controversy about crashes.

**Musk:** There are over a million auto deaths per year worldwide. The goal of Autopilot is to save lives. If used correctly, it's statistically safer than a human driver.

**Mossberg:** When will we have fully autonomous cars?

**Musk:** I think we're less than two years away from complete autonomy in good circumstances. Highway autonomy is pretty much solved. It's the city driving that's the hardest part.

**Swisher:** So when can I take a nap in my Tesla?

**Musk:** (laughs) In about two years for highway driving. City driving, maybe three or four years.

**Mossberg:** You mean three or four years from regulatory approval, or from technical capability?

**Musk:** Technical capability. Regulatory approval will depend on the regulators. But I think from a technical standpoint, we'll have solved the problem within 24 to 36 months.

## Apple and Competition

### Apple's Car Project

**Swisher:** Apple is working on a car. You're competing with them now.

**Musk:** It's obvious they're working on something. They've hired over a thousand engineers. You can't keep that secret.

**Mossberg:** How do you feel about competing with Apple?

**Musk:** It's good for Tesla. It increases the legitimacy of EVs. If Apple does it, then it must be real. It's a big market, there's room for multiple players.

**Swisher:** You've recruited people from Apple.

**Musk:** Yeah, and they've recruited people from us. Actually, they hired five times as many people from Tesla as we hired from them. We jokingly call Apple the "Tesla graveyard." 

**Swisher:** That's harsh.

**Musk:** It's not that they want to work at Apple. It's that they didn't make it at Tesla. They get hired at Apple.

**Mossberg:** Is that a little dismissive?

**Musk:** If you don't make it at Tesla, you go work at Apple. I'm not dismissive of Apple. I'm a big fan of Apple products. They make great phones and laptops.

### Openness vs. Closed

**Swisher:** You talked about open sourcing Tesla's patents.

**Musk:** We didn't "open source" them exactly. We said we wouldn't sue anyone who uses our patents in good faith to build electric vehicles. Our real competition is not other EV companies—it's the 100 million gasoline cars produced every year.

## Artificial Intelligence

### AI Concerns

**Swisher:** You've been warning about AI dangers. Why?

**Musk:** I think we should be very careful about artificial intelligence. If I were to guess at what our biggest existential threat is, it's probably that.

**Mossberg:** More than climate change? More than nuclear war?

**Musk:** Yes, I think AI is far more dangerous than nukes. If there's something that's going to be more intelligent than us, what's our value in that scenario?

**Swisher:** This sounds like science fiction.

**Musk:** No, it's very real. The rate of improvement of AI is exponential. It's not linear. Most people think linearly, but technology improves exponentially.

### DeepMind and Google

**Swisher:** You invested in DeepMind before Google bought it.

**Musk:** Yeah, just to keep an eye on it. I was concerned about what they were building.

**Mossberg:** So you invested to watch the technology?

**Musk:** Yeah, to understand what's happening. The guy running DeepMind, Demis Hassabis, is one of the smartest people I know. And he's concerned too.

### Neuralink

**Swisher:** Are you building something to respond to AI?

**Musk:** Well, I'm not going to announce Neuralink tonight, but...

**Swisher:** Wait, what did you just say?

**Musk:** (laughs) I might have said too much. There's something else I'm working on related to AI.

**Mossberg:** What's Neuralink?

**Musk:** I'm not going to say more. But the idea is to create a high-bandwidth interface between the brain and computers. We need to somehow have better symbiosis with digital intelligence.

## Work Life Balance

### 100+ Hour Weeks

**Swisher:** You work 100+ hours a week, split between two companies. That's insane.

**Musk:** It is. I wouldn't recommend it.

**Mossberg:** You're also raising five kids.

**Musk:** Yeah, I see my kids on the weekends. They come hang out at the office sometimes.

**Swisher:** Don't your kids complain?

**Musk:** They do sometimes. But they understand that what we're doing is important. SpaceX is about making humanity multi-planetary. Tesla is about sustainable energy.

**Mossberg:** Why don't you just pick one?

**Musk:** I can't. They're both too important. If one failed, I'd have to focus on the other. But right now, both are doing well enough that I can split my time.

### Personal Sacrifice

**Swisher:** You don't see this as a sacrifice?

**Musk:** It's hard. No question. Maybe I should work less. But there's so much to do. These companies are growing exponentially. The demands are intense.

## Key Insights from Code Conference

### 1. Mars Pricing Sensitivity
Musk's price target: $100,000/person to Mars. Compare to median US home price—Mars needs to feel "affordable."

### 2. Manufacturing is Hard
"Everyone thinks the hard part is the design. Building things at scale with high quality is super difficult." Manufacturing is Tesla's moat.

### 3. AI as Existential Risk
Musk: AI "far more dangerous than nukes." This wasn't mainstream in 2016—he was early on this warning.

### 4. Apple Competitors Are "Failed" Tesla Employees
The "Tesla graveyard" comment generated headlines. Musk clarified: Apple gets Tesla's non-superstars.

### 5. Two-Year Autonomy Prediction
"Technical capability in 24-36 months"—proved too optimistic. Still struggling with full autonomy years later.

### 6. Patent Non-Assertion
Not "open sourcing" but won't sue for EV patents. Real competitor is gasoline, not other EVs.

### 7. Neuralink Tease
First public mention of Neuralink idea—human-computer symbiosis as response to AI.

## Context and Impact

June 2016 was a peak moment:
- Model 3 pre-orders: 373,000 (stunning demand signal)
- Mars timeline: 2018 cargo, 2024 humans (proved too aggressive)
- Autopilot safety discussions after first fatality (May 2016)
- Apple car project rumors at peak
- AI warnings before they became mainstream

Musk's comments about Apple being "Tesla graveyard" were controversial and he later apologized/clarified. The interview showcased his characteristic combination of extreme technical ambition and willingness to make provocative statements.

Neuralink was revealed accidentally—Musk teased it and then wouldn't elaborate. The company wouldn't be formally announced for another year (March 2017).
