# Steve Jobs - Pixar and Animation

**Date:** 1995-2006  
**Source:** Various interviews, documentaries, and talks  
**Focus:** Steve Jobs' role at Pixar Animation Studios

---

## About This Compilation

While Steve Jobs is best known for Apple, his role as CEO of Pixar Animation Studios from 1986 to 2006 was equally transformative. This file compiles Jobs' insights on Pixar, animation, creativity, and the intersection of technology and art.

---

## The Pixar Story

### Acquisition (1986)

**Steve Jobs:** I bought Pixar from George Lucas for $5 million in 1986. Lucas wanted to sell the computer graphics division. It was losing money. Everyone told me not to do it. They said I was crazy.

But I saw something. I saw these people who were combining technology and art in a way I'd never seen before. They were making computers that could create images that looked real. That was magical.

### The Early Years (1986-1995)

**Context:**
- Pixar originally sold high-end computer hardware
- Hardware business failed; pivoted to software
- Software business struggled; pivoted to animation
- Jobs invested $50 million over 9 years before profitability
- Nearly sold Pixar multiple times

**Famous quote:**
> "If I knew then what I know now, I would have never done it. But I'm so glad I did."

### Toy Story (1995)

**Steve Jobs on the premiere:**
I sat in the theater on November 22, 1995, watching Toy Story with an audience for the first time. I had seen it a hundred times in production. I knew every frame. But watching it with an audience — seeing them laugh, seeing them cry — that was different.

When the credits rolled and the audience applauded, I turned to John Lasseter and said, "We did it." That was one of the best moments of my life.

**Impact:**
- First fully computer-animated feature film
- $361 million worldwide box office
- Established Pixar as a creative force
- Proved computer animation could tell emotional stories

### The IPO (1995)

**Steve Jobs:** After Toy Story succeeded, we took Pixar public. I owned 80% of the company. The IPO valued Pixar at $1.5 billion. In one day, I went from being worth $50 million in debt to being worth $1.5 billion.

But that wasn't the important part. The important part was that Pixar was now independent. We could make our own movies. We didn't have to answer to Disney or anyone else.

### The Disney Partnership

**Steve Jobs on the early Disney deal:**
We signed a deal with Disney in 1991 to make three films. They would finance and distribute. We would create. It was a co-production deal.

But here's what I learned: Never let the money people control the creative people. In every negotiation, I fought for Pixar's creative independence. That was more important than money.

### Pixar's Creative Culture

**Steve Jobs on Pixar's building:**
I spent more time designing the Pixar building than any Apple product. Why? Because creativity happens when people bump into each other. We put the bathrooms in the center. We made one entrance. We forced people to walk through common areas.

The atrium has the mail, the cafeteria, the meeting rooms. Everyone has to go there. That's where ideas cross-pollinate.

**Key insight:**
- Physical space matters for creativity
- Serendipitous encounters spark innovation
- Remove barriers between departments
- Make the space a product in itself

### The Brain Trust

**Steve Jobs on Pixar's "Brain Trust":**
John Lasseter, Ed Catmull, Andrew Stanton, Pete Docter, Lee Unkrich — these guys were the Brain Trust. They would review each other's work. Brutally honest. No politics. Just making the story better.

I watched them work. I learned from them. That culture of candid feedback — that's what made Pixar special. At Apple, we had the same thing. The best idea wins. Not the most senior person.

### Creativity vs. Technology

**Steve Jobs:** People think Pixar is a technology company. It's not. It's a creative company that happens to use technology.

The technology serves the story. Always. We don't make movies to show off our computers. We use computers to tell stories that couldn't be told any other way.

That's the same philosophy at Apple. Technology married with liberal arts. The humanities. That's what yields products that make our hearts sing.

### Selling Pixar to Disney (2006)

**Steve Jobs:** In 2006, we sold Pixar to Disney for $7.4 billion. I became Disney's largest shareholder. But here's what mattered: John Lasseter became Chief Creative Officer of Disney Animation. Ed Catmull became President.

We didn't just sell Pixar. We saved Disney Animation. They were making terrible movies. We brought the Pixar culture to Disney. And it worked. Tangled, Frozen, Wreck-It Ralph — those are Pixar-influenced Disney movies.

**Terms of the deal:**
- Pixar remained independent studio in Emeryville
- John Lasseter given control of Disney Animation
- Pixar culture preserved
- Steve Jobs became Disney's largest shareholder

### What Jobs Learned at Pixar

**On storytelling:**
At Pixar, I learned that story is king. Everything serves the story. If a scene doesn't move the story forward, cut it. No matter how beautiful it is.

I brought that to Apple. Every feature must serve the user experience. If it doesn't, cut it. No matter how technically impressive.

**On collaboration:**
Pixar movies take 200-300 people and 4-5 years. No one person makes a Pixar movie. It's deeply collaborative.

Apple is the same. The iPhone takes thousands of people. Hardware, software, services, retail — all working together. Collaboration is everything.

**On iteration:**
Pixar movies start terrible. The first version of Toy Story was awful. Woody was a jerk. The story didn't work. They rewrote it completely.

That's normal. That's how great work happens. Iterate. Iterate. Iterate. Until it's right.

At Apple, we do the same. The first iPhone prototype was a disaster. We kept iterating. Until it was right.

### Pixar's Business Lessons

**Steve Jobs on Pixar's model:**
Pixar had something rare: Creative people running the company. John and Ed weren't MBAs. They were artists and technologists. That's why they made great decisions.

Most media companies are run by business people. They make decisions based on spreadsheets. That's why they make bad movies.

At Apple, I'm not a computer scientist. I'm not an engineer. But I know product. That's what matters.

### The Pixar-Apple Connection

**Steve Jobs:** Pixar and Apple fed each other. At Pixar, I learned about managing creative people. About giving them freedom while maintaining standards. About building a culture where great work happens.

At Apple, I applied those lessons. The iPhone team was like a Pixar film crew. Small, focused, incredibly talented. Given freedom but held to high standards.

And what I learned at Apple about technology — about making complex things simple — that helped at Pixar.

### Jobs' Pixar Legacy

**Films released during Jobs' tenure:**
1. Toy Story (1995)
2. A Bug's Life (1998)
3. Toy Story 2 (1999)
4. Monsters, Inc. (2001)
5. Finding Nemo (2003)
6. The Incredibles (2004)
7. Cars (2006)

**Notable:** Every Pixar film during Jobs' tenure was a critical and commercial success. The studio never had a flop.

### Famous Pixar Quotes

**Steve Jobs:**
- "Pixar is the most successful thing I've ever done."
- "If I had never dropped in on that single calligraphy course in college, the Mac would have never had multiple typefaces. And since Windows just copied the Mac, it's likely that no personal computer would have them." — Connecting Pixar design sensibility to Apple
- "The people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world are the ones who do." — Pixar/Apple philosophy

## Key Insights

### 1. Technology Serves Art
At Pixar, computers served storytelling. Not the other way around. This philosophy — technology married with liberal arts — became Apple's core identity.

### 2. Creative People Should Run Creative Companies
Jobs believed that creative companies should be led by product people, not business people. This was true at Pixar and Apple.

### 3. Iteration Is Invisible
Audiences see the final product. They don't see the hundreds of revisions, the discarded ideas, the scenes that were cut. Greatness comes from invisible iteration.

### 4. Space Shapes Behavior
Jobs designed Pixar's building to force serendipitous encounters. Physical environment shapes creative culture.

### 5. Candor Creates Quality
Pixar's Brain Trust provided brutally honest feedback. No politics. Just making the work better. This culture of candor was essential to quality.
