# Steve Jobs - D8 Interview with Walt Mossberg (2010)

**Date:** June 1, 2010  
**Source:** All Things D (D8 Conference)  
**Location:** Palos Verdes, California  
**Interviewer:** Walt Mossberg and Kara Swisher  
**Guest:** Steve Jobs (Apple CEO)

---

## About This Interview

Steve Jobs' final All Things D appearance came at D8 in 2010, during a tumultuous period for Apple. Topics included Flash, the lost iPhone prototype, Foxconn, competition with Google, and the iPad's future. This was Jobs in full "Steve mode" — defiant, visionary, and unapologetic.

The interview occurred two months after the iPad launch (already sold 2 million units) and shortly after Jobs' public "Thoughts on Flash" essay.

---

## Full Transcript

### On Apple Surpassing Microsoft

**Walt:** The first question is about Apple surpassing Microsoft in market valuation.

**Steve:** It's surreal, but it doesn't really mean anything.

### On Flash and HTML5

**Walt:** You recently wrote "Thoughts on Flash." Even if everything you say is true, is it really fair to consumers to be so abrupt and cut them off?

**Steve:** Apple is a company that doesn't have the most resources in the world, and the way we've succeeded is to bet the right technological horse, to look at technologies that have a future. We try to pick things that are in their springs. And if you choose wisely, you can be quite successful.

Apple has a history of doing that. We were the first company to dump the floppy and later, to adopt USB. Sometimes when we get rid of things, people call us crazy. But sometimes you just have to pick the things that are going to be the right horse to ride forward. And Flash has had its day—but HTML5 is starting to emerge. The video looks better and it works better and you don't need a plug-in to run it. And while 75 percent of the video on the Web may be available in Flash, a lot of it is available in HTML5 as well.

**Walt:** What about developers? How are they impacted?

**Steve:** We didn't set out to have a war over Flash. We made a technical decision. And it wasn't until the iPad that Adobe raised a stink. They came after us. That's why I wrote "Thoughts on Flash." We were getting tired of being trashed by Adobe in the press.

**Walt:** What if people demand Flash? What if they say the iPad is crippled without Flash?

**Steve:** We're just trying to make great products. We don't think Flash makes a great product, so we're leaving it out. Instead, we're going to focus on technologies that are in ascendancy. If we succeed, people will buy them and if we don't they won't. And, so far, I have to say, people seem to be liking the iPad. We are selling an iPad every 3 seconds.

### On the Lost iPhone Prototype

**Walt:** The inevitable lost-iPhone question. Where do you come down on this?

**Steve:** To make a wireless product work well, you have to test it. And one of our employees was carrying one and there's a debate about whether it was left in a bar or stolen. And the person who found it decided to sell it. And it turned out this person plugged it into his roommate's computer and that roommate called the police.

The police showed up and took this guy's computers. And the DA is investigating it. I don't know where it will end up. In other words, it's a police matter.

### On Foxconn

**Kara:** Any comments on the Foxconn suicides?

**Steve:** Apple is extraordinarily diligent and rigorous about vetting its manufacturing partners. Foxconn is not a sweatshop. They've got restaurants and swimming pools. For a factory, it's a pretty nice factory.

The recent suicides at Foxconn, which number 13 at last count, are still below the national average in the U.S. But this is very troubling to us. So we send over our own people and some outside folks as well, to look into the issue.

### On Platform Wars

**Walt:** You spent a significant portion of your career involved in a platform war with Microsoft. And you lost. But now there are new platforms out there and you're doing quite well on them, as are others—Google and Facebook. So there's a new platform war going on. Do you see it like that?

**Steve:** We never saw ourselves in a platform war with Microsoft, either. Maybe that's why we lost. But we never thought of ourselves in a platform war; we just wanted to make good products.

**Walt:** What about Google? The relationship has clearly changed there, hasn't it?

**Steve:** Well, they're competing with us. We didn't go into search.

**Kara:** How do you look at Google as a competitor? Eric was on your board.

**Steve:** They decided to compete with us and got more and more serious.

**Walt:** Do you feel betrayed by Google?

**Steve:** My sex life is great, how's yours?

[Laughter]

**Kara:** Will Apple remove Google from the iPhone and iPad?

**Steve:** No. Again, we are simply trying to make the best products we can and that the market will decide whose is better. Right now, we have the better product.

### On Search and Siri

**Walt:** Why did Apple buy Siri, a search company?

**Steve:** I don't know if I would describe Siri as a search company. They're not in the search area. They're in the AI area. Then he adds, a bit vehemently: We're not going into search.

### On AT&T

**Walt:** What about AT&T, whose network continues to face criticism?

**Steve:** They're doing pretty good in some ways and in others they could do better. We meet with them once a quarter. Remember, they deal with way more data traffic than anyone else. And they're having trouble. But they have the fastest 3G network and they're improving. I wish they were improving faster.

I'm convinced that any other network, had you put the iPhone on it, would have had the same problems.

We found a way to sell the phone that we wanted to sell and to define it the way we wanted to define it. We were able to change the rules of the game, and that's what got us excited about the phone business. AT&T took a big leap on us and decided they were going to trust us to do the right thing with the phone. And that's worked out quite well for both of us.

### On the iPad's Origin

**Walt:** Did Apple know it would build a tablet before it built the iPhone?

**Steve:** I'll tell you a secret. It began with the tablet. I had this idea about having a glass display, a multitouch display you could type on with your fingers. I asked our people about it. And six months later, they came back with this amazing display. And I gave it to one of our really brilliant UI guys. He got [rubber band] scrolling working and some other things, and I thought, 'my God, we can build a phone with this!' So we put the tablet aside, and we went to work on the iPhone.

### On Publishing and the iPad

**Kara:** What does the iPad mean for the publishing industry? Is it the savior?

**Steve:** One of my beliefs very strongly is that any democracy depends on a free, healthy press, and so when I think of the most important journalistic endeavors in this country, I think of things like the Washington Post, the New York Times, The Wall Street Journal and publications like that. And we all know what's happened to the economics of those businesses. I don't want to see us descend into a nation of bloggers.

Anything that we can do to help the news-gathering organizations find new ways of expression so that they can afford to keep their news-gathering and editorial operations intact, I'm all for.

### On Tablets vs. PCs

**Walt:** In order for the tablet to succeed, people have to feel that it's worth carrying around. Do you think the tablet will succeed the laptop?

**Steve:** When we were an agrarian nation, all cars were trucks, because that's what you needed on the farm. But as vehicles started to be used in the urban centers, cars got more popular. Innovations like automatic transmission and power steering and things that you didn't care about in a truck as much started to become paramount in cars.

PCs are going to be like trucks. They're still going to be around, they're still going to have a lot of value, but they're going to be used by one out of X people. I think that we're embarked on that. Is the next step the iPad? Who knows? Will it happen next year or five years from now or seven years from now? Who knows? But I think we're headed in that direction.

### On Content Creation

**Walt:** What are your thoughts on content creation on the iPad? Some people believe tablets aren't good devices for content creation.

**Steve:** Well, why wouldn't they be good for content creation? It can't be that the software isn't powerful enough, because it is. I think it's just a matter of people learning to use a new form factor.

### On Apple's Philosophy

**Kara:** What is it that drives you? What is it that makes Apple special?

**Steve:** We just try to make great products. That's what we do. We try to make things that we think people are going to love. And sometimes we're right, and sometimes we're wrong. But we always try.

I think what makes Apple special is that we really care. We really care about making great products. We don't just want to make good products. We want to make great products. And that takes time, and it takes effort, and it takes a lot of hard work. But that's what we do.

## Key Insights

### 1. "Just Trying to Make Great Products"
Jobs' repeated mantra throughout the interview. Apple's entire philosophy distilled into one sentence.

### 2. The Flash War
Jobs was defiant and unapologetic about dropping Flash. He framed it as a technical decision, not a business one — though Adobe saw it differently.

### 3. "My Sex Life Is Great"
Jobs' famous deflection when asked about betrayal by Google. This became one of the most quoted lines from any Steve Jobs interview.

### 4. The iPad Started First
The iPhone was actually a detour: "It began with the tablet... I thought, 'my God, we can build a phone with this!' So we put the tablet aside."

### 5. "PCs Are Going to Be Like Trucks"
Jobs' famous analogy comparing PCs to trucks and tablets to cars. Most people will use tablets (cars); professionals will use PCs (trucks).

### 6. No Regrets About Adobe
"We didn't set out to have a war over Flash. We made a technical decision. And it wasn't until the iPad that Adobe raised a stink. They came after us."

### 7. The Lost iPhone
Jobs acknowledged the lost prototype was "very painful" but emphasized it was a police matter. His demeanor suggested deep irritation.

### 8. On Google Competition
"They decided to compete with us and got more and more serious. We didn't go into search."
