## 1. My 9 Favorite Growth Frameworks

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/my-9-favorite-growth-frameworks  
**Published:** October 25, 2024

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

Frameworks are mental shortcuts—compressed big ideas you can access when needed. This post shares the 9 frameworks Elena returns to repeatedly, plus her own bonus frameworks.

### Frameworks Mentioned

**1. Growth Loops (Reforge)**
- Growth loops create compounding cycles vs. linear funnels
- For truly scalable strategy, you need a loop that creates a flywheel, not just pouring resources into the top of a funnel
- Dropbox example: New user adds content → Shares with somebody → Recipient becomes new user (circular loop)
- *Action:* Map your growth loops and measure their performance

**2. Racecar Growth Framework (Lenny Rachitsky & Dan Hockenmaier)**
- Separates growth loops (engine) from optimizations (lubricants) from one-off tactics (turbo boosts) from funnels (fuel)
- Use this to categorize team activities and maintain balance—avoid over-indexing on fuel or lubricants only
- *Action:* Audit your growth activities through this lens

**3. The Adjacent User (Bangaly Kaba)**
- How to expand beyond initial product-market fit when growth stalls
- Identify adjacent users: people close enough to your target audience that they can be folded into existing product
- Instagram used this to grow from 400M to 1B users
- *Action:* Identify personas beyond your core target that already benefit from your product

**4. The 4 Growth Frameworks for $100M+ Products (Brian Balfour)**
- Requires 4 intersecting fits: Product-Channel Fit; Channel/Model Fit; Model-Market Fit; Market-Product Fit (reverse!)
- Without model-channel fit specifically, "you are dead in the water"
- *Action:* Audit all 4 fits before scaling

**5. Founder-Led Growth (Willie Tran)**
- Founders must own growth efforts in early stages—hiring for growth too soon can ruin a company
- No one understands product and market better than founders
- Wait until $1M ARR before considering growth specialization
- *Action:* Founders should drive growth personally until product-market fit is clear

**6. Find the Fast-Moving Water (James Currier/NFX)**
- Growth depends heavily on the market you're in
- Jump into fast-moving water (rapidly expanding industry) for higher chance of success
- Some companies execute incredibly well but struggle in stagnant categories
- *Action:* Evaluate your market's momentum honestly

**7. S-Curves and Product Sequencing (Casey Winters)**
- Companies must move from one growth phase to the next via new products or growth loops
- Visualized as S-curves—as acquisition loops hit limits, identify when to invest in new loops
- *Action:* Plan new growth loops before current ones plateau

**8. Please Don't A/B Test That (Tal Raviv)**
- Over-testing is as dangerous as under-testing
- Strategy is often the underlying issue, not the tactical layer that A/B tests validate
- Don't micro-optimize—focus on big-picture items
- *Action:* Create cross-functional alignment on "experimentation lifecycle"

**9. Opinionated Defaults (Adam Fishman)**
- Product teams often release amazing functionality as opt-in (off by default), then wonder why adoption is low
- Setting strategic defaults drives growth by making user decisions easier
- *Action:* Flip key features to ON by default

### Elena's Bonus Frameworks

- **Paid vs. Free Feature Decision Tree:** Framework for deciding which features to paywall
- **Reverse Trials:** Best way to nail free-to-paid conversion
- **The Power of Profiling (in Onboarding):** Secret key to activation
- **How to Think About Freemium:** Comprehensive guide
- **Growth in B2B vs B2C:** The not-so-secret origins of PLG

### Key Quotes

> "For a truly scalable strategy, you can't keep pouring resources into the top of a funnel… you need a loop that creates a flywheel."

> "Strategy is often the underlying issue, not the tactical layer that A/B tests validate."

> "This is an extension of the Growth Loops framework. The first step is categorizing all of my team's Growth activities according to the Racecar elements."

## 2. 6 Months at Lovable: Throwing Out the Playbook

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/6-months-at-lovable-and-why-i-had  
**Published:** November 10, 2025

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

AI-native companies operate fundamentally differently. The pace is intense because LLM capabilities evolve faster than roadmap cycles, and customer expectations shift simultaneously. This creates a "PMF treadmill" where product-market fit is temporary and constantly shifting.

### Key Shifts in AI-Era Growth

**1. Growth Works on New "Features"**
- 90% of traditional growth was experimentation and optimization
- In AI companies, most of the job is inventing entirely new loops, launching new initiatives, creating new product surfaces
- Work feels closer to product innovation than funnel tuning

**2. Traditional Marketing Isn't the Lever**
- Paid marketing, SEO, campaigns, nurture programs are fine but not what moves an AI company
- Real distribution is word of mouth, creator economy, and community
- Looks more like consumer growth patterns than traditional B2B

**3. Growth Surfaces Are Collapsing**
- Products converging into "a text box with an agent behind it"
- Fewer knobs to turn on the surface
- Growth work sits deep in agent quality, model selection, reasoning behavior, personality

**4. Constant Lookout for New Growth Channels**
- In middle of distribution shift—watching for new distribution windows opening
- Will it be ChatGPT's app marketplace? Something with Sora?

**5. AI Skillset is Crucial**
- Days are completely different than 2 years ago
- Constantly building in AI tools, discussing ideas with ChatGPT, trying new AI features

**6. Growth is Confusing During PMF Treadmill**
- In past, finding PMF meant moving to scaling
- Here, PMF is temporary—growth/scaling stage is also temporary and confusing
- Approach: Over-index on innovation, not optimization

**7. Roles Are Blending**
- Walls between job titles are thin
- Designers do marketing, marketers shape product, engineers do product work
- Generalist mindset is necessary

**8. Organizational Structure Can't Keep Up**
- Development velocity too high for clean process
- Must be comfortable in messy middle
- Plans are short, ownership lines shift
- Current environment rewards people who can create clarity instead of waiting for it

### Actionable Tactics

- Plan in 2-3 week directional bets, not quarters
- Optimize for learning speed, not comfort
- Default to shipping over polishing
- Spend more time editing ideas than managing process
- Assume current mental model will expire soon

### Key Quotes

> "I've spent my career in growth roles where 70-80% of what I already knew mapped to the new job. At Lovable, maybe 30-40% of my experience transfers."

> "If your product isn't getting talked about, there is no amount of optimization that can backfill that gap."

> "Growth is embedded inside core product, not layered on top."

> "The skills that matter most will be: Letting go quickly, re-learning faster, working across boundaries, and always building things people love enough to talk about."

## 3. Here Is How I Approach Starting a New Job

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/here-is-how-i-approach-starting-a  
**Published:** July 9, 2025

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

The first 3 months require balancing learning with delivering. This framework provides a clear progression: protect what's working → quick wins → big bets → strategy.

### The 30-60-90 Day Framework

**Day 1: Protect What's Working**
- Identify what's driving results and DON'T MESS IT UP
- Remove friction to make it work even better
- Ask everyone: "What's working?" or "Why are we growing?"
- Patterns emerge quickly from different opinions

**Days 2-30: Quick Wins**
- 80% positive impact based on previous patterns
- Classify as "optimizations"
- Identify by: (1) past tactics with copy/paste potential, (2) fresh eyes on product, (3) asking everyone for ideas
- For Growth: optimizing key pages, improving onboarding, pricing improvements, introducing trials, testing paid channels, tightening analytics

**Day 30: Big Bets**
- Think about step-function changes
- Shop ideas around, stress-test, ask why they wouldn't work
- Don't commit yet—absorb context to fine-tune intuition

**Day 30-90: Strategy**
- Open a Miro board with rough strategic bets
- Document what you see before losing outsider perspective
- Initial instincts land about 60-70% right

**In Parallel: Operational Improvements**
- Changes that make entire organization more effective
- New rituals, documentation, roles & responsibilities

### Interim Roles Are Different

When you're interim, you're not there to discover what's broken—you're there to fix it fast.

Order shifts to:
1. Work on the big bet
2. Find easy wins
3. Double down on what's working
4. Shape the strategy

Interim work should always be tightly scoped. If the company can't clearly articulate what they want you to fix before you join, don't take the job.

### Traps to Avoid

- **Overpromising early wins:** Setting unrealistic expectations before understanding context backfires
- **Shipping without alignment:** Moving fast without syncing with stakeholders wastes cycles and erodes trust
- **Fixing things that aren't broken:** What looks broken may be working fine for non-obvious reasons

### Key Quotes

> "If you over-index on action, you'll likely misfire because you're missing context. But if you over-index on just learning, you'll create anxiety and unmet expectations."

> "The deeper you go inside a company, the harder it becomes to see the product like a customer."

> "In messy environments, narrowing your scope is the fastest way to create clarity."

## 4. I Want to Eliminate My Own Job

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/i-want-to-eliminate-my-own-job  
**Published:** August 1, 2025

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

95% of people working in Growth today are doing very basic, repeatable tasks. The field has stagnated—we claim we want to innovate, but almost nobody is actually doing it. AI presents an opportunity to automate Growth 101 and move the field forward.

### The Problem: Growth Field Stagnation

- Growth emerged 10-15 years ago as a revolutionary discipline bridging product, marketing, and engineering
- Energy led to Reforge and new frameworks
- But the field stagnated—same problems, same solutions, same failures at every company
- Same story at every company: Activation not done properly, monetization not optimized, viral loops not measured, UGC lifeless

### The Opportunity: Codify Growth 101

What if every AI-native app shipped with Growth tactics out-of-the-box?

**Out-of-the-box experience should include:**
- A/B testing infrastructure
- Analytics and tracking/attribution
- Agents built on operator experience:
  - Onboarding optimization agent (adapts flows based on behavior)
  - Lifecycle email agent (identifies correlations, triggers targeted messages)
  - CRO agent (analyzes site, runs conversion experiments automatically)

### What Happens to Growth Roles?

Yes—this will make many current roles obsolete. But it's an opportunity, not a threat.

Growth professionals can move beyond foundational work to focus on:
- Creative, strategic, human elements only they can bring
- Coming up with new Growth loops in the product
- Working on innovative campaigns
- Exploring new channels

This is how you gain first-mover advantage instead of waiting for the market to move on, leaving you behind.

### Key Quotes

> "95% of people working in Growth today are doing very basic, repeatable tasks. We all claim we want to innovate, but almost nobody is actually doing it."

> "We spend way too much time on the foundations: figuring out Google Tag Manager, setting up analytics to measure events properly, optimizing landing pages for better conversion, or measuring activation correctly."

> "AI isn't a threat, it's a chance to raise the bar. Growth 101 has been holding us back for too long."

## 5. Growth Is Now a Trust Problem

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/growth-is-now-a-trust-problem  
**Published:** January 13, 2026

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

All of us are fighting a war on three fronts thanks to AI: (1) Customer product expectations higher than ever, (2) Distribution channels collapsing, (3) Everyone (big incumbents, new startups, even customers with vibecoding) coming for your value prop. The new playbook is built around one concept: Trust.

### The Distribution Collapse

**Dead/Dying Channels:**
- **SEO/Organic Search:** AI slop reduced content barrier; people go to ChatGPT/Claude/Perplexity instead of Google
- **Paid Search (SEM):** Economics obvious—total searches not growing, costs skyrocketing; competing with AI tools at $20/month
- **Corporate Social:** Platforms hostile to corporate accounts and external links

**Trust-Based Channels:**
- **Employee-led Social:** Founders/employees sharing thinking, failures, roadmap publicly demonstrates transparency
- **Influencer/Creator Partnerships:** Transfer of trust when someone bets their reputation on your product
- **Community-Driven Growth:** Users become your distribution channel
- **Product-Led Brand:** Brand now comes from product experience, not marketing campaigns

### The Retention Problem

AI is commoditizing product capabilities. Traditional value props fall into three categories:
1. Cheaper
2. More efficient (faster)
3. More effective (better outcome)

Problem: AI is faster than any traditional software, more efficient than your workflow tool, answers questions better than your knowledge base. If your primary value prop is efficiency or cost savings, you're competing with something that beats you on both dimensions at $20/month.

### The Trust Moat

When all capabilities get commoditized, users will stick with products they trust.

**How to Build Trust:**
- **Transparent roadmap sharing:** Real visibility into what you're building and why
- **Responsive iteration based on feedback:** Speed matters, but in the right direction
- **Amazing UX / 'WOW' moments:** Delightful details, thoughtful interactions, anticipating needs
- **Thoughtful lifecycle comms:** Every email/update either builds or reduces trust
- **Monetization aligned with outcomes:** Getting paid only when customer succeeds

### Key Quotes

> "The classic distribution systems are falling apart."

> "When all the capabilities get commoditized, users will stick with the products they trust."

> "Brand is a product job now, not just a marketing job. The product itself must demonstrate trustworthiness."

> "The question isn't whether to adopt this approach. The question is how quickly you can make the shift before your traditional channels and value propositions collapse completely."

## 6. To PLG or Not PLG?

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/to-plg-or-not-plg  
**Published:** August 24, 2023

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

The quickest way to fail with Product-Led Growth is to slap a freemium or trial version on a product and hope users flock to it. PLG requires answering how to acquire, retain, and monetize given your product's unique functionality and market conditions.

### Redefining Growth

**Casey Winters' definition:**
> "Most product and marketing teams are built to create or expand the core value provided to customers. Growth is connecting more people to the existing value."

Growth answers three questions predictably, sustainably, and defensibly:
- How do we acquire customers?
- How do we monetize customers?
- How do we retain customers?

### Why Traditional Pirate Framework (AARRR) Fails

Marketing → Acquisition, Product → Retention, Sales → Monetization creates:
- **Not defensible:** Marketing channels commoditized, battle of budgets
- **Not predictable:** Success in acquisition doesn't translate to activation/monetization; leaks between stages
- **Not sustainable:** Continually "feed the beast" instead of self-running engine

### Growth Model Menu

|  | Product | Marketing | Sales |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition** | Virality, UGC | Paid, Content | Outbound, Partners |
| **Monetization** | Self-serve | E-commerce | Sales team |
| **Retention** | Product stickiness | Brand, Community | Account management |

Options are not either-or—they create self-reinforcing systems. Layer elements methodically.

### Product-Led Growth Definition

Product has ability to acquire, activate, engage, or monetize without human intervention. Product doesn't have to work independently across all areas—you might have product-led acquisition and sales-led monetization.

### Offensive vs. Defensive PLG

- **Offensive:** Product uniquely designed to inherently drive growth and sell itself (Figma, Slack)
- **Defensive:** Product can be adapted to drive some growth, but most growth comes from other strategies (Salesforce)

### The B2B PLG Challenge

**The Escalator Framework:**
- Acquire individual users
- Activate and engage TEAMS
- Sell to company (enterprise value)

**Why teams matter:** A company won't pay $50K for value perceived by one employee. They will if significant portion of workforce derives value.

**Requirements at each step:**
- **Acquisition:** Motivation (eager to solve problem), Ability (can use self-serve), Permission (org approvals)
- **Team Activation:** Collaboration features, network effects
- **Company Sale:** Admin features, security, SSO, compliance

### Key Quotes

> "The quickest way to fail with a Product-Led Growth strategy is to slap a freemium or trial version on a product and hope users will flock to it."

> "What is the best way for us to acquire, retain, and monetize a customer, given our product's unique functionality and market conditions?"

> "You must not only acquire individual users but also activate and engage a *team* before selling to a *company*."

## 7. Reverse Trials: Examples

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/reverse-trials-examples  
**Published:** April 12, 2024

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

Reverse Trials combine the best of trials and freemium. They start every new signup on a trial (usually without opt-in or credit card), give access to paid features, then transition to freemium if no payment occurs.

### Why Traditional Models Fail

**Trials:**
- Great for conversion but credit-card-upfront slows top of funnel
- Users feel shortchanged when trial isn't long enough

**Freemium:**
- Draws large initial user base, high engagement
- Struggles to convert—even best performers see only 5% conversion rate

### The Reverse Trial Advantage

Combines urgency of trial-based models with broad accessibility of freemium:
- No opt-in required for trial
- No credit card required
- Access to all or select paid features from start
- Smooth transition to freemium after trial ends

**Results:** Significantly boost user acquisition, engagement, conversion, and turbocharge growth loops with sustained freemium usage.

### Real Examples

**Canva:**
- Profiles every signup for intent
- Defaults to team offering with individual trial "escape hatch"
- No way to start without trial
- Lists 3 biggest upgrade reasons clearly
- Reassurance about trial timeline

**Asana:**
- Profiles new signups
- 30-day paid trial splash modal—"trial or bust"
- Could improve by showing what's included vs. free

**Notion:**
- More conservative—asks for credit card
- Escape hatch to freemium available
- Could show free vs. paid difference better

**Airtable:**
- 14-day Reverse Trial for team experience
- Crystal clear definition: "At end of trial, we'll automatically move you to Free plans unless you choose to upgrade"

### Key Quotes

> "By blending the urgency of trial-based models with the broad accessibility of freemium plans, Reverse Trials could significantly boost user acquisition, engagement, and conversion."

> "It's like enjoying your cake and eating it, too—except with software, where there are no calories, only conversions!"

## 8. Don't Reinvent User Profiling During Onboarding

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/dont-reinvent-user-profiling-during  
**Published:** December 13, 2024

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

First-party data (asking users directly) is the only reliable way. Third-party data is 50-60% inaccurate (up to 80-90% for consumers/freelancers/SMB). This post provides the skeleton structure that works across dozens of companies.

### The Profiling Framework (3 Steps)

**Step 1: Tell Us About Yourself**
Focus on information you need about the customer:
- Intent (personal or professional)
- Role, seniority
- Familiarity with tool
- What they plan to do

**Key questions:**
- "What will you use [product] for?" (identify personal/non-ICP users)
- "What's your role?" (identify decision-makers)
- "What are you planning to do?" (migration vs. scratch—different problems)

**Step 2: Tell Us About Your Company**
(Only show if they chose "for business use")
- Checkbox: "Anyone on your domain can join" (helps team discoverability)
- Company size (segment identification—SMB self-serve, enterprise needs sales)
- Industry (if sales targets by industry)

**Step 3: Invite Your Team**
Encourage inviting team during onboarding:
- Creates awareness of multiplayer mode
- Use form logic (don't show to companies ≤2 people)
- Default to Admin role (multiple admins = redundancy against failure)
- Option to share join link

**Step 4: Tell Us About Your Use Case (Bonus)**
- Remove blinders on expected use
- Use Reforge's Use Case Framework: Problem (customer's words), Persona, Alternative, Why they chose you, Frequency
- Condense to 3-5 options max

### Why Profiling Works

- Even with ~15% dropoff, overall activation volume may/will go up
- Collecting information boosts psychological momentum, increasing activation likelihood
- Investment grows as users progress

### Design Tips

- Only ask what you will use
- Don't make it too pretty—simple flows perform best
- Stay away from open-ended fields (typing kills completion rates)
- Use radio buttons or dropdowns

### Key Quotes

> "First-party data is the only way to go. Period."

> "Third-party data is inaccurate 50-60% of the time. Rates go up to 80-90% if dealing with consumers, freelancers, agencies, or SMB segments."

> "Even though there's a 15% dropoff in the survey stage… the overall activation volume may/will go up!"

## 9. Elena's 2024 B2B Product-Led Sales Guide

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/elenas-2024-b2b-product-led-sales  
**Published:** August 8, 2024

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

Product-Led Sales (PLS) is an expansion play on top of PLG. It uses end-user self-serve usage as the basis for generating sales opportunities. This comprehensive guide covers when to start, how to produce hand-raisers, PQA/PQL definitions, and sales structure.

### When to Start PLS

**Prerequisites:**
1. Self-Serve Product-Market Fit—if you're trying to find PMF with sales team, you're not doing PLS
2. Hand-Raisers appearing organically (users submitting sales forms, reaching via support/social)
3. Sales Floor—deals above ~$10K/year (where credit cards fail, POs needed)

**Scaling Hand-Raisers:**
- Add sales plan to pricing pages
- "Contact sales" in admin, settings, billing
- Contextual feature walls (volume limits, SSO, integrations, dedicated success manager)

### The Monetization Escalator

**Acquire User → Activate Team → Monetize Company**

**Team Network Effects:**
- Set metrics at team level, not individual
- Lead with team use case, messaging, plans
- Enable collaboration and multi-user adoption
- Create network effects (each new user benefits entire team)

### Differentiation Options

**1. Product Differentiation:**
- Admin features (compliance, SSO, tiered privileges, security reviews)
- User functionality (integrations, volume limits)
- Don't let sales sell self-serve SKUs—separate plans entirely

**2. Services:**
- Prioritized support, onboarding, professional services
- Watch cost dynamics—cost of sale becomes higher

**3. Process:**
- Human help with decision committees, contract negotiations, PO process
- Bare minimum—real cost of bringing in sales

**4. Pricing:**
- Volume discounts
- Danger: Without product differentiation, leads to internal channel conflict
- Best case: Sell enterprise at 3x self-serve price (Miro example)

### Product Qualified Accounts (PQAs)

Accounts that act, smell, and look like hand-raisers but aren't hand-raising.

**PQA Triggers (start with these, not behavioral):**
- **Volume:** Reached 10 users in product
- **Velocity:** Stark change in usage (e.g., uploaded 25 docs yesterday vs. 1/week)
- **Breadth of feature use:** More functionality = more value = more likely to expand

**Behavioral triggers (amplifiers only):**
- Attended webinar, visited Enterprise page, viewed Terms of Use

### Product Qualified Leads (PQLs)

You have an economic buyer in the account.

**Finding Economic Buyers:**
- Use onboarding profiling (role, seniority)
- Decision-makers usually in IT or departmental heads
- Manager/Director/VP/Chief level

**When No Buyer Exists:**
1. **Find a Champion:** Super user with high usage/NPS who introduced product
   - Partner with them (don't sell to them)
   - They become your internal salesperson
   
2. **ABM/Demand Gen:** Go find the buyer externally
   - Warm outreach: "Your team is already using our product and getting value"
   - Usage numbers as powerful icebreaker

### PLS Incentives

- **Sales-Led:** Focused on ACV (high acquisition cost loaded upfront)
- **PLS:** Focused on logo count (deals at sales floor: $10-15K)
- Expansion happens over 2-3 years to exceed top-down ACV

### Key Quotes

> "Product-led sales utilizes end-user self-serve usage as the basis for generating sales opportunities and leads."

> "Acquire a user, but Activate a team, Grow the team so you can Monetize a company."

> "The best Growth teams are automating this process."

## 10. I Bet You Are Doing Product Activation All Wrong

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/hey-b2b-i-bet-you-are-measuring-activation  
**Published:** October 5, 2023

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

90% of monetization and retention issues stem from incorrect activation measurement. Activation is taking a user from signing up to establishing a habit around your core value prop.

### The 3 Steps of Activation

**1. Setup Phase**
User performs necessary actions to get to core value prop.
- SurveyMonkey: Create survey, add questions, pick collection method
- Miro: Create board, add elements, invite/share
- Dropbox: Add/upload inventory

⚠️ **Trap:** Don't mistake setup for value delivery. Setup is only step 1.

**2. Aha Moment**
User has received and realized core value.
- SurveyMonkey: Receiving and viewing 5+ responses
- Miro: Collaborating on board with 2+ people
- Dropbox: Editing, viewing, or inviting to shared file/folder

⚠️ **Must be measurable quantitatively** and cannot be simple vanity metric (login/view doesn't mean value received).

**3. Habit Loop**
First habit formation showing they want value again.
- Measured in desired frequency: daily, weekly, monthly
- Examples: 3 out of 5 days/week over last 2 weeks; 3 out of last 4 weeks

⚠️ **Not:** "Active last week" (one-off event). Must show pattern.

## 11. Subscription Churn Metrics and Benchmarks

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/subscription-churn-benchmarks-and  
**Published:** March 28, 2025

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

Churn is the invisible ceiling on your business. No matter how strong acquisition is, if you don't address churn, it holds you back. Benchmarks are reference points, not targets—evaluate in context of your product and market.

### High-Level Benchmarks

**Net Revenue Retention (NRR):**
- Formula: (Starting MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churned) ÷ Starting MRR
- Excellent: >120% (expansion exceeds churn)
- Good: 100-120%
- Poor: <100%
- Leading SaaS (Snowflake, Datadog): 120-130%+

**Monthly Churn Rate by Segment:**
- Consumer SaaS: 3-5%
- SMB: 3-5%
- Mid-Market: 1-2%
- Enterprise: 0.5-1%

⚠️ Even 5% monthly churn = 46% annual customer loss. 10%+ monthly = 70%+ annual churn.

### Operator-Focused Churn Metrics

**First-Term Churn (Priority #1):**
- Where majority of churn happens (volume and rate)
- "Good": ~25% churn after first term (both monthly and annual)
- If >25%, you have a juicy opportunity
- Why high? Customers buy on promise; if value not delivered fast enough, they bounce

**Auto-Renew Cancellation Behavior:**
- ~50% cancel right after subscribing ("cancel so I don't forget")
- ~50% cancel in final week before renewal (mentally checked out)
- Recovery target: At least 20% of early cancellers
- **Use as early warning system** when testing new experiences

**Churn Reasons Distribution:**
1. Too expensive (~35%)
2. Don't need it anymore (~20%)
3. Missing features, hard to use, poor support, buggy, found alternative, other

**Involuntary Churn:**
- 40% of annual first-term churn due to payment failure
- #1 reason: Insufficient funds (~40% of involuntary)
- Recovery target: At least 50% through retry logic

**Mobile App Churn:**
- Day 30 retention: iOS 4.13%, Android 2.59%
- Monthly churn: ~15%
- First-term churn: 40% drop
- Annual: 50-60% monthly churn rate, 70% first-term drop

### Retention Tactics

- Drive paid feature utilization (if not using what they paid for, they won't stick)
- Get activation right (setup, aha, habit)
- Ensure healthy ongoing engagement from paid users
- Make reactivating auto-renew one click across all surfaces
- Be aggressive with payment failure comms (email + in-product)
- Show what they'll lose when cancelling (Canva does this best)
- Score users for churn risk; offer discounts/comped time for high-risk
- Offer pause option for seasonal needs
- Let users move down tiers without friction
- Move tenured monthly customers to annual (target: 20% conversion by end of year 1)
- Human touch for high-value accounts

### Key Insight

Churn is an outcome, not root cause. Real inputs are poor activation and weak engagement. Fix underlying issues first—otherwise you're just treating symptoms.

### Key Quotes

> "Once you find product-market fit and start scaling, churn becomes the invisible ceiling on your business."

> "First-term churn is where the majority of churn happens, both in volume and rate."

> "Even 5% monthly churn rate results in losing 46% of customers annually."

> "Subscription churn is an outcome, not the root cause. The real inputs are poor activation and weak engagement."

The biggest mistake with product-led sales: hiring a sales team too soon. PLS anchored on ability to use self-serve usage to generate pipeline for sales. Without hand-raisers first, laying sales on top just falls flat.

### The Wrong Approach

Hiring sales team before:
- Self-serve PMF established
- Hand-raisers appearing organically
- Clear sales floor ($10K+ deals) identified

**Result:** Sales team has no pipeline, founders/executives still doing all selling, wasted resources.

### The Right Approach

**Phase 1: Build self-serve foundation**
- Get product-led growth working
- Generate organic usage
- Create conversion to paid self-serve

**Phase 2: Wait for hand-raisers**
- Users contacting support/social asking for more
- Requests for team/enterprise features
- Natural pull from market

**Phase 3: Then add sales**
- Pipeline exists before first hire
- Sales can focus on closing, not creating demand
- Immediate productivity

### Why This Matters

Sales reps are expensive ($100-300K). Need to produce 3-5x their salary to justify investment. Without hand-raiser pipeline, impossible to achieve.

### Key Quote

> "If you're trying to find product-market fit (or PMF) with your sales team, you're not doing PLS. You are just doing top-down sales."

## 27. Is B2C and B2B Growth... the Same?

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/is-b2c-and-b2b-growth-the-same  
**Published:** August 8, 2023

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

Short answer: Nope. While B2B is increasingly mirroring B2C with intuitive interfaces and consumer-like experiences, fundamental differences remain that impact growth strategy.

### Key Differences

**B2C:**
- Individual decision-maker
- Fast purchase cycle
- Lower price points
- Emotional buying triggers
- Large addressable market
- Focus on individual activation and retention

**B2B:**
- Multiple stakeholders
- Slow purchase cycle
- Higher price points  
- Rational/ROI buying triggers
- Smaller addressable market
- **Must focus on team activation, not just individual**

### Where They Converge

- User experience expectations (B2C sets bar for B2B)
- Product-led entry points
- Self-serve activation
- Freemium/trial models
- Mobile-first design

### Where They Diverge

- **Sales complexity:** B2B needs sales touch for enterprise
- **Expansion model:** B2B requires team network effects
- **Retention drivers:** B2B is about organizational value, not just individual habit
- **Monetization:** B2B has expansion/upsell as core strategy

### The Lesson

Don't blindly apply B2C growth tactics to B2B. The team/organizational layer fundamentally changes the game. 

**B2B golden rule:** Acquire users, activate teams, monetize companies.

### Key Quote

> "Short answer: Nope."

## 29. Create Growth Turbo Boosts: User Conferences

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/create-growth-turbo-boosts-user-conferences  
**Published:** April 22, 2024

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

User conferences are powerful growth "turbo boosts"—one-off tactics that create step-function growth spikes. They can accelerate all growth loops simultaneously when done right.

### Why Conferences Work

- **Network effects acceleration:** Users meet each other, form relationships around your product
- **Content generation:** Presentations, case studies, testimonials for months/years
- **Community building:** Users become advocates, support each other
- **Product feedback:** Direct conversations with power users
- **Sales pipeline:** High-intent prospects in one place

### Conference Types

**1. User Conference (Annual):**
- Large flagship event
- Product announcements
- Customer stories
- Training/certification

**2. Roadshow (Multi-city):**
- Smaller, regional events
- Lower cost per attendee
- Broader geographic reach

**3. Virtual/Hybrid:**
- Lower barrier to attendance
- Easier to scale
- Less relationship depth

### Success Factors

- **Customer stories first:** Let users present, not just company
- **Networking time:** Don't overschedule—relationships form in hallways
- **Product access:** Show roadmap, gather feedback
- **Community formation:** Help attendees connect with each other
- **Follow-up:** Capitalize on momentum post-event

### Measuring Success

- Attendance numbers
- Pipeline generated
- Content created
- Community activity post-event
- Retention of attendees vs. non-attendees

### Key Quote

> "User conferences are powerful growth 'turbo boosts'—one-off tactics that create step-function growth spikes."

## 30. Six Popular Growth Tactics That Don't Actually Work

**Source:** https://www.elenaverna.com/p/six-popular-growth-tactics-that-dont  
**Published:** September 6, 2024

### Core Insights and Key Takeaways

"Get growth quick" works about as well as "get rich quick." These tactics are popular but don't deliver sustainable results. Recognize them to avoid wasting resources.

### The 6 Tactics

**1. Viral Loops as Primary Strategy:**
- Viral loops are outcomes of great product, not tactics you can bolt on
- Can't manufacture virality where natural sharing doesn't exist
- Focus on product value first

**2. Growth Hacking (the bad kind):**
- Short-term tricks that don't scale
- Often violate platform terms or user trust
- Create spikes followed by crashes

**3. Aggressive Email Capture:**
- Pop-ups, gates, interstitials that annoy users
- High volume, low quality leads
- Damages brand and trust

**4. Buying Users:**
- Paid acquisition without retention plan
- CAC unsustainable without LTV support
- Creates vanity metrics, not real growth

**5. Feature Gating Everything:**
- Paywalling basic functionality
- Frustrates users before they experience value
- Kills activation and word of mouth

**6. Copying Competitor Tactics:**
- What works for them may not work for you
- Different context, audience, timing
- Playing catch-up instead of leading

### What Actually Works

- Building genuine product value
- Creating sustainable growth loops
- Optimizing existing channels before adding new ones
- Measuring retention, not just acquisition
- Testing incrementally

### Key Quote

> "'Get growth quick' works about as well as 'get rich quick.'"
