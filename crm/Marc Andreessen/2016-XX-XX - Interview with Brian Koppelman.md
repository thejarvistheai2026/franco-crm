# Marc Andreessen - Interview with Brian Koppelman (2016)

**Date:** 2016  
**Source:** The Moment with Brian Koppelman  
**Interviewer:** Brian Koppelman

---

## About This Interview

Andreessen discusses his early influences, founding Netscape, the browser wars, and his evolution as an investor and thinker.

---

## The Interview

### On Growing Up

**Brian Koppelman:** How did you get into computers?

**Marc Andreessen:** I grew up in rural Wisconsin. Small town, no tech industry. But my dad bought a computer for the house when I was 10. I just started playing with it.

**Koppelman:** No one around you was into tech?

**Andreessen:** No one. I taught myself from books. The library had programming books. I'd check them out, type in the code, see what happened.

It was isolating but also freeing. No one told me what I couldn't do.

### On College

**Koppelman:** You went to the University of Illinois. Why there?

**Andreessen:** They had a supercomputer center. One of the best in the world. I wanted access to that.

I worked at the NCSA—the National Center for Supercomputing Applications. That's where the web browser was born.

### On Creating Mosaic

**Koppelman:** Tell me about Mosaic.

**Andreessen:** In 1992, the web existed but it was text-only. Tim Berners-Lee had created it, but it was for scientists. Not user-friendly.

I thought: what if we added images? What if we made it graphical? What if normal people could use it?

I built Mosaic with Eric Bina. We added images, clickable links, a back button. It seems obvious now, but at the time it was revolutionary.

**Koppelman:** How old were you?

**Andreessen:** 22. Just out of college.

### On Netscape

**Koppelman:** From Mosaic to Netscape. What happened?

**Andreessen:** The University owned Mosaic. They wouldn't let us commercialize it. So we left and built a new browser from scratch.

Netscape Navigator. We launched in 1994. By 1995, we were the fastest-growing software company in history.

**Koppelman:** The IPO was huge.

**Andreessen:** The Netscape IPO in 1995 kicked off the dot-com boom. We went public at $28, closed at $58. I was 24. It was surreal.

### On Microsoft

**Koppelman:** Then Microsoft came after you.

**Andreessen:** Yeah. Bill Gates saw the web as a threat to Windows. He was right—it was.

Microsoft bundled Internet Explorer with Windows. Gave it away for free. We couldn't compete with free when it was pre-installed on every PC.

**Koppelman:** How did that feel?

**Andreessen:** Devastating. We had built something amazing. And we were losing to bundling, not to a better product.

But it was also a lesson. Distribution beats product. Microsoft had distribution. We didn't.

### On Loudcloud/Opsware

**Koppelman:** After Netscape, you started Loudcloud.

**Andreessen:** Yeah. We were too early on cloud computing. 1999. The technology wasn't ready. The market wasn't ready.

We pivoted to software—Opsware. Data center automation. Sold to HP for $1.6 billion in 2007.

**Koppelman:** Lessons from that?

**Andreessen:** Timing matters. Being too early is the same as being wrong. Also: persistence. We almost died multiple times. But we kept going.

### On Becoming a VC

**Koppelman:** Why venture capital?

**Andreessen:** After selling Opsware, I had time and money. I started angel investing. Loved it.

Then Ben Horowitz and I decided to start a firm. Andreessen Horowitz. 2009.

**Koppelman:** Why then?

**Andreessen:** The financial crisis. Everyone was pessimistic. But we saw opportunity. The iPhone had launched. Cloud computing was real. Social networks were emerging.

It was the perfect time to start a tech-focused VC firm.

### On What Makes a Good Founder

**Koppelman:** What do you look for in founders?

**Andreessen:** Three things:

**1. Intelligence**
Not just IQ. The ability to learn fast, adapt, see patterns.

**2. Courage**
Startups are hard. You need people who will keep going when things look impossible.

**3. Ethics**
This is underrated. You can't build a great company with dishonest people. Trust is everything.

### On Luck

**Koppelman:** How much of success is luck?

**Andreessen:** A lot. But luck favors the prepared.

I got lucky with Mosaic. But I had spent 10 years learning to code. I was ready when the opportunity came.

You can't control luck. But you can control whether you're ready when luck strikes.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Self-Taught
Andreessen taught himself programming from library books in rural Wisconsin.

### 2. Timing Matters
Being too early (Loudcloud) is as bad as being wrong.

### 3. Distribution Beats Product
Microsoft won the browser war through bundling, not better technology.

### 4. Persistence
Netscape lost to Microsoft. Loudcloud pivoted. Both required persistence.

### 5. Luck + Preparation
Luck matters, but you must be ready when it comes.
