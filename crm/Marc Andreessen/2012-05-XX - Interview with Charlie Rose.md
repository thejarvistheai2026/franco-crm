# Marc Andreessen - Interview with Charlie Rose (2012)

**Date:** May 2012  
**Source:** The Charlie Rose Show, PBS  
**Interviewer:** Charlie Rose

---

## About This Interview

Following his "Software Is Eating the World" essay, Andreessen discusses the future of technology, startups, and innovation with Charlie Rose.

---

## The Interview

### On Software Eating the World

**Charlie Rose:** You wrote that software is eating the world. Explain.

**Marc Andreessen:** Every industry is becoming a software industry. The companies that win are the ones that understand this and build software at their core.

**Rose:** Give me an example.

**Andreessen:** Look at Amazon. They're a software company that happens to sell things. Their competitive advantage is their software—their logistics, their recommendations, their platform.

Barnes & Noble is a retail company that happens to have a website. That's why they're losing.

### On Silicon Valley

**Rose:** Why does Silicon Valley keep producing these companies?

**Andreessen:** It's the culture. Failure is acceptable here. In most places, if you start a company and it fails, you're damaged goods. Here, people say "what did you learn?"

Also, the density. Everyone knows everyone. Information flows fast. Ideas cross-pollinate.

### On Innovation

**Rose:** Are we in a new tech bubble?

**Andreessen:** I don't think so. In 1999, companies went public with no revenue. Today, the big tech companies have massive revenues and profits. Facebook, Google, Apple—these are real businesses.

The valuations might be high, but the businesses are real.

**Rose:** What about Facebook's IPO?

**Andreessen:** I'm on the board, so I can't say much. But I will say: Facebook has a billion users. A billion. That's unprecedented in human history. The monetization will follow.

### On Entrepreneurship

**Rose:** What makes a great entrepreneur?

**Andreessen:** Three things:

**1. Determination**
Startups are hard. Most fail. You have to be willing to push through years of difficulty.

**2. Flexibility**
Your first idea is probably wrong. You have to be willing to pivot.

**3. Intelligence**
Not just IQ, but the ability to learn fast, adapt, understand your users and your market.

### On Venture Capital

**Rose:** How do you pick winners?

**Andreessen:** We don't. We can't. What we do is:

1. Meet lots of founders
2. Look for the combination of big idea + great team
3. Invest in many
4. Help them as much as we can
5. Double down on the ones that work

VC is a hits business. One Airbnb pays for fifty failures.

### On the Future

**Rose:** What's next?

**Andreessen:** Three big areas:

**1. Mobile**
Smartphones are the most important technology since the PC. Everyone will have one. Everything will run on them.

**2. Cloud**
Computing moving to the cloud. Companies won't own servers. They'll rent computing power.

**3. Big Data**
We can now process massive amounts of data. This enables new applications—personalization, prediction, optimization.

### On Education

**Rose:** Will technology change education?

**Andreessen:** Absolutely. The current system is broken—too expensive, not accessible enough.

Online education will democratize access. The best professors teaching millions of students. That's coming.

### On Healthcare

**Rose:** What about healthcare?

**Andreessen:** Healthcare is ripe for disruption. It's information-based, but the information doesn't flow. Records are siloed. Patients don't have access to their own data.

Software will fix this. It will take time—healthcare is heavily regulated—but it will happen.

## Key Insights

### 1. Core Competency Is Software
Companies that treat software as their core capability win.

### 2. Silicon Valley Culture
Acceptance of failure and information density create innovation.

### 3. This Is Not 1999
Current tech companies have real revenues and profits.

### 4. Mobile, Cloud, Data
Three major waves that would define the next decade.

### 5. Education and Healthcare
Two massive industries ripe for software disruption.
