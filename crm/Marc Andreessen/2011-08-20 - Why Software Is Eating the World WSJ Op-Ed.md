# Marc Andreessen - Why Software Is Eating the World (WSJ Op-Ed)

**Date:** August 20, 2011  
**Source:** The Wall Street Journal  
**Author:** Marc Andreessen

---

## About This Essay

Perhaps the most influential technology essay of the 2010s. Andreessen argued that every industry would be transformed by software, predicting the wave of digital transformation that followed.

---

## The Essay

### The Premise

**Marc Andreessen:** More and more major businesses and industries are being run on software and delivered as online services—from movies to agriculture to national defense.

Many of the winners are Silicon Valley-style entrepreneurial technology companies that are invading and overturning established industry structures.

Over the next 10 years, I expect many more industries to be disrupted by software, with new world-beating Silicon Valley companies doing the disruption in more cases than not.

### Six Decades of Computing

**Andreessen:** My own theory is that we are in the middle of a dramatic and broad technological and economic shift in which software companies are poised to take over large swathes of the economy.

More and more major businesses and industries are being run on software and delivered as online services.

### The Evidence

**Andreessen:** Today, the world's largest bookseller, Amazon, is a software company—its core capability is its amazing software engine for selling virtually everything online, no retail stores necessary.

On top of that, while Borders was thrashing in the throes of impending bankruptcy, Amazon rearranged its web site to promote its Kindle digital books over physical books for the first time.

Now even the books themselves are software.

**Music:** The world's largest music retailer, iTunes, is a software company.

**Video:** Netflix is a software company that delivers video over the Internet.

**Recruiting:** LinkedIn is a software company that delivers recruiting services.

**Finance:** The best new stock exchange, BATS, is a software company.

**Photography:** The fastest growing entertainment companies, Zynga and Rovio, are software companies.

**Telecom:** Skype is a software company that disrupted the phone industry.

**Retail:** Groupon and Foursquare are software companies disrupting retail.

**Defense:** The most powerful military force in the world, the U.S. military, is heavily software-driven.

**Automotive:** Today's cars now have more software than mechanical parts.

**Agriculture:** Precision agriculture using GPS and software is transforming farming.

### Why Now?

**Andreessen:** Why is this happening now?

Six decades into the computer revolution, four decades since the invention of the microprocessor, and two decades into the rise of the modern Internet, all of the technology required to transform industries through software finally works and can be widely delivered at global scale.

**1. Computing is cheap:**
Over two billion people now access the Internet, and billions more have mobile phones.

**2. Software distribution is instant:**
Software can be delivered at zero marginal cost over the Internet.

**3. Software development is global:**
The tools and infrastructure are mature and accessible.

### The New Industrial Revolution

**Andreessen:** This is a train wreck in slow motion for many traditional businesses.

The retail industry is being restructured by software. The financial services industry is being restructured by software. The entertainment industry is being restructured by software. The education industry is being restructured by software. The healthcare industry is being restructured by software.

And this is still early days. We are in the deployment phase of the software revolution.

### The Opportunity

**Andreessen:** For entrepreneurs, this is the opportunity of a lifetime. Every industry is up for grabs. Every industry is being reimagined as a software business.

For investors, this is the best time to be investing in technology since the 1990s—perhaps better, because the underlying infrastructure is so much more mature.

For established companies, this is a wake-up call. You need to become a software company, or you will be disrupted by one.

### The Future

**Andreessen:** Over the next 10 years, the battles between incumbents and software-powered insurgents will be epic.

Joseph Schumpeter coined the term "creative destruction" to describe how new innovations destroy old industries. We are entering a period of maximum creative destruction.

The future belongs to software.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Software Is the Core Competency
Companies that treat software as their core capability win. Companies that treat it as a cost center lose.

### 2. Distribution Has Changed
Software can reach billions instantly at zero marginal cost. Physical distribution is no longer a moat.

### 3. Every Industry Is Vulnerable
No industry is safe from software disruption. If you're not a software company, you're at risk.

### 4. This Is Still Early
In 2011, Andreessen saw this as the beginning. The transformation would take decades.

### 5. The Infrastructure Is Ready
Cheap computing, global Internet, mature tools—everything needed for software to eat the world was in place.

---

## Impact

This essay became the defining thesis of the 2010s tech boom. It:
- Legitimized software as a serious industry
- Justified massive tech valuations
- Predicted the disruption of retail, media, finance, and more
- Coined a phrase that became the era's mantra

It was written right before the mobile boom, before the cloud explosion, before AI. Andreessen saw the pattern before most.
