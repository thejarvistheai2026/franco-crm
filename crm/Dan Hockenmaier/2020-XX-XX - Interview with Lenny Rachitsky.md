# Dan Hockenmaier - Interview with Lenny Rachitsky

**Date:** 2020  
**Source:** Lenny's Newsletter  
**Interviewer:** Lenny Rachitsky

---

## About This Interview

Dan discusses marketplace strategy, common mistakes, and lessons from his time at Thumbtack.

---

## The Interview

### On Thumbtack's Early Days

**Lenny Rachitsky:** How did Thumbtack start?

**Dan Hockenmaier:** Thumbtack started with a simple idea: help people find local service professionals.

The founders noticed that finding a plumber, electrician, or tutor was surprisingly hard. Yellow Pages were outdated. Craigslist was sketchy. There had to be a better way.

**Rachitsky:** What was the initial approach?

**Hockenmaier:** They manually onboarded service providers. Called them, verified them, added them to the platform. Then they waited for customers.

This was pre-2010, so they didn't have the marketplace playbook yet. They were figuring it out as they went.

### On the Pivot

**Rachitsky:** Tell me about the pivot.

**Hockenmaier:** We started with a directory model. Service providers paid for listings. Customers browsed and called.

This was fine, but it wasn't a real marketplace. There were no transactions happening on the platform. No network effects.

We pivoted to a lead-gen model. Customers posted jobs. Service providers paid to quote on those jobs. This was much better.

**Rachitsky:** What made it better?

**Hockenmaier:** Finally, we could measure transactions. We could see which suppliers were active. We could optimize for matches, not just listings.

And we had a real revenue model—service providers paid per lead, not per listing.

### On Solving Chicken-and-Egg

**Rachitsky:** How did you solve the chicken-and-egg problem?

**Hockenmaier:** We focused on supply first. Here's why:

In home services, supply is harder to acquire than demand. Anyone can post "I need a plumber." But finding qualified, available plumbers is hard.

So we invested heavily in supply acquisition. We had a team calling service providers. We offered them free leads early on. We made it easy to join.

Once we had supply, demand followed.

### On Liquidity

**Rachitsky:** When did you know you had liquidity?

**Hockenmaier:** Three signs:

**1. Match rate**
When most customer requests got multiple quotes. We aimed for 3-5 quotes per request.

**2. Time to first quote**
When service providers responded within hours, not days.

**3. Conversion rate**
When a meaningful percentage of requests led to hired professionals.

Once those metrics looked good in a category, we knew we had liquidity.

### On Expansion

**Rachitsky:** How did you decide when to expand to new categories?

**Hockenmaier:** We waited until we had liquidity in core categories—plumbing, electrical, tutoring, etc.

Then we expanded category by category. Each new category needed its own supply acquisition push.

We didn't expand geographically until we were confident in our model. And even then, we did it slowly.

**Rachitsky:** What constrained growth?

**Hockenmaier:** Supply acquisition. Always. Finding, vetting, and onboarding service professionals was expensive and time-consuming.

Demand was easier. Once we had supply, we could acquire customers through SEO, SEM, word of mouth.

### On Take Rate

**Rachitsky:** How did you think about take rate?

**Hockenmaier:** Early on, we kept it low. We wanted to attract suppliers.

As we built liquidity and differentiation, we gradually increased it. But we were always careful—too high, and suppliers would leave.

**Rachitsky:** What's the right take rate?

**Hockenmaier:** It depends. Uber takes 20-30%. Airbnb takes 3-15%. Amazon takes 8-15%.

The key question: what value are you providing? If you're providing discovery, payment, trust, scheduling—higher rates are justified.

### On Disintermediation

**Rachitsky:** How did you handle disintermediation?

**Hockenmaier:** This was a constant battle. Customers would find a plumber on Thumbtack, then hire them directly next time.

We combated this by:
- Building trust (reviews, verifications)
- Providing ongoing value (scheduling, payments)
- Creating lock-in (subscription services, guarantees)

But you can never fully prevent it. You just have to provide enough value that staying makes sense.

### On Competition

**Rachitsky:** Were you worried about competition?

**Hockenmaier:** Early on, no. We were building something new.

Later, yes. HomeAdvisor grew fast. Angie's List (before the merger) was strong.

But marketplace competition is interesting. Once you have liquidity, it's hard to displace you. Buyers and suppliers both have switching costs.

So we focused on building the best experience and maintaining liquidity. That's the best defense.

### On Leaving Thumbtack

**Rachitsky:** Why did you leave?

**Hockenmaier:** I was there for 6 years. Thumbtack was doing well. I wanted a new challenge.

I joined Reforge to help other companies with marketplace strategy. And to work on a broader set of problems.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Supply Side Constrained
For Thumbtack, supply was always harder than demand.

### 2. Lead-Gen Model Worked
The request-for-quote model created measurable transactions.

### 3. Liquidity Metrics Matter
Match rate, time to quote, conversion—track these obsessively.

### 4. Geographic Expansion Last
Wait until you have the model working before expanding.

### 5. Disintermediation is Inevitable
Build enough value that customers stay anyway.
