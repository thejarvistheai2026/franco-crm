# Dan Hockenmaier - Why Marketplaces Win

**Date:** 2019  
**Source:** Reforge Essay

---

## About This Essay

Dan Hockenmaier, former Head of Strategy at Thumbtack, explains why marketplace businesses are structurally superior and how to build them successfully.

---

## The Essay

### The Power of Marketplaces

**Dan Hockenmaier:** Marketplaces are the most defensible business model in the world. Once they reach liquidity, they're nearly impossible to compete with.

Why? Because they have built-in network effects. More buyers attract more sellers. More sellers attract more buyers. This creates a virtuous cycle that compounds over time.

### What Makes a Marketplace

**Hockenmaier:** A marketplace has three components:

**1. Suppliers** — People who provide goods or services
**2. Buyers** — People who consume goods or services
**3. Platform** — The infrastructure that connects them

The platform doesn't own inventory. It doesn't employ suppliers. It simply facilitates transactions and takes a cut.

This is why marketplaces are so capital-efficient. Uber doesn't own cars. Airbnb doesn't own homes. Amazon (marketplace) doesn't own most inventory.

### The Liquidity Challenge

**Hockenmaier:** The hardest part of building a marketplace is reaching liquidity.

Liquidity is the minimum critical mass where transactions happen naturally. Before liquidity, the marketplace is useless. After liquidity, it's unstoppable.

The chicken-and-egg problem: Buyers won't come without suppliers. Suppliers won't come without buyers.

### Solving the Chicken-and-Egg Problem

**Hockenmaier:** There are several strategies:

**1. Single-player mode**
Provide value to one side before the other exists. OpenTable provided reservation software to restaurants before diners cared. Then they added the marketplace.

**2. Curated launch**
Start with a small geography or category. Dominate it, then expand. Uber launched in SF first.

**3. Supply subsidies**
Pay suppliers to join early. Subsidize losses until liquidity.

**4. Bring your own audience**
If you already have users, add marketplace functionality. Amazon added third-party sellers to existing shoppers.

**5. Vertical integration**
Own supply initially, then open up. Amazon sold its own products before adding marketplace.

### The Three Phases of Marketplace Growth

**Hockenmaier:** Marketplaces evolve through three phases:

**Phase 1: Liquidity (0-2 years)**
- Focus on supply
- Solve chicken-and-egg
- Get to minimum transaction volume

**Phase 2: Growth (2-5 years)**
- Scale both sides
- Expand geographies/categories
- Optimize take rate

**Phase 3: Optimization (5+ years)**
- Defend position
- Increase monetization
- Expand adjacent services

### The Metrics That Matter

**Hockenmaier:** Key marketplace metrics:

**Liquidity metrics:**
- Match rate (% of searches with results)
- Time to fill (how fast supply meets demand)
- Utilization rate (% of supply actively transacting)

**Growth metrics:**
- GMV (Gross Merchandise Value)
- Take rate (% of GMV kept as revenue)
- Network effects strength (new user acquisition cost declining)

**Retention metrics:**
- Repeat purchase rate
- Supplier retention
- Buyer retention

### Common Marketplace Mistakes

**Hockenmaier:** I see these constantly:

**1. Underfunding supply acquisition**
You need to subsidize supply early. Most marketplaces underinvest here.

**2. Expanding too fast**
Don't expand until you have liquidity in your core market. Premature expansion kills marketplaces.

**3. Ignoring retention**
Acquisition is easy. Getting buyers to purchase again is hard. Focus on repeat rate.

**4. Taking too much too early**
Don't optimize take rate before you have liquidity. Lower take rates accelerate growth.

**5. Treating both sides equally**
Usually one side is harder to acquire. Identify which side is the constraint and focus there.

### Why Marketplaces Fail

**Hockenmaier:** Marketplaces fail when:

**Low fragmentation:** If supply is concentrated (few big players), marketplace adds no value.

**High transaction frequency:** If people buy once every 10 years (weddings, funerals), retention is impossible.

**No network effects:** If adding users doesn't improve the experience.

**Commoditized supply:** If all suppliers are identical, buyers have no reason to stay.

**High disintermediation risk:** If buyers and suppliers can easily go around the platform.

### The Thumbtack Story

**Hockenmaier:** At Thumbtack, we connected homeowners with local service professionals.

We solved chicken-and-egg by:
1. Scraping existing service provider data to seed supply
2. Letting buyers post jobs (request for quote)
3. Paying suppliers to respond early on

Once we hit liquidity in a category (plumbers, electricians), growth became self-sustaining.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Network Effects are Everything
Marketplaces compound. Each user makes the product better for others.

### 2. Liquidity is the Goal
Everything before liquidity is hard. Everything after is easy.

### 3. One Side Usually Constrains
Identify which side (supply or demand) is harder to acquire. Focus there.

### 4. Early Subsidies are Required
Expect to lose money early. Investment in liquidity pays off later.

### 5. Retention Over Acquisition
Repeat purchases matter more than new users.
