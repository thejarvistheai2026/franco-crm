## Commencement Address Transcript

### Opening: Congratulations and Reality

**Jensen Huang:** Congratulations, Class of 2024. You made it.

I'm honored to be here at Caltech, one of the greatest institutions of science and engineering in the world. I speak to you today not just as CEO of NVIDIA, but as an engineer who still writes code, still reads papers, and still gets excited about technological breakthroughs.

I want to talk about your future. And I want to start with some uncomfortable truths.

### On Setbacks and Pain

**Jensen Huang:** First, I hope that you suffer setbacks. I hope that you experience pain. I hope you get embarrassed. I hope you fail.

Now that may sound strange coming from a commencement speaker. But hear me out.

Every setback I had, I learned from. Every embarrassment made me more humble. Every failure taught me something that success never could.

The resilience you build from failure, the humility you build from embarrassment, the courage you build from pain — these are the traits that will serve you for a lifetime.

When I started NVIDIA, we made so many mistakes. Our first product failed completely. We chose the wrong architecture. We almost went bankrupt. I had to tell my employees we might not make payroll.

Those were hard days. But looking back, they taught me more than any success could have.

### The World Being Reinvented

**Jensen Huang:** You're graduating into a world being completely reinvented. You are graduating into the most extraordinary technological moment since the beginning of the computer age.

The computer age has entered a new chapter — the chapter of generative AI. The computer is learning to reason. The computer is learning to generate. The computer is learning to understand.

This is a fundamental shift. Computing used to be about retrieval — you type a query, you get a document. Now it's about generation — you describe what you want, the computer creates it.

This changes everything. It changes how we work. It changes how we learn. It changes how we discover.

And you are at the center of this transformation. Caltech graduates understand the science, the math, the engineering to make this real.

### On Doing Dumb Things

**Jensen Huang:** Let me share something else. Smart people do dumb things. I did so many dumb things.

I dropped out of a PhD program. I started a graphics chip company when everyone said graphics was a dead end. I invested in a technology direction that most experts said was wrong.

So many smart people do dumb things. The key is to learn from your mistakes. The key is to have the humility to admit when you're wrong and change direction.

At NVIDIA, we almost failed because we were too stubborn about our architecture. When we realized we were wrong, we pivoted. We threw out years of work and started over. That pivot saved the company.

Don't let pride prevent you from admitting mistakes. Don't let sunk cost fallacy trap you in bad decisions.

### The Responsibility of Excellence

**Jensen Huang:** You graduate from Caltech. That means something special. The world will expect more from you. You should expect more from yourself.

You have world-class training in science and engineering. You have the tools to solve hard problems. You have a responsibility to use those tools for good.

The world needs you. The world needs Caltech. The world needs you to solve hard problems.

Climate change. Disease. Food security. Energy. These are engineering problems. These are Caltech problems.

And now AI is opening entirely new frontiers. Protein folding. Climate modeling. Fusion energy. These are problems that might have been unsolvable in your parents' generation. But they might be solvable in yours.

### Learning by Doing

**Jensen Huang:** I want to tell you one more thing. Learn by doing.

You can study physics for years. But you don't really understand it until you build something that uses physics.

You can read about machine learning papers. But you don't really understand it until you train a model and watch it fail and iterate and improve.

The best way to learn engineering is to engineer. The best way to learn science is to experiment.

Don't wait for perfection. Don't wait until you know everything. Build things. Break things. Learn things.

That's how I learned. I still write code. I still debug problems. I still learn every day. And I'm 61 years old now.

### Closing: The Journey Ahead

**Jensen Huang:** Class of 2024, you begin your journey today into a world of extraordinary possibility.

AI will change every industry you enter. Every company you join. Every problem you tackle.

You have the training. You have the intellect. You have the opportunities.

But I hope you also have setbacks. I hope you feel pain. I hope you fail sometimes. Because that's how you'll grow.

The world needs your resilience. The world needs your humility. The world needs your courage.

Congratulations again, Class of 2024. Go change the world. And don't forget to learn from your dumb mistakes along the way.

Thank you.

## Analysis

**Why this speech matters:**
- Rare commencement address from active tech CEO
- Counter-intuitive advice ("hope for setbacks")
- Connects personal failures to broader life lessons
- Inspires without being preachy
- Acknowledges smart people make mistakes

**Comparisons to other commencement speeches:**
- Steve Jobs Stanford 2005: More personal narrative, cancer mortality
- Jensen Caltech 2024: More technical framing, AI transformation, embracing failure
- Both emphasize that "you can't connect the dots looking forward"
