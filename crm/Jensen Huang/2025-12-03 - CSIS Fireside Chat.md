## Interview Transcript

### Opening: The Role of CSIS

**Jensen Huang:** Thank you, Dr. Hamre, for hosting this. CSIS plays a vital role in these conversations.

I've come to appreciate that technology and policy are inseparable now. When you're building the infrastructure for a $100 trillion industry transformation, policymakers and technologists need to speak the same language.

### China's AI Development

**Dr. Hamre:** How should we think about China's AI progress?

**Jensen Huang:** With clear eyes and respect. Here's the reality:

China has 50% of the world's AI researchers. Walk the halls of OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind, NVIDIA. You'll find Chinese researchers everywhere. These are not Chinese AI researchers. They are world-class AI researchers who happen to be Chinese.

You cannot hold back Chinese AI development. You cannot stop a billion people from advancing technology. Any policy built on this assumption is built on sand.

**Dr. Hamre:** What about DeepSeek specifically?

**Jensen Huang:** DeepSeek is deeply excellent work. Their models are competitive. Their training innovations are real. Their MoE architecture is innovative.

To deny this, to dismiss it as imitation, is a lack of confidence so deep that I can't tolerate it. Competition makes everyone better. America needs competition to stay sharp. China advancing AI is not a failure of American policy — it's the natural consequence of global scientific progress.

### The AI Diffusion Framework

**Dr. Hamre:** You've been critical of the AI diffusion restrictions. Explain your thinking.

**Jensen Huang:** The framework as written is exactly wrong.

Here's why: AI is a full-stack technology. Leadership requires excellence at every layer — chips, systems, infrastructure, models, applications. You cannot sacrifice one layer to protect another. That's not strategy, that's self-sabotage.

The framework restricts American companies from selling chips to China. But China represents 50% of the AI research market. By ceding that market, we weaken our own ecosystem. We reduce the flywheel that makes American technology dominant.

The positive feedback loop depends on scale: more developers, more applications, more demand, more investment, better technology, more developers. Breaking that cycle hurts American competitiveness.

**Dr. Hamre:** What should the framework be instead?

**Jensen Huang:** Accelerate adoption of American technology everywhere. Compete vigorously in every market. Win on merit. Generate tax revenue, create jobs, offset trade deficits.

The goal is not to slow China. The goal is for America to go faster.

### National Security Considerations

**Dr. Hamre:** What about national security concerns with advanced AI?

**Jensen Huang:** Valid concerns. But let's be precise about the actual risks.

Open models like Llama have guardrails. They can be deployed securely in controlled environments. The compute itself is not the weapon — the application layer determines usage.

We should focus security efforts on:
1. Sensitive model weights for frontier models
2. Inference capabilities that could enable harm
3. Supply chain integrity
4. Domestic AI infrastructure resilience

Not on preventing Chinese researchers from accessing GPUs for legitimate research.

### Huawei and Competition

**Dr. Hamre:** How do you view Huawei as a competitor?

**Jensen Huang:** Directly: Huawei is a formidable competitor. They are a world-class technology company. Their Ascend chips are real. Their software stack is developing. Their researchers are excellent.

Attempting to compete by denying them market access is short-term thinking. Long-term competitiveness requires relentless innovation, not market restrictions.

America wins by building superior technology, not by preventing alternatives from existing.

### Energy and Geopolitics

**Dr. Hamre:** How does energy factor into AI competition?

**Jensen Huang:** Energy is the critical constraint.

Training frontier models requires gigawatts of power. Countries with energy abundance — Saudi Arabia, UAE, parts of the US — have strategic advantages.

But here's the key: accelerated computing is far more energy-efficient than alternatives. Blackwell delivers 25x better performance per watt than CPU alternatives.

The countries that build AI infrastructure with American accelerated computing technology will have sustainable, scalable advantages.

### The Future of Competition

**Dr. Hamre:** Where does this competition go over the next decade?

**Jensen Huang:** Here's my honest assessment:

China will be a major AI power regardless of American policy. They have the talent, the resources, and the will.

America's strategy should be to compete so effectively that Chinese technology is always追赶 (catch-up), never领导 (leading).

That requires:
1. Continued investment in basic research
2. Massive infrastructure buildout for AI factories
3. Immigration policies that attract global talent
4. Education systems that produce world-class researchers
5. Regulatory clarity that enables innovation

Restricting market access is a distraction from this strategy. It feels like action, but it undermines the goal.

### Closing: A Call for Pragmatism

**Jensen Huang:** I deeply believe in American competitiveness. NVIDIA is an American company. Our technology, our talent, our headquarters are here.

But competitiveness requires clear-eyed assessment of reality. China will advance. Competition will intensify. The question is whether American policy accelerates our progress or impedes it.

I urge policymakers to focus on winning, not on preventing others from competing.

Thank you.
