## Interview Transcript

### Opening: The "No Task Beneath Me" Philosophy

**Dean Levin:** Jensen, you said that one of your leadership principles is that no task is beneath you. Why is that?

**Jensen Huang:** No task is beneath me because I remember what I did. I cleaned a lot of toilets. I mean a lot of toilets. I've cleaned more toilets than all of you combined. And I'll still do it if that's necessary.

You know, when you're in a reform school in Kentucky at age 10, you clean toilets every day. It's expected. And I was happy to do it. And I would be happy to do it today.

I started working at Denny's when I was 15. Graveyard shift. I was a dishwasher, busboy, waiter. And I still would do any of those things today. No task is beneath me because I've done them and I respect the people who do them.

### Career Planning and the Future

**Dean Levin:** What advice would you give Stanford students about career planning?

**Jensen Huang:** If you have excellent career planning, none of us would work here. If we had excellent career advice, we'd all be in trouble. I never had career advice. I just did what I loved.

I studied electrical engineering because I loved it. I went to work at AMD because I loved chips. I started Nvidia because I believed in graphics computing. I didn't think about what the career implications were.

If someone told me in 1993 that GPUs would be how the world does AI, I would have told them they were crazy. We just focused on building great graphics. We didn't try to predict the future. We tried to create it.

### AI, Computing, and First Principles

**Dean Levin:** How do you think about AI and the future of computing?

**Jensen Huang:** We're at the beginning of a new computing era. The computer as we know it has been reinvented. What used to be retrieval-based, now it's generative. What used to be instruction-based, now it's intention-based. You tell the computer what you want, and it figures out how to do it.

This is a fundamental change. It's bigger than the PC revolution. It's bigger than the mobile revolution. This is a new way of computing entirely.

### The Power of Long-Term Focus

**Jensen Huang:** The smartest thing I ever did was to just not give up. We're still doing the same thing we started 30 years ago. Accelerated computing. Parallel processing. The vision hasn't changed. The technology has evolved, the applications have expanded, but it's the same fundamental belief.

People ask me when I retire. I say: "I'm doing exactly what I always wanted to do. Why would I stop?"

### Education and First Principles

**Dean Levin:** How has your education shaped your thinking?

**Jensen Huang:** Stanford taught me to think about first principles. Why do things work the way they do? Not just how, but why.

I tell our engineers: Don't just learn the API. Don't just learn the tool. Understand why the architecture is designed this way. Because when you understand the fundamentals, you can anticipate change. You can see around corners.

Computer graphics is all about physics, about light, about geometry. AI is about mathematics, about pattern recognition, about learning. If you understand the fundamentals, you can apply them to anything.

### Leadership Style

**Dean Levin:** How would you describe your leadership style?

**Jensen Huang:** I try to lead from first principles. I want people to understand why we're doing what we're doing, not just what to do.

I also believe in being approachable. Anyone can email me. Anyone can challenge me. I want people to push back. If everyone agrees with me, we're not thinking hard enough.

And I believe in the long term. We're building for 10 years from now, not next quarter. That gives you freedom to make hard choices.

But most importantly, I believe that no task is beneath anyone. I started at the bottom. I respect people who do the work, whatever the work is.

### Closing: Advice for Stanford Students

**Jensen Huang:** I want every Stanford student to remember you're going to graduate from one of the finest universities in the world, and you are going to have opportunities that most people don't have, and you carry a responsibility for that.

Take on hard problems. Do something that matters. And remember: No task is beneath you.
