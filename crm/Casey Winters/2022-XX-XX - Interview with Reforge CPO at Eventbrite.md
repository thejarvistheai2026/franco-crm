# Casey Winters - Interview with Reforge (CPO at Eventbrite)

**Date:** 2022  
**Source:** Reforge Podcast  
**Interviewer:** Reforge Team

---

## About This Interview

Casey discusses his transition to Eventbrite, lessons from Pinterest, and how he thinks about product leadership in complex organizations.

---

## The Interview

### On Leadership

**Reforge:** You're now CPO at Eventbrite. How do you think about product leadership?

**Casey Winters:** Product leadership is about three things:

**1. Strategy**
- Where are we going?
- What problems are we solving?
- What's the sequencing?

**2. Execution**
- Are we shipping?
- Are we measuring?
- Are we learning?

**3. People**
- Do people have context?
- Are they empowered?
- Are they growing?

Strategy without execution is useless. Execution without strategy is busywork. Both without people are impossible.

### On Growth vs. Product

**Reforge:** Should growth report into product or be separate?

**Winters:** It depends on the company. At Pinterest, growth was its own function. At Eventbrite, product owns growth.

The important thing: whoever owns it needs to understand full-funnel. Acquisition, activation, retention, monetization. Can't optimize one and ignore the others.

**Reforge:** What's the danger of separating growth from product?

**Winters:** Growth teams optimize metrics without understanding user value. They ship growth hacks that hurt long-term retention.

Product teams build features without understanding growth. They ship things nobody uses.

The answer: either integrate them completely or have very tight collaboration.

### The Eventbrite Challenge

**Reforge:** Eventbrite is very different from Pinterest. What's unique about it?

**Winters:** Events are complex. Every event is different:
- Music festivals vs. yoga classes
- Free meetups vs. $500 conferences
- One-time events vs. recurring series

Plus, we have two-sided marketplace dynamics. Organizers create events. Attendees discover and buy tickets. Both matter.

And then COVID happened. We had to pivot to virtual events basically overnight.

**Reforge:** How did you handle that?

**Winters:** We moved fast. Created virtual event products, integrated with Zoom and other platforms, changed discovery to include virtual.

It was hard, but we learned about how quickly we can move when we have to.

### On Metrics

**Reforge:** What metrics matter most to you?

**Winters:** Depends on the business stage:

**Early stage:**
- Are people using it?
- Do they love it?
- Can we get more?

**Growth stage:**
- CAC
- LTV
- Retention curves

**Scale stage:**
- Unit economics
- Payback period
- Margin
- Efficiency

At Eventbrite, we look at:
- Number of events created
- Tickets sold
- GMV (Gross Merchandise Value)
- Organizer retention
- Attendee repeat rate

### On Roadmaps

**Reforge:** How do you think about product roadmaps?

**Winters:** I don't love traditional roadmaps. "We're shipping X in Q2, Y in Q3..."

Better approach:

**1. Themes**
- What problems are we solving this quarter?
- Keep it high-level.

**2. Bets**
- What are our big bets?
- What's the expected impact?
- What will we learn?

**3. Flexibility**
- Things change
- Good ideas emerge
- Bad ideas get killed

Having a rigid 6-month roadmap is not helpful. Having clear priorities and sequencing is.

### On Failure

**Reforge:** How do you handle product failures?

**Winters:** Failure is data. You shipped something, people didn't use it, you learned.

The key questions:
- Did we learn something?
- Was the failure cheap?
- Did we fail fast?

If yes, it's a good failure.

If we spent a year building something nobody wants, that's a bad failure. Not because it failed, but because it was expensive and slow.

### On Advice for PMs

**Reforge:** What advice would you give product managers?

**Winters:** Five things:

**1. Know your user**
- Spend time with them
- Read their feedback
- Watch them use your product
- Don't just look at dashboards

**2. Understand the business**
- How do we make money?
- What are our costs?
- What are our constraints?

**3. Think in systems**
- How does this feature connect to others?
- What loops are we building?
- What's the compounding effect?

**4. Ship fast, learn fast**
- Don't wait for perfect
- Get to users as fast as possible
- Iterate based on feedback

**5. Build growth thinking**
- How will people find this?
- How will they understand it?
- How will they keep using it?

### On Product-Market Fit

**Reforge:** How do you know if you have product-market fit?

**Winters:** There's no single metric, but signs include:

- **Retention curves flatten**
  If users keep coming back after months, you have something

- **Word of mouth**
  People tell others without you asking

- **Organic growth**
  You're growing without paid acquisition

- **Users would be disappointed if it disappeared**
  Ask them: "How would you feel if [product] went away?"
  If >40% say "very disappointed," you probably have PMF

- **Usage "pulls"**
  People asking for things, wanting more

### The Adjacent User Revisited

**Reforge:** Is the Adjacent User Theory still your core framework?

**Winters:** Absolutely. I use it constantly.

At Pinterest, it unlocked international growth, male users, Gen Z.

At Eventbrite, I'm thinking about adjacent organizers—people who could run events but face barriers.

The theory is: you've captured the easy users. The growth is in removing barriers for the adjacent users.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Growth Must Be Integrated
Growth can't be siloed from product. Full-funnel thinking is required.

### 2. Two-Sided Marketplaces Are Hard
Both sides matter. Both need value.

### 3. Roadmaps Should Be Thematic
Rigid timelines don't work. Flexible, prioritized themes do.

### 4. Failure Is Data
Learn cheaply and quickly.

### 5. PMs Must Understand Users and Business
Not just one or the other. Both.
