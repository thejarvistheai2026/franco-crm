# Paul Graham - Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule

**Date:** July 2009  
**Source:** Paul Graham's Essays

---

## About This Essay

A foundational essay on time management that introduced the concept of "maker's schedule" vs "manager's schedule" and explained why they conflict.

---

## The Essay

### Two Types of Schedule

**Paul Graham:** There are two types of schedule, which I'll call the manager's schedule and the maker's schedule.

The manager's schedule is for bosses. It's embodied in the traditional appointment book, with each day cut into one-hour intervals.

When you work on the manager's schedule, meetings are the default. You can change your schedule on the fly, and it's no problem.

### The Maker's Schedule

**Graham:** The maker's schedule is different. It's used by people who make things—programmers, writers, designers.

Makers generally prefer to use time in units of half a day at least. You can't write or program well in units of an hour. That's barely enough time to get started.

### The Conflict

**Graham:** When someone on the manager's schedule meets someone on the maker's schedule, the meeting is a disaster for the maker.

A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon by breaking it into two pieces, each too small to do anything hard in.

**Graham:** How much is an hour meeting really? For a maker, it's not one hour plus the meeting. It's the context switching cost. It's the fact that you can't do deep work.

### Solutions

**Graham:** How do we address this conflict?

For makers: Protect your time aggressively. Batch meetings. Keep mornings free.

For managers: Be aware that your schedule destroys other people's productivity. Don't schedule meetings with makers without thinking about the cost.

### Y Combinator Policy

**Graham:** At YC, we try to respect the maker's schedule. Office hours are at specific times. Partners don't just drop by. We protect the makers' time.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Two Schedules Exist
Managers and makers operate on fundamentally different time structures.

### 2. Context Switching Cost
Meetings don't cost just the meeting time—they cost the surrounding productivity.

### 3. Half-Day Minimum
Makers need uninterrupted blocks of at least half a day.

### 4. Awareness Matters
Managers should understand the cost they impose on makers.

### 5. Protective Measures
Makers must aggressively defend their time from meeting creep.

---

## Famous Quotes

- "There are two types of schedule: the manager's schedule and the maker's schedule."
- "A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon."
- "For a maker, it's not one hour plus the meeting. It's the context switching cost."

---

## Impact

This essay introduced vocabulary that Silicon Valley now takes for granted. "Maker's schedule" became a standard term for explaining why developers and writers need uninterrupted time.
