# Paul Graham - How to Start a Startup (Stanford CS183B)

**Date:** September 2014  
**Source:** Stanford CS183B: How to Start a Startup  
**Location:** Stanford University

---

## About This Lecture

Paul Graham's foundational lecture on how to start a startup, delivered as part of Stanford's CS183B course. This talk became one of the most-viewed startup lectures in history.

---

## The Lecture

### The Definition

**Paul Graham:** The way to start a startup is not to build a product. It's to build something people want.

*[Pause]*

That sounds obvious but it's not. Most founders start by building a product. They have an idea, they build it, then they try to find customers. That's backwards.

The right way is: Start with the customer. Find something they desperately need that doesn't exist. Then build it.

### Why Startups Are Hard

**Graham:** Startups are not for everyone. They're brutally hard. The reason they're hard is that you're trying to do something new. If it were easy, someone would have done it already.

The difficulty is the filter. It keeps out people who aren't sufficiently committed.

### The Idea

**Graham:** Don't worry about the idea being small. Airbnb seemed like a crazy idea for unemployed designers. Dropbox seemed like "just another file syncing service." Uber seemed like "just a black car service."

The best ideas often look stupid initially. If they looked obviously good, big companies would have already done them.

### The Team

**Graham:** For a startup, you need cofounders. Not just one cofounder. Ideally, two or three. Why? Because startups are so hard that you need people to share the burden with.

Also, startups pivot. The initial idea almost never works exactly as planned. You need people you can trust to go through that process with.

### Do Things That Don't Scale

**Graham:** One of the most common mistakes is trying to build something that scales from day one. Don't do that.

The initial version of your product should be manual, hacky, and held together with duct tape. Airbnb door-to-door in New York. Uber driving people around themselves. Stripe manually onboarding every user.

Do things that don't scale. Get users. Learn. Then automate.

### Growth

**Graham:** The only thing that matters for a startup is growth. Not revenue. Not press. Not awards. Growth.

If you're growing 10% a week, you're a startup. If you're not growing, you're just a small business.

### Why Now?

**Graham:** The best startups have an answer to "Why now?" Why didn't this work five years ago? Why will it work now?

Usually the answer is some technology just became cheap enough, or some behavior just changed, or some platform just emerged.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Start with the Customer
Most founders build first, find customers second. Reverse it.

### 2. Ideas Look Stupid at First
Good startup ideas look like bad ideas to most people initially.

### 3. Do Things That Don't Scale
Manual, unscalable work in the early days is not just okay—it's necessary.

### 4. Growth is the Only Metric
Everything else is secondary to growth rate.

### 5. Cofounders are Essential
You need people to share the burden and go through pivots with.

## Impact

This lecture became the foundation of Y Combinator's startup advice and influenced a generation of founders. The "Do Things That Don't Scale" concept became particularly influential.
