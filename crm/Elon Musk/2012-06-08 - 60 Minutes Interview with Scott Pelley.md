# Elon Musk - 60 Minutes Interview (2012)

**Date:** June 8, 2012  
**Source:** CBS 60 Minutes  
**Location:** SpaceX Hawthorne, California / Tesla Fremont  
**Interviewer:** Scott Pelley  
**Guest:** Elon Musk (CEO of SpaceX and Tesla)

---

## About This Interview

This 2012 60 Minutes interview captured Elon Musk during a pivotal moment. SpaceX had just completed its first successful mission to the ISS with the Dragon capsule (May 2012), becoming the first private company to send a spacecraft to the space station. Tesla was preparing to launch the Model S. Musk was simultaneously running two companies that many thought would fail.

The interview revealed Musk's resilience after early failures and his response to critics who doubted both SpaceX and Tesla.

---

## Topics Covered

### SpaceX Dragon ISS Mission

**Scott Pelley:** Three weeks ago, you did something that no private company has ever done. You sent your Dragon spacecraft to the International Space Station.

**Elon Musk:** It's an incredible milestone for humanity. The United States has been unable to send astronauts to the space station for almost two years now. And now we have a private company that can do what only governments have done before.

**Pelley:** You said you were watching the launch and you weren't sure if you were watching a dream or a nightmare.

**Musk:** Yeah, because every launch before that had failed in some way. The first three launches of Falcon 1 failed. I was watching that fourth launch and I was thinking, this is either going to be the best day of my life or the end of my company.

### Early Failures

**Pelley:** You were $100 million into this. Three failed launches. You had enough money for one more try. How did you handle that?

**Musk:** I remember thinking, this is it. If this fourth launch fails, I'm done. I'm out of money. I had enough for one more attempt. And it succeeded. That was a very emotional moment.

**Pelley:** But you had $100 million invested at that point. You could have lost everything.

**Musk:** Yeah. It was a tough time. I remember waking up with nightmares that we would fail. That I would have to tell everyone that we couldn't do it. That all those people who believed in me would be let down.

### Response to Critics

**Pelley:** There were a lot of people who thought you were going to fail. Neil Armstrong, the first man on the moon, testified to Congress that your approach was wrong.

**Musk:** (voice cracks) I was very sad to see that. I wish he would come visit us. Come see what we're doing. I think he would change his mind.

**Pelley:** Did you expect to be dismissed by heroes?

**Musk:** I have respect for Armstrong and Gene Cernan. They were heroes of mine. It was really tough. But we're trying to do something important here. We're trying to advance space technology and make humanity a multi-planet species.

**Pelley:** What do you say to them now?

**Musk:** Come see the hardware. See what we've built. I think it would change their minds.

### Running Two Companies

**Pelley:** You're running two companies simultaneously, SpaceX and Tesla. Both are incredibly demanding. Both were on the brink of failure. Why didn't you pick one?

**Musk:** They're both important. SpaceX is about making life multi-planetary, ensuring humanity's long-term survival. Tesla is about sustainable energy, transitioning the world away from fossil fuels. Both matter.

**Pelley:** But you're working 100-hour weeks. You were sleeping on the factory floor at Tesla during the Model S ramp-up.

**Musk:** Yeah, we were really struggling with the Model S production. The company was running out of money. I had to do whatever it took. If that meant sleeping on the factory floor, that's what I did.

### Vision for Mars

**Pelley:** You've said you want to die on Mars. But not on impact.

**Musk:** (laughs) Yeah, exactly. I want to see humanity become a spacefaring civilization. I want to see a self-sustaining city on Mars. That's the goal.

**Pelley:** Why Mars?

**Musk:** Mars is the closest planet that can support life. It's the backup drive for civilization. If something happens to Earth — an asteroid, a supervolcano, nuclear war — we need to be on another planet.

**Pelley:** When do you think you'll send people to Mars?

**Musk:** I think we could do it in 10 to 20 years. Maybe sooner if things go well.

### Tesla and Electric Vehicles

**Pelley:** You're building the Model S here at this factory. $57,000 base price. Who's going to buy it?

**Musk:** People who want the best car in the world. This isn't a compromise car. This is a car that's dramatically better than anything else.

**Pelley:** But it's electric. People worry about range anxiety.

**Musk:** The Model S goes 300 miles on a charge. That's enough for almost any daily use. And we're building a Supercharger network so you can drive across the country for free.

**Pelley:** Critics say you're making toys for the rich.

**Musk:** Every new technology starts out expensive. The first cell phones cost thousands of dollars. Now everyone has one. We have to start with premium cars to fund the technology, then move down-market.

### Personal Sacrifice

**Pelley:** You've invested everything in these companies. Tesla almost went bankrupt in 2008. You were divorcing. You were sleeping on friends' couches. Wasn't there a point where you thought this was too much?

**Musk:** 2008 was the worst year of my life. SpaceX had three failed launches. Tesla was running out of money. I was going through a divorce. I was borrowing money from friends to pay rent.

**Pelley:** How close did you come to giving up?

**Musk:** I didn't give up because... if something is important enough, you do it even if the odds are not in your favor. This is important. The future of humanity depends on sustainable energy and making life multi-planetary.

**Pelley:** You're betting everything on ideas that most people think are crazy.

**Musk:** I don't think they're crazy. I think they're inevitable.

## Key Insights from 60 Minutes Interview

### 1. Resilience Through Failure
Musk was one failed launch away from losing everything with SpaceX. The fourth launch succeeding saved both SpaceX and his vision.

### 2. Responding to Heroes
Being criticized by Neil Armstrong genuinely hurt Musk. His response: invite them to see what SpaceX was actually building.

### 3. Importance Over Odds
"If something is important enough, you do it even if the odds are not in your favor." This became a Musk mantra.

### 4. Personal Sacrifice
Musk slept on factory floors, borrowed money for rent, and went through divorce while trying to keep Tesla alive. The personal cost was enormous.

### 5. The Multi-Planet Vision
Musk's Mars vision isn't about exploration—it's about "the backup drive for civilization." Existential risk mitigation.

### 6. S-Curve Technology Economics
Electric vehicles must start expensive (Model S) to fund technology development that enables mass-market cars (Model 3).

### 7. Working Through Pain
Even when recounting the hardest moments, Musk framed them as necessary costs for important goals. No self-pity.

---

## Context and Impact

When this interview aired in June 2012:
- SpaceX had just completed its first ISS mission (Dragon COTS 2)
- Tesla was about to deliver the first Model S vehicles
- Both companies had survived near-death experiences
- Many still doubted both would succeed long-term

Musk's raw emotion when discussing Armstrong's criticism surprised viewers. This wasn't the confident tech billionaire—it was someone genuinely hurt by being dismissed by his childhood heroes.

The interview humanized Musk while reinforcing his determination. Scott Pelley's questioning highlighted just how close both companies had come to failure, making their survival more impressive.
