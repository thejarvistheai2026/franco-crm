# Elon Musk - Making Humans an Interplanetary Species (IAC 2016)

**Date:** September 27, 2016  
**Source:** International Astronautical Congress, Guadalajara, Mexico  
**Location:** Guadalajara, Mexico  
**Speaker:** Elon Musk (CEO of SpaceX)  
**Presentation:** Making Humans an Interplanetary Species

---

## About This Presentation

This was the first major reveal of SpaceX's Mars colonization architecture. Standing before the international space community, Musk unveiled the Interplanetary Transport System (ITS), a massive rocket and spacecraft designed to send 100 people to Mars at a time. The presentation combined technical details with a vision for making humanity multi-planetary.

This presentation fundamentally changed the conversation about Mars—shifting from "if" to "when" and "how much."

---

## The Why: Making Life Multi-Planetary

### Opening Statement

**Elon Musk:** The future is going to be incredibly exciting. I think the most important thing I want to convey is that I think it's important for us to become a spacefaring civilization, and to be out there among the stars. We have a choice of which future we want.

The probable life of Earth is about 4.5 billion years. Over that time it's gradually going to get hotter and hotter, and eventually Earth will be swallowed by the sun. There will be some extinction event—with a probability of one—that's where we're headed.

The alternative is to become a spacefaring civilization and be out there among the stars. That's what we want.

### The Catastrophe Argument

**Musk:** History is going to bifurcate along two directions. One path is we stay on Earth forever, and then there will be some eventual extinction event. I don't have an immediate doomsday prophecy, but eventually history suggests there will be some extinction event.

The alternative is to become a spacefaring civilization and a multi-planet species—which I hope you agree is the right way to go.

### Why Mars?

**Musk:** So why Mars? There are really two fundamental paths: either we're going to become a multi-planet species, a spacefaring civilization, or we're going to be stuck on one planet until some eventual extinction event.

Mars is the closest planet that can support life. We know of no other planet that could support life in this solar system. There may be some other candidates around other stars, but those are very far away.

## The How: Interplanetary Transport System (ITS)

### Vehicle Architecture

**Musk:** This is the Interplanetary Transport System... This is the full-size vehicle. The height is 122 meters with the spaceship on top. About 400 feet. It's quite tall.

**Booster:** The booster is designed to have a thrust of 28 million pounds at liftoff. That's about 4 times the thrust of a Saturn V. It has 42 Raptor engines on the base. The liftoff thrust is 128 meganewtons.

**Spaceship:** On top of the booster is the spaceship. It's got a maximum payload of 450 tons to Mars with refilling. The aspirational goal is 100 passengers per flight, although in the presentation graphics I think I showed 40.

### Key Innovations

**Refilling in Orbit:** One of the key elements is refilling the spaceship in orbit. We get the spaceship to orbit, then we send a tanker up to fill it up. This is critical for achieving full reusability.

**Raptor Engine:** The Raptor engine is a full-flow staged combustion cycle engine, which is a really exotic type of engine. It's never been used before in a flight vehicle.

**Carbon Fiber Construction:** The tanks will be made of advanced carbon fiber. This is actually quite new—carbon fiber is great in tension, but not so great in compression with cryogenic fluids, so that's a key technology.

### Mars Entry, Descent, and Landing

**Musk:** When the spaceship arrives at Mars, it's entering at high velocity. It uses aerobraking and the heat shield to slow down. Then it uses a supersonic retro-propulsion landing with the engines.

The spaceship will land on Mars using propulsive landing—just like the Falcon 9 booster lands on Earth. But this time, it's landing with people on board.

## The Cost: Making It Affordable

### The Fundamental Problem

**Musk:** Historically, the cost of going to Mars has been prohibitive. It's been estimated at around $10 billion per person under the current approach.

The fundamental constraint is that you can't just go to Mars once. You need reusability. The cost of a Boeing 747 is about $300 million. If you had to throw away a 747 after every flight, air travel would cost $1 million per person for cross-country travel.

But because you can reuse the aircraft, the cost becomes much more reasonable.

### Reusability is Key

**Musk:** With full reusability—both the booster and the ship fully reusable—the cost drops dramatically. The goal is to get the cost down to around $100,000 per ton to Mars.

With a 100-person ship, that means the per-person cost could eventually be around $100,000 to $200,000.

### Funding the Development

**Musk:** How do we pay for this? We expect to generate substantial revenue from launch services. We also intend to do satellite launches for internet connectivity—this would be a huge revenue source.

Additionally, I think we could raise a couple of billion dollars from public-private partnerships and government support.

### The Timeline

**Musk:** When do we think this is going to happen?

- 2018: First cargo mission to Mars with a Dragon
- 2022: First cargo mission with the full system
- 2024: First crewed mission

We want to establish a Mars colony. Not just a few people for a few years, but a self-sustaining city of a million people.

---

## Life on Mars

### The Vision

**Musk:** What would it be like? Initially, there would be lots of cargo ships arriving, and the initial crew would be going out there establishing the propellant plant, building out the base, and preparing for future flights.

Once you have the propellant plant built, you can make methane and oxygen from Martian resources. That enables return trips.

### Martian Architecture

**Musk:** The base will start with a few crew members. Then we send more. Eventually, you'll have thousands of people. Then tens of thousands. Then hundreds of thousands.

The key threshold is when you have enough people and industry on Mars that if the resupply ships from Earth stopped coming for any reason, the city could survive.

## Q&A Session Highlights

### On Timeline Realism

**Audience:** These timelines seem very aggressive. Is this realistic?

**Musk:** I think it's realistic, but we need to be careful not to get complacent. SpaceX has a strong track record of delivering on schedules, but we also tend to be optimistic.

**Audience:** What if you're wrong about something fundamental?

**Musk:** That's always a possibility. But we've been working on the key technologies—the Raptor engine, the composite tanks, the propulsive landing. These are all things we've been developing and testing.

**Audience:** What about radiation?

**Musk:** Radiation is a concern, but it seems manageable. The radiation dose for a trip to Mars is about the same as getting a CT scan every week for six months. It's not great, but it's not deadly.

We can add a radiation shelter in the cargo bay where people can go during solar storms. And once you're on Mars, the atmosphere provides shielding.

**Audience:** What would you tell skeptics?

**Musk:** I'd say come with us. Watch what we're doing. We have a strong team at SpaceX. We've been doing things that people said were impossible for 14 years now.

When we started, people said we couldn't possibly build our own rockets. When we built Falcon 1, they said we couldn't possibly build something bigger. When we built Falcon 9, they said reusability was impossible. Now we've landed 16 boosters.

## Key Insights from Mars Presentation

### 1. Mars as Backup Drive
Musk's framing: Mars isn't about exploration, it's about "the backup drive for civilization." Existential risk mitigation, not adventure.

### 2. Reusability is Everything
The 747 analogy: Reusability is what makes Mars economically possible. $10 billion/person → $200,000/person.

### 3. Aggressive Timelines
2018 cargo (Dragon), 2022 full system cargo, 2024 crewed. These timelines proved too optimistic, but the vision remained.

### 4. Self-Sustaining Threshold
The critical metric: Can Mars survive without Earth resupply? That's when the colony becomes truly "backed up."

### 5. Enormous Scale
ITS was massive: 122 meters tall, 100 passengers, 28 million pounds thrust. This was bigger than Saturn V.

### 6. Orbital Refilling
Critical architecture choice: Refill in Earth orbit before departing for Mars. Enables much larger payloads.

### 7. Propellant from Mars
Make your own return fuel on Mars using Martian resources. ESSP: Make fuel where you need it.

## Context and Impact

When this presentation was given (September 2016):
- SpaceX had just achieved its first successful booster landing (December 2015)
- The Falcon Heavy had not yet flown (first flight January 2018)
- Mars colonization was considered science fiction by most
- NASA's Mars plans were decades away and hundreds of billions of dollars

The impact was immediate:
- Established SpaceX as the serious player in Mars colonization
- Forced NASA and other space agencies to respond
- Created public imagination around Mars cities
- The architecture evolved (ITS became BFR, then Starship), but the vision remained

The 100-passenger Mars ship shown in 2016 became the Starship we see today—though many technical details changed, the fundamental approach (full reusability, orbital refilling, propulsive landing) remained.
