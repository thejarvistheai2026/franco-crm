## Detailed Notes

### Innovation in Science and Technology Has Slowed

Eric and Peter agree they're among the few people who reach the same conclusions about the relative stagnation in science and technology; the ways in which this is deranging our culture, our politics, and our society; and how we need to find some bold ways out through a portal to a different world.

It's striking how out of sync these insights feel with so much of our society, even in 2019. Eric adds that too much time is being spent on the debate of whether there has in fact been stagnation — THERE HAS.

### The Innovation Stagnation Lies Within the World of Atoms

Peter dates this era of stagnation back to the 1970s. This stagnation is most present within the "world of atoms." Perhaps there's an exception in the world of bits, software, and internet (essentially all of Silicon Valley), but questions remain: "The iPhone is now looking the same as it did 7-8 years ago."

Peter is optimistic and thinks we could do better: "We might not be able to advance on all fronts in every direction, but I think there's more space on the frontier than just in the world of bits."

### Why Is It Hard to Convince People Innovation Has Slowed?

Eric's point: "Go into a room and subtract off all the screens. How do you then know you're not in 1973?"

In late modernity there's simply too much knowledge for any individual human to understand all of it. This results in hyper-specialization and hyper-expertise and, as a side effect, people who only police themselves. As it's gotten harder to evaluate what's going on, it's gotten easier for people to lie and exaggerate about the progress of their respective industries.

### The Problem With Universities

The great scientists of our time were polymaths (like Richard Feynman) — they knew a lot about many different subjects. This has since changed — disciplines have gotten more rigid and people have gotten more specialized.

It's actually become taboo to have other interests besides your respective field in the university/research system. Eric notes: "There's no level you can rise to in the field that allows you to question the assumptions of that field."

### Physics and Biology Progress

Physics gave us a whole lot: Atomic devices, nuclear power, the semiconductor, the world wide web, molecular biology, and the communication revolution. "You could make the argument that physics has been really underrated in powering the world economy" — Eric Weinstein.

In physics, there was a clear translation of hard science to real-world applications. But this translation has been absent in areas like genetics — we've had very little to show in the realm of gene therapy for all our newfound biology knowledge.

Peter's take: "I continue to think we could be making a lot more progress in biology." Perhaps biology is more complicated than physics, OR — perhaps biology attracts people who are less intelligent in the realm of mathematics/science compared to physics.

### Solution to Student Debt Crisis

Peter's solution:
1. Make college debt dischargeable in bankruptcy (currently it's nondischargeable)
2. If people went bankrupt, part of the debt would have to be paid for by the university
