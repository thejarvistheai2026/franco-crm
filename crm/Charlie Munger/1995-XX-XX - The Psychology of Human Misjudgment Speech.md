# Charlie Munger - The Psychology of Human Misjudgment (Speech)

**Date:** 1995  
**Source:** Harvard University Speech  
**Location:** Cambridge, Massachusetts

---

## About This Speech

Munger's most famous talk, delivered to Harvard students. He outlines 25 cognitive biases that cause human misjudgment—what he calls his "24 Standard Causes of Human Misjudgment." This speech became the foundation for his thinking on decision-making and psychology.

---

## The Speech

### Introduction

**Charlie Munger:** I'm going to play a minor trick on you today—because the subject of my talk is the art of stock picking as a subdivision of the art of worldly wisdom. That enables me to start talking about worldly wisdom—a much broader topic that interests me because I think it would be enormously useful to most of you.

### The 25 Cognitive Biases

**1. Reward and Punishment Superresponse Tendency**
"Never think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives."

FedEx couldn't get their night shift to work fast enough. They tried everything—supervision, training, threats. Nothing worked. Then they changed the pay structure: pay by the shift to pay by the plane. Workers started finishing early and going home. Incentives matter more than almost anything.

**2. Liking/Loving Tendency**
We ignore faults of people we like. We favor people, products, and actions merely because we like them. This distorts judgment.

**3. Disliking/Hating Tendency**
Opposite of above. We distort facts to hate people we dislike. Politics is full of this.

**4. Doubt-Avoidance Tendency**
The brain of man is programmed with a natural tendency to quickly remove doubt by reaching some decision. We hate uncertainty.

**5. Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency**
We resist changing our minds. Once we've made a decision, we stick with it—even when evidence changes. "An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure."

**6. Curiosity Tendency**
The only counterweight to other biases. Curiosity can save us from our own stupidity.

**7. Kantian Fairness Tendency**
We want things to be fair, even when fairness isn't rational. This can lead to poor decisions.

**8. Envy/Jealousy Tendency**
"It is not greed that drives the world, but envy." Buffett has said this. Envy is a powerful destructive force.

**9. Reciprocation Tendency**
We feel obligated to return favors—even when we shouldn't. Salespeople exploit this constantly.

**10. Influence-from-Mere-Association Tendency**
We judge things by association, not merit. Advertising exploits this. "Coke is associated with happiness."

**11. Simple, Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial**
Reality is too painful to accept, so we distort it. Addicts do this. So do people in bad relationships.

**12. Excessive Self-Regard Tendency**
We all think we're above average. 90% of drivers think they're above average. Mathematically impossible.

**13. Overoptimism Tendency**
"What a man wishes, that also will he believe." We believe what we want to believe.

**14. Deprival-Superreaction Tendency**
Losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. This makes us irrational about losses.

**15. Social-Proof Tendency**
We do what others do. This helped our ancestors survive. Now it makes us follow crowds off cliffs.

**16. Contrast-Misreaction Tendency**
We judge things by contrast, not absolute value. $1000 seems small after $10,000. Large after $100.

**17. Stress-Influence Tendency**
Light stress can improve performance. Heavy stress causes dysfunction. Know the difference.

**18. Availability-Misweighing Tendency**
We overweight vivid, recent, or easily recalled information. We underweight important but boring information.

**19. Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency**
Skills decline if not practiced. Knowledge fades if not used.

**20. Drug-Misinfluence Tendency**
Drugs and alcohol destroy judgment. Obvious but worth stating.

**21. Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency**
Age declines cognitive function. Hard to fight, but awareness helps.

**22. Authority-Misinfluence Tendency**
We trust authority figures—even when they're wrong. Milgram experiments proved this.

**23. Twaddle Tendency**
Humans love nonsense, fluff, and complexity. Simple truths are boring.

**24. Reason-Respecting Tendency**
We respect reasons—even bad ones. "Because" is a magic word.

**25. Lollapalooza Tendency**
When multiple biases combine, you get extreme outcomes—good or bad. This is how cults form. This is how great businesses are built.

### The Antidotes

**Munger:** So what do we do about these biases?

**1. Use Checklists**
Pilots use checklists. Surgeons use checklists. You should too.

**2. Invert**
Instead of asking "how do I succeed?" ask "how do I avoid failure?" Then don't do those things.

**3. Learn the Big Ideas**
You don't need to know everything. Learn the big ideas from multiple disciplines.

**4. Stay Rational**
It's hard. But awareness is the first step.

## Key Insights

### 1. Psychology Is Underrated
Most education ignores psychology. But understanding human behavior is essential for good decisions.

### 2. Biases Are Universal
These aren't just for "other people." Everyone has them. Including you.

### 3. Incentives Rule
"Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."

### 4. Multiple Models
You need mental models from multiple disciplines—psychology, economics, physics, biology.

### 5. Inversion Works
Figuring out what not to do is often easier than figuring out what to do.

## Impact

This speech became the foundation for:
- Behavioral economics
- Decision science
- Munger's own investment philosophy
- The "mental models" movement

It was delivered in 1995, before Kahneman won the Nobel Prize, before "Thinking Fast and Slow." Munger was ahead of his time.
