# Jeff Bezos - 60 Minutes Interview (1999)

**Date:** October 26, 1999  
**Source:** CBS 60 Minutes  
**Interviewer:** Bob Simon  
**Location:** Amazon Headquarters, Seattle

---

## About This Interview

At the height of the dot-com bubble, Amazon was worth more than every bookstore chain combined. Bezos discussed his vision and the skepticism he faced.

---

## The Interview

### On the Internet Boom

**Bob Simon:** Amazon has been called the most successful company in the history of the World Wide Web. Your stock is up 3,000% in two years. Does this make sense?

**Jeff Bezos:** The stock market can be very fickle. We don't spend much time thinking about the stock price.

### On Barnes & Noble

**Simon:** Your biggest competitor just launched their website. Are you worried?

**Bezos:** We wake up every morning and try to serve our customers better. If we do that well, competition takes care of itself.

**Simon:** But they're huge. You're tiny.

**Bezos:** It's not about size. It's about agility and focus. We were built for the Internet. They're trying to adapt to it.

### The Dot-Com Bubble

**Simon:** Critics say this is a bubble. That Amazon is overvalued.

**Bezos:** Maybe they're right. I don't know. What I know is that we have real customers, real revenues, and we're growing rapidly. We're focused on the business, not the stock.

### On Being Worth $10 Billion

**Simon:** You're one of the richest people in the world. How does that feel?

**Bezos:** It's mostly on paper. And honestly, it's distracting. I'd rather talk about what we're building.

### Work-Life Balance

**Simon:** You're known for working long hours. Do you have any hobbies?

**Bezos:** I like to read. I like to be with my family. I love what I do, so work doesn't feel like work.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Ignore the Stock, Focus on Customers
Even as Amazon's stock soared, Bezos maintained focus on the business fundamentals.

### 2. Agility Over Size
Positioned Amazon's smaller size as an advantage in the fast-moving Internet space.

### 3. Long-Term Conviction
Maintained that the business was real regardless of stock price volatility.
