## PART 1: TOP 30 BLOG POSTS

### 1. Mental Models: The Best Way to Make Intelligent Decisions
**Published:** Ongoing (Cornerstone Article)  
**Source:** fs.blog/mental-models/  
**One-liner:** The comprehensive guide to ~100 mental models organized by discipline — your toolkit for better thinking.  
**Key Insights:**
- Mental models = simplified explanations of how things work
- Single-discipline thinking = man with only a hammer seeing every problem as nail
- Latticework of mental models = combining multiple disciplines for superior thinking
- Key categories: Physics, Chemistry, Biology, Systems, Numeracy, Microeconomics, Military/War, Human Nature
- Best thinkers use models from multiple domains

### 2. The Map is Not the Territory
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/map-and-territory/  
**One-liner:** Our representations of reality are not reality itself — critical distinction for clear thinking.  
**Key Insights:**
- Maps = abstractions/simplifications; Territory = actual complexity
- Dangerous to confuse mental models with actual reality
- Experts provide maps; choose cartographers wisely
- Updating maps requires reconciling truth with desire
- Simplification is necessary but creates blind spots

### 3. Circle of Competence
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/circle-of-competence/  
**One-liner:** Understanding edge and limitations — play games where you have an advantage.  
**Key Insights:**
- Circle size matters less than knowing the boundaries
- Within circle = advantage; Outside circle = others have advantage
- Warren Buffett: Willing to miss anything outside his circle
- Charlie Munger: "God bless our stupid competitors. They make us rich."
- Better to be 160 IQ thinking it's 150 than 160 thinking it's 200

### 4. First Principles Thinking
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/first-principles/  
**One-liner:** Breaking down complex problems to irreducible truths — the reasoning method used by Elon Musk and Aristotle.  
**Key Insights:**
- First principles = fundamental truths that can't be broken down further
- Socratic questioning + Five Whys to establish first principles
- Going 1-2 levels deeper than others reveals new solutions
- Example: Elon Musk breaking rockets down to raw material costs
- Rebels and disruptors think this way; conventional thinking stops at surface

### 5. Second-Order Thinking
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/second-order-thinking/  
**One-liner:** Thinking beyond first consequences to ask "And then what?" — Howard Marks' framework for superior performance.  
**Key Insights:**
- First-order = immediate, obvious; Second-order = interactions and time
- Howard Marks: First-level thinking is superficial; everyone can do it
- Ask: What are consequences in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?
- Many extraordinary outcomes are first-order negative, second-order positive
- Requires thinking through stakeholder responses (employees, competitors, regulators)

### 6. Inversion
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/inversion/  
**One-liner:** "Invert, always invert" — instead of seeking brilliance, avoid stupidity (Charlie Munger's favorite mental model).  
**Key Insights:**
- Ask: "What would guarantee failure?" rather than "How do I succeed?"
- Carl Jacobi: "man muss immer umkehren" (invert, always invert)
- Subtracting bad decisions often easier than adding good ones
- Avoid iatrogenics (harm from intervention)
- "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there" — Munger

### 7. Chesterton's Fence
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/chestertons-fence/  
**One-liner:** "Do not remove a fence until you know why it was put up in the first place" — understand before changing.  
**Key Insights:**
- G.K. Chesterton parable: fence exists for a reason
- Reform should be preceded by understanding
- Respect past wisdom before changing future
- Example: hierarchy-free companies ignoring why hierarchies exist
- Applies to habits, laws, social norms

### 8. How to Read a Book (Summary of Mortimer Adler)
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/how-to-read-a-book/  
**One-liner:** The four levels of reading — most people stop at level 2 and miss most value.  
**Key Insights:**
- Level 1 (Elementary): Reading to entertain
- Level 2 (Inspectional): Systematic skimming to inform
- Level 3 (Analytical): Reading to understand (the real work)
- Level 4 (Syntopical): Reading to master (comparing multiple books)
- Four questions to ask every book: What is it about? What is being said in detail? Is it true? What of it?

### 9. Probabilistic Thinking
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/probabilistic-thinking/  
**One-liner:** Navigating uncertainty by thinking in probabilities rather than certainties.  
**Key Insights:**
- Identify what matters, calculate odds, check assumptions, decide
- Probabilities update with new information
- Easier to believe something false is accurate than deal with being wrong
- Say: "I don't know for sure, but there's a 63% chance of X"
- Embracing uncertainty = better decisions, avoiding overconfidence

### 10. Thought Experiment
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/thought-experiment/  
**One-liner:** The sandbox of the mind — explore implications without real-world constraints.  
**Key Insights:**
- Stripping away noise to focus on essence
- Simplified models reveal core principles
- "What if?" questions start innovations
- Test boundaries of understanding
- Historical examples: Galileo's dropping objects, Schrodinger's cat

### 11. Hanlon's Razor
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/hanlons-razor/  
**One-liner:** "Never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by stupidity" — powerful safeguard for relationships.  
**Key Insights:**
- Malice is rare; stupidity is common
- Assume good faith; resist sinister motives without evidence
- Shifts perspective from defensive to empathetic
- Reduces drama and stress
- Find practical solutions instead of escalating blame

### 12. Occam's Razor
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/occams-razor/  
**One-liner:** "The simplest explanation is most likely the correct one" — but know when complexity is required.  
**Key Insights:**
- Prefer simplest theory until proven otherwise
- Doesn't mean truth is always simple
- Too simple = fails to capture reality
- Too complex = collapses under own weight
- Balance: understand when it works for and against you

### 13. The Work Required to Have an Opinion
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/the-work-required-to-have-an-opinion/  
**One-liner:** Most opinions are weakly held; strong opinions require deep work.  
**Key Insights:**
- Charlie Munger: "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything that I don't know the other side's argument better than they do"
- First requirement: understand opposing viewpoint
- Second requirement: test your own assumptions
- Third requirement: acknowledge uncertainty
- Weak opinions = cheap; Strong opinions = require work

### 14. Focus is About Saying No
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/focus/  
**One-liner:** Steve Jobs: "I'm actually as proud of the things we haven't done as the things I have done" — productivity through elimination.  
**Key Insights:**
- Anyone can say no to bad ideas
- Only focused people say no to good ideas
- Innovation requires saying "no" to 1,000 things
- Warren Buffett: Focus on 5 highest-priority goals; avoid everything else
- Strategy is choosing what NOT to do

### 15. The Blank Sheet Method
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/blank-sheet-method/  
**One-liner:** Before reading, summarize what you already know — primes your brain for insight.  
**Key Insights:**
- Forces you to inventory existing knowledge
- Identifies gaps and assumptions
- Makes reading active rather than passive
- Creates mental hooks for new information
- Improves retention and comprehension

### 16. The Feynman Technique
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/feynman-technique/  
**One-liner:** Learning by teaching — if you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.  
**Key Insights:**
- Step 1: Choose concept and study it
- Step 2: Teach it to a child (simple language, no jargon)
- Step 3: Identify gaps in understanding
- Step 4: Review and simplify
- Richard Feynman: "What I cannot create, I do not understand"

### 17. Multiplying by Zero
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/multiplying-by-zero/  
**One-liner:** Irreversible mistakes that undo everything — identify and avoid these at all costs.  
**Key Insights:**
- Some errors are total losses (multiplying by zero)
- Examples: permanent injury, death, catastrophic reputation loss
- Survivable errors compound; total losses end game
- Margin of safety protects against multiplying by zero
- Charlie Munger: "Avoid the big stupidity"

### 18. Decision Journal
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/decision-journal/  
**One-liner:** Document decisions to calibrate judgment and learn from outcomes.  
**Key Insights:**
- Record: decision, context, expected outcome, reasoning
- Review: actual outcome vs. expected
- Calibrates judgment over time
- Identifies patterns in errors
- Improves decision quality through feedback

### 19. The Red Queen Effect
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/red-queen-effect/  
**One-liner:** "It takes all the running you can do to keep in the same place" — from biology to business.  
**Key Insights:**
- Origin: Lewis Carroll's Through the Looking-Glass
- In nature: predator-prey arms race
- In business: need continuous innovation to maintain position
- Standing still = going backward
- Applies to personal development, technology, competition

### 20. Margin of Safety
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/margin-of-safety/  
**One-liner:** Ben Graham's concept: room for error — things don't always go as planned.  
**Key Insights:**
- Critical in investing: buy at significant discount to intrinsic value
- Applies to engineering, planning, decision-making
- Provides buffer against negative outcomes
- Probability of being wrong is higher than you think
- More important as consequences increase

### 21. The Lindy Effect
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/lindy-effect/  
**One-liner:** The longer something has survived, the longer it's likely to survive — time-tested concepts vs. new trends.  
**Key Insights:**
- Perishable: predictable lifespan (human, car battery)
- Non-perishable: lifespan increases with age (book, technology, idea)
- Time-tested wisdom > new fad
- Applies to books, ideas, technology, companies
- Classical texts have Lindy; self-help books often don't

### 22. Bike Shedding
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/bike-shedding/  
**One-liner:** Trivial matters getting disproportionate attention — Parkinson's Law of triviality.  
**Key Insights:**
- Complex topics get less discussion because they're... complex
- Simple topics (like bike shed color) get endless debate
- People focus on what they understand
- Danger: critical issues ignored while trivialities debated
- Solution: assign appropriate attention by consequence

### 23. The Curse of Knowledge
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/curse-of-knowledge/  
**One-liner:** Knowledge blinds us to what it's like to NOT know — communication barrier.  
**Key Insights:**
- Expert can't remember what it's like to be a beginner
- Leads to poor teaching and communication
- Assumes shared context that doesn't exist
- Explains technical jargon and insider language
- Antidote: empathy for the learner's perspective

### 24. Survivorship Bias
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/survivorship-bias/  
**One-liner:** Seeing only successes and ignoring failures — the "visible winners" problem.  
**Key Insights:**
- Example: WWII planes — reinforce where non-shot-down planes were hit
- We study successful startups, not failed ones
- Advice from survivors may be wrong (luck vs. skill)
- "No one who has lost is writing about losing"
- Counter: study failures to understand success

### 25. Confirmation Bias
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/confirmation-bias/  
**One-liner:** Seeking evidence that confirms existing beliefs — avoiding disconfirming information.  
**Key Insights:**
- Natural tendency to seek confirming evidence
- Information contradicting beliefs causes discomfort
- Social media algorithms amplify this
- Strong opinions held without examining counter-arguments = weak
- Solution: actively seek disconfirming evidence

### 26. The Availability Heuristic
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/availability-heuristic/  
**One-liner:** Judging probability by how easily examples come to mind — leads to systematic errors.  
**Key Insights:**
- Recent events > historical data
- Vivid events > common events
- Media coverage distorts perception
- Examples: fear of flying vs. driving, shark attacks
- Counter: look at base rates, not headlines

### 27. Bayes' Theorem
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/bayes/  
**One-liner:** Updating beliefs with new evidence — the mathematical framework for learning.  
**Key Insights:**
- P(A|B) = P(B|A) * P(A) / P(B)
- Prior probability + new evidence = updated probability
- Key insight: initial beliefs matter, but evidence updates them
- Practical application: don't start from scratch with each new fact
- Avoids binary thinking (true/false) in favor of probability

### 28. Compounding
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/compounding/  
**One-liner:** Small gains adding up over time — the most powerful force in the universe (Einstein).  
**Key Insights:**
- Compound interest: earnings generate earnings
- Applies to knowledge, relationships, reputation, skills
- Early start matters enormously
- Small improvements daily = massive results over years
- Patience required; results invisible initially

### 29. Velocity vs Speed
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/velocity/  
**One-liner:** Direction matters — fast in wrong direction is worse than slow in right direction.  
**Key Insights:**
- Speed = rate of motion (scalar)
- Velocity = speed + direction (vector)
- Busyness ≠ Progress
- Efficiency doing wrong tasks = waste
- Before optimizing speed, confirm direction

### 30. Reciprocity
**Published:** Ongoing  
**Source:** fs.blog/reciprocity/  
**One-liner:** Treat others as they treat you — the golden rule of social interaction.  
**Key Insights:**
- Give what you want to receive
- Cynicism breeds cynicism; generosity breeds generosity
- "Best way to achieve success is to deserve success"
- Small changes in action change entire world
- Go positive and go first (without expectation)
