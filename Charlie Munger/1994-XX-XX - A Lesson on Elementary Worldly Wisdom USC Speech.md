# Charlie Munger - A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom (USC Speech)

**Date:** 1994  
**Source:** USC Business School Speech  
**Location:** Los Angeles, California

---

## About This Speech

Munger's famous speech on worldly wisdom—the idea that you need mental models from multiple disciplines to make good decisions. This talk introduced the "latticework of mental models" concept.

---

## The Speech

### Worldly Wisdom

**Charlie Munger:** What is elementary, worldly wisdom? Well, the first rule is that you can't really know anything if you just remember isolated facts and try and bang 'em back. If the facts don't hang together on a latticework of theory, you don't have them in a usable form.

You've got to have models in your head. And you've got to array your experience—both vicarious and direct—on this latticework of models.

### The Latticework

**Munger:** You've got to have multiple models—because if you just have one or two that you're using, the nature of human psychology is such that you'll torture reality so that it fits your models. Or at least you'll think it does.

### The Models You Need

**Munger:** What are the models? Well, the first rule is that you've got to have multiple models—because if you just have one or two that you're using, the nature of human psychology is such that you'll torture reality so that it fits your models.

**Mathematics:**
You have to understand compound interest. You have to understand basic probability. You have to understand elementary statistics.

**Accounting:**
Accounting is the language of practical business life. You have to understand it. But it's not sufficient—accounting is merely a starting place.

**Psychology:**
The psychology of misjudgment is terribly important. I've given a whole talk on that.

**Biology/Physiology:**
You need some understanding of how the world works biologically. Evolution, natural selection.

**Engineering:**
Quality control, backup systems, breakpoints—these are engineering concepts that apply to business.

**Economics:**
Supply and demand, incentives, markets. But don't get too academic—most economics is wrong.

**Complex Systems:**
Understanding that systems have emergent properties. The whole is different from the sum of parts.

### The Importance of Inversion

**Munger:** Many hard problems are best solved when they are addressed backward. The way complex adaptive systems work and the way mental constructs work, problems frequently get easier—and I mean much easier—if you turn them around in reverse.

Instead of asking "how do I get X?" ask "how do I avoid not-X?" Then don't do those things.

### The Example of Coca-Cola

**Munger:** Let me give you an example of how to think about a business using multiple models.

How would you create Coca-Cola from scratch?

**Biology:** Create a drink that triggers dopamine—sweet, caffeinated, carbonated.

**Psychology:** Associate it with happiness. Red and white colors. Santa Claus. Make it ubiquitous so it's always available when you want happiness.

**Economics:** Create massive scale economies. Bottling plants everywhere. Cheaper than competitors.

**Complex Systems:** Create a virtuous cycle. More availability → more sales → more advertising → more brand recognition → more availability.

That's how you build a trillion-dollar company.

### The Importance of Reading

**Munger:** In my whole life, I have known no wise people (over a broad subject matter area) who didn't read all the time—none, zero. You'd be amazed at how much Warren reads—and at how much I read. My children laugh at me. They think I'm a book with a couple of legs sticking out.

### The Circle of Competence

**Munger:** You have to figure out what your own aptitudes are. If you play games where other people have the aptitudes and you don't, you're going to lose. And that's as close to certain as any prediction that you can make.

You have to figure out where you've got an edge. And you've got to play within your own circle of competence.

### Patience

**Munger:** The big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in the waiting.

If you just take the top 10 business investments we've ever made, they account for most of our success. We don't make that many good decisions. We just make a few, and then we sit on our asses.

### The Importance of Ethics

**Munger:** The highest form that civilization can reach is a seamless web of deserved trust—not much procedure, just totally reliable people correctly trusting one another.

In your own life what you want is a seamless web of deserved trust. And if your proposed marriage contract has 47 pages, my suggestion is you not enter.

## Key Insights

### 1. Mental Models
You need a latticework of mental models from multiple disciplines. One or two models is not enough.

### 2. Inversion
Solve problems backward. Figure out what to avoid.

### 3. Reading
Wise people read constantly. There's no substitute.

### 4. Circle of Competence
Know what you know and what you don't. Stay in your circle.

### 5. Patience
The big money is in waiting, not trading.

### 6. Ethics
Trust is the highest form of civilization.

## Impact

This speech introduced the "latticework of mental models" concept that influenced:
- The mental models movement
- Decision science
- Interdisciplinary thinking
- Investment philosophy

It was delivered in 1994 and remains one of the most-cited talks on worldly wisdom.
