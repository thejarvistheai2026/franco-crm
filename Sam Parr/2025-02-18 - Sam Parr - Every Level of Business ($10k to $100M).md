## Level 1: Making $10,000

### The Mindset
- The first $10,000 is actually quite easy
- Most people overthink it and make it harder than it needs to be
- Fear is the biggest obstacle — whether you're successful or brand new, starting something new feels like a cold plunge
- The difference is: experienced entrepreneurs know the fear is normal and push through anyway

### How to Get There
**Option 1: Service-Based (Easiest Path)**
- Find 2-3 companies that need a service
- Do it for free first to get testimonials
- Get proof of results
- Then charge for the service
- Easiest if you're willing to do labor

**Option 2: Product-Based**
- Sam made his first $10,000 selling an info product online
- Another example: Sam also made his first $10,000 as a hot dog vendor with a stand called "Southern Sam's - We Wiener as Big as a Baby's Arm"
- Started with only $500 for supplies
- Sold out first day, reinvested in inventory

### Key Insight: Products vs. Services
> "I never felt rich doing services. When I created a product and made money, that's when I felt rich. When I was doing a service and working for it, I did not feel rich."

### The First Dollar Is Always Exciting
> "I've made maybe $100 million plus in revenue on the internet. The first dollar is always so exciting. It doesn't matter how much money you have. The first is always incredibly exciting. That rush never goes away."

### Common Mistakes at This Level
- **Not asking:** People pitch their service, name their price ($10,000), then immediately try to justify it when the buyer pauses. You have to sit in the silence.
- **Charging too little:** You're probably not charging enough. For many businesses, $10,000 is nothing.
- **Thinking too small:** It's almost easier to make $1M than $10K because you're forced to think bigger.

### Most Valuable Skill
> "Copywriting, without a doubt. Copywriting is the act of figuring out how to get what's in my brain into your brain and figuring out how to get you to do what I want you to do. It's just sales. We're all selling something — whether I'm trying to sell someone to go on a date with me, convince someone to work for me, raise money... it's all writing, it's all storytelling."

### Getting Customers
- **Forums/Reddit:** If you can write, get first customers on message boards
- **Yelp:** If you're a service provider, call people on Yelp
- **Cold calling:** Call 100 people, you'll get 2 interested
- **Cold email:** Sam built his life on cold email. 5 of his 6 best men were met via cold email.

### The ADA Framework for Cold Outreach
- **Attention:** Get their attention (acknowledge it's a cold email)
- **Interest:** Show you did your research — reference something specific they wrote years ago
- **Desire:** Make them desire what you offer
- **Action:** Give specific next steps: "Email me back if interested. If not, just say 'f off' and I'll never talk to you again."

### Follow Up or Die
> "I emailed Tim Westergren (founder of Pandora) 52 times. On the 52nd one, he said 'Yeah, sure.' I asked why he didn't respond before — he said 'I just didn't see them.'"

## Level 2: Making $100,000

### The Reality
> "$100,000 isn't scaling. You can still blunt force your way to $100,000. You can be a sledgehammer. You don't need to be a precision knife. You can chop down a tree with a sledgehammer if you swing enough times and hard enough."

### The Difference Between $10K and $100K
- **It's the same thing, just done more times**
- Don't overthink it — keep doing what worked
- Don't look up until you hit $2-3M in revenue

### What Changes at $100K/Month
- You need **distribution** — a way to get customers at scale
- Options: Build an audience, buy ads, cold call/email at scale, knock on doors, go to conferences
- Learn to buy ads

### Hiring Your First Employee
> "Hire whatever you suck at. Most startup founders are sales/marketing people — keep doing that. Hire operating people who keep the trains running on time."

**First Hire Philosophy:**
- The thing you're doing that drives sales — keep doing that
- Hire people to do everything else (fulfillment, operations)
- Until $5M in sales, YOU are the seller and marketer

**John Rockefeller's Rule:**
> "I would rather have 1% of 100 people's effort than 100% of my own effort."

### The One-Person Business Reality
> "I own a product called CopyThat.com. It's a copywriting course. It makes 6 figures a year. I have not logged into it in 8 months."
- Possible to make $100K+ as a solo operator
- But Sam doesn't find it fulfilling — relationships and people are more meaningful

### Time Horizon
- Getting to $3-5M is a pretty straightforward path
- Getting past $5-7M requires teams, systems, and leadership (the hard part)

## Level 3: Making $1,000,000

### The Defining Trait
> "Overcoming fear. The ability to overcome fear and take that first step."

### What Breaks People at This Level
**Inability to hire and make systems**
- Many founders are impatient
- They build to $1-2M, get bored after 3 years, bail and start over
- This is a "consistent habit of losers" — quitting even when it's working

### What You're Selling Doesn't Matter
> "Do you think what you sell really matters if $1 million is the goal? Nope. I don't."

**Example — Sam's Mother-in-Law:**
- Haitian immigrant, stay-at-home mom
- At age 54-55, started a cleaning business
- Made $1M+ revenue with no "entrepreneurial background"
- "If she can do it, anyone can do it"

### TAM (Total Addressable Market) Is Usually Big Enough
> "Virtually 100% — I would probably say literally every business can get to $10 million in revenue. The problem is it may take a long time and you're not willing to wait, or you might have to change the model."

### Co-Founders: Yes or No?
> "I think it's way easier to get to $10 million+ with a co-founder. Life's lonely. I prefer having a business partner."

**What to Look For in a Co-Founder:**
- Same values, opposite personalities
- Someone you respect and admire
- If you're analytical, find someone outgoing
- If you're emotional, find someone level-headed

### Normalizing Success
> "I remember surrounding myself with young guys in San Francisco when we were 21-23. When you're trying to do something scary, you need people around you egging each other on: 'You can do this, you got this, you're the best.'"

**The Kenyan Runner Story:**
- Sam ran track with a Kenyan who ran sub-4-minute miles
- Asked how he was so fast — genetics?
- "No. We were all raised being told we were born to run, that we would be the best. It was just what was expected of us."

### Goal Setting — The Four Fs
Sam's framework for goal setting:
1. **Family**
2. **Fitness** 
3. **Finance**
4. **Fun**
(Or **Faith** if religious)

> "You gotta be a total man. How your body feels, taking care of family financially, having a great time doing it."

**Fitness Challenge Examples:**
- Be an average NFL combine wide receiver (bench, 40-yard dash, vertical leap)
- Run a 21-minute 5K and bench 300 lbs in the same hour
- Do a 50-mile run

### Setting Inputs vs. Outputs
> "For family, it's hard to set metric goals. But you can set inputs. For me: I put my daughter to sleep every night. I do not travel. I haven't slept away from home since they've been born. That's my goal — I will not travel, therefore I will be there."

## Level 4: Making $10,000,000

### The Shift
> "At $10 million, it's people and systems. Getting the right people in the right seats and creating systems and checklists that the business can scale."

**Jason Lumpkin (VC) on Scaling:**
> "If a company gets to $10 million, it's inevitable that it gets to $100 million if they create the right systems."

### No Business Is "Unscalable"
> "Every business can get to $10 million. An agency? I know agencies with tens of thousands of employees making billions. What's not scalable? An individual's time — but that's why you hire people."

**Example — The Laundromat:**
- Could you get a laundromat to $10M?
- Open 100 locations
- Franchise
- Teach people how to have a laundromat and license your lead-gen system
- "Will that get to $10 million? For sure."

### The Rocket Ship vs. Cool Car
> "VC is fuel. It's rocket fuel for a rocket ship. I was building a cool ass car — fast, cool, that I loved. That was my media company. It grew 75% a year, made me wealthy, I had fun. A lot of my friends built rocket ships with VC. Of the 30 that did, about 2 worked out amazingly. The rest crashed."

### When Everything Feels Insurmountable
> "There's thresholds at maybe $1M, $10M, and some number maybe at $30-50M. Every challenge feels insurmountable, but it's definitely overcomeable."

### Selling The Hustle
> "I wanted to sell and hit a certain number. My business partner Joe told me $20 million was enough that you'll never have to work again. I was like, 'Alright, I will try to make that happen.'"

**Felix Dennis (Maxim Magazine):**
> "If I could do it all over again, I would work as hard as I could to make $20-35 million by age 35. That would just make you comfortable. I spent so much of my life in stress. Even when my companies were doing huge revenue, I never took money out — I was stressed all the time."

### What $10M Feels Like
> "I felt amazing. It was awesome. I'd been super broke — used to sneak onto buses, put 3 slices of pizza in a Whole Foods box meant for 1. It felt awesome not to be broke. It felt awesome to provide for my family."

### The Identity Shift
> "You can just kind of become anything you want. But you kind of have to pretend that you're it and then morph into it. Fake it till you make it."

**Ralph Lauren's Story:**
> "I wanted to be an actor but wasn't good-looking or charismatic enough. But I noticed when I wore cowboy outfits, I felt more like a cowboy. When I wore military uniform, I felt more tough, more brave. With my clothing, I want to give people costumes. When you dress like something, you become it."

## Level 5: Making $100,000,000

### The Honest Answer
> "I've never done it, so I'm trying to figure that out too. But I know a lot of people who have, and I think the answer is **time**. That's what I'm betting on. Things take a lot more time than you think."

**Example — My First Million Podcast:**
> "We've done 750 episodes. I remember when we started, I thought by month 6 we'd be at 100,000 downloads per episode. That took 4 years. I thought it would take 6 months. It took 3 years. It took forever."

### How to Keep Going When You Want to Quit
> "I feel like I want to quit all the time. All the time. I usually just put myself in positions where I can't bail — burning the boats."

**Why He Shows Up:**
> "We have this amazing producer Ari. If we don't do the podcast, I don't know if she would have this job. It feels good to show up and do a good job for her sake. I have to show up at this meeting every Monday and Wednesday at noon."

### What $100M Founders Have in Common
**Two Paths:**
1. **The Freaks:** Do it in 5 years by picking a "wicked market" — where the ball is rolling downhill and you just have to hold on
2. **The Long-Timers:** Just do it for a long time with solid execution

**Mark Lore (Diapers.com, Jet.com):**
> "I'm just amazing at raising money. It wasn't that good of an idea to start Jet.com — it was fine. But I raised crazy amounts of money, hired the best people, and we just got after it."

### The Power of Peer Groups
> "Haven't you ever hung out with someone who's just really amazing and you leave on a high? My friend David Senra has this podcast Founders. He has $100K+ in cameras. I asked if the difference between his cameras and DSLRs actually matters. Without skipping a beat: 'I'm trying to be the best in the world. Everything matters.' I left on a high. Excellence in everything."

**How to Access These People:**
- Join peer groups (Hampton, YPO, Vistage, associations)
- Pay to be around people at your level or above
- Country clubs, masterminds, curated communities

### What Great Founders Ask
> "'What did you learn from this lesson?' I told a successful founder about something bad that happened. He asked: 'Are you sure you learned the right lesson?' Maybe the lesson wasn't that the copy didn't work — maybe it was 'why were you tinkering with that when all these other things are important?'"

**Alex Hormozi on Initiatives:**
> "When you start a new initiative, the whole company's productivity goes down 20%. It has to get back to baseline, then be significantly greater than that 20% decrease. Otherwise, the new initiative cost you."

### The Integrity Factor
> "In my experience, people who are super successful are very high integrity. I've seen amazingly big deals happen on handshakes or just text messages. Keeping your word is a commonality."

**Sam's Business Partner Joe:**
> "He sold his first company for $114 million at age 24. He pays bills the second they come in — even with net-90 terms. We have this value called 'pride' — we show pride in how we treat people. If we fire someone, we go above and beyond on severance."

### Hampton's Vetting Process (4 Steps)
1. **Application:** Must have $3M+ revenue, $3M+ funding, or $10M+ exit
2. **Interview:** Membership team gives thumbs up/down
3. **Founder Review:** Sam and co-founder Joe watch all approved interviews
4. **Community Vote:** Members see your application and must approve

> "We get thousands of applications a month. It's a 3% acceptance rate."

### Are Your Goals Worth It?
> "Should someone set a goal to make $100 million? No. I mean, maybe they could, but no. Should someone set a goal to run a marathon at 2:30? No. Only if that fits their desire, if they think it will make them happy, if the journey will be exciting. That's not inherently good to me."

> "People should just pick what they want and pursue it relentlessly. I hope it makes them happy."

## Key Mental Models & Principles

### 1. The Eeky Guy (Ikigai Variation)
- What the world wants
- What they want to pay for  
- What you're good at
- What you care about

### 2. Follow Up or Die
- Persistence beats resistance
- 52 emails to Tim Westergren
- Most people stop at 1-2 follow-ups

### 3. Systems = Freedom
- At $10M, you need systems and the right people in right seats
- Most decisions are reversible (except cap table issues)

### 4. The Total Man Framework
- Family
- Fitness
- Finance
- Fun
- (Faith)

### 5. Treat Money Mean, Keep It Keen
> "Don't put money on a pedestal. Treat it mean, keep it keen. You got to treat the money mean a little bit. Respect it, but don't worship it."

### 6. Everything is Sales
> "Copywriting is just sales. We're all selling something — whether trying to get someone on a date, convince someone to work for us, raise money, or MLK giving a speech. It's all storytelling."

### 7. The Mormon Door-Knocking Philosophy
> "Mormons on mission knock on doors to convert people to their religion. Then they come work in sales: 'Oh, I just have to sell software with a 2-3% conversion rate? I knocked on 10,000 doors and got one guy to come to a religious event. This is easy.'"

### 8. Normalize Success
> "Surround yourself with people who were raised being told they would be great. Tell yourself and your peers: 'You got this, you're the best, you're born to do this.'"

### 9. Plus Talent
> "If this person quits, will you say 'goodbye, have a nice day' or 'please don't go, I need you so much'? If you have the second feeling, that's plus talent."

### 10. Manifest Cowboys
> "I just love being a guy who has an idea and can turn it into reality really quickly. Ralph, manifest cowboys."

## Summary: The Five Levels

| Level | Key Focus | What Breaks People |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **$10K** | Service or simple product, copywriting, cold outreach | Not asking, charging too little, fear |
| **$100K** | Same thing more times, first hires, 40-50 hrs/week | Overthinking, trying to "scale" too early |
| **$1M** | Systems, hiring, patience, co-founders | Impatience, quitting when it works, poor hiring |
| **$10M** | People in right seats, systems, checklists | Not building systems, wrong team |
| **$100M** | Time, capital, solid execution, right market | Not giving it enough time, poor capital allocation |
