- Startup CEOs: when framing your competition to investors, recognize that in investors’ eyes, is a hierarchy of competition, from most worrisome to least. (1) Venture funded startup competitors: investors are extremely worried when there are other venture funded startups trying to solve the same problem for the same customer segment as you are. Many will pass until a clear winner emerges. (2) Large tech company’s core product: if you are tackling the same problem as a mega cap tech company, that’s the second most worrisome to investors. You better have a multi year lead and some unique competitive advantage / distribution channel, and even that might not be enough to convince investors. (3) Large tech company’s secondary product: it’s not as worry inducing, bur you’ll still need to have a proactive plan that shows how you will win. (4) Legacy competitors / manual processes: this is the best case scenario. Implication: a crowded venture landscape (> 2 companies) clustered around a problem space is one of the most challenging things to surmount.

- WEDGE PRODUCTS If your product is complex, your sales process is long, or you're selling to large enterprises (or all of the above), you might need a wedge product. A wedge product is a lightweight, easy-to-use, self-serve product that can be instantly used by an individual (without going through a purchasing process) and delivers value very quickly. Think of the wedge product as a gateway drug to the broader, more complex offering. In some cases, the wedge product could be a simpler version of your product (eg: Atlassian does a great job here of having free versions of their products); in other cases, it might be a completely different product. HubSpot CRM is a powerful standalone free CRM that supports unlimited users, and acts as a gateway to sell HubSpot's suite of marketing, sales, and customer service tools. Similarly, Square POS is a free standalone POS (downloadable from the app store, no less!) that enables an easier onramp to use payment processing. As a hypothetical example, for a complex security product, could a lightweight "vulnerability scanner" be a powerful wedge product? The goal of the wedge product is to dramatically lower the friction to acquire and onboard new customers, and to broaden your top of funnel. Its success should be evaluated purely based on adoption (versus on revenue). CEOs/Founders: In this era of payback periods efficiency and longer enterprise sales cycles, a self-serve wedge product can be a game changer. It's worth thinking deeply about what a good wedge product for your offering might be.
	- source: https://x.com/gokulr/status/1664026902464720896
	- pages: [[ Product]] 

- BUILD AN “ALWAYS ON” PRODUCT Most products must be actively used to derive value. The customer has to do work to get benefit from it. And post-use, it just lies there, fallow, until used again. Think old school CRM or email marketing. They lie inert unless used and require a tremendous amount of activation energy to properly deploy. Now, consider an “always on” product: One that is always on, seamless, automated. One that drives customers / revenue or lowers cost in the background, in an evergreen way. Set and forget. Think Google AdWords. Configure your creative and campaign / budget parameters, click and it will run forever, adjust bids, and drive customers unceasingly , even while the customer is asleep. Which do you think customers prefer? Founders: take a hard look at your product. Is it always on? In the age of generative AI, it has never been easier to transform your “customer activated / static” product to an “always on” product that keeps running, improving, delivering value. Don’t try to boil the ocean - start with one module that’s most suited for this, make it “always on” in some way, and then move to the next module. Your customers - and business - will thank you for it. Otherwise, you risk getting disrupted by a product that just “works” (and never stops working).

- HIRE A SMART GENERALIST This post is three years old but I figure it's worth repeating each year for the newest annual cohort of founders. Founders: if you're harried and not able to juggle all the balls that need juggling, pls consider hiring a smart generalist. And if you need potential hiring pools for this person, look to second year MBA students at your local business schools. Hire them as interns, evaluate them through a 6-12 month period, and then convert them to full-time employees upon graduation. This kind of role (generalist bizops at a tech startup) is what many B-School students aspire to. And after 1-2 years in this role, they move on to a functional role (product, marketing, customer success, ops, etc) while backfilling their role. Win-win.
