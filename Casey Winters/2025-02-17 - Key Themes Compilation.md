# Casey Winters - Key Themes Compilation

**Compiled:** 2025-02-17  
**Sources:** 10 major essays/talks (2017-2022)

---

## Overview

Casey Winters is the Chief Product Officer at Eventbrite and former Head of Growth at Pinterest. He's best known for creating frameworks that help product teams understand and execute sustainable growth.

His work focuses on practical, data-driven approaches to growing products—distinguishing between growth hacks (tactics) and growth systems (strategies).

---

## Core Themes

### 1. The Adjacent User Theory

**The Framework**
Products grow until they hit a ceiling. The ceiling isn't market saturation; it's that you've captured all users who are currently willing to try your product.

To continue growing, identify and remove barriers for the "adjacent user"—the person who knows about your product, might find it useful, but isn't using it.

**Key Insights**
- Growth stalls are user-ceiling problems, not product-market fit problems
- Adjacent users are close to using you—they just have a barrier
- Common barriers: awareness, access, language, capability, trust
- Remove barriers one user type at a time

**Application**
- Map current users (who's using it now?)
- Identify adjacent users (who's blocked?)
- Find the barrier (survey/interview)
- Remove it
- Repeat

**Famous Quote:** "Products grow until they don't."

---

### 2. Product-Market Fit Isn't Enough

**The Framework**
PMF is necessary but insufficient for growth. You also need:
- Product-Channel Fit (can acquire users profitably)
- Retention (do users keep coming back?)
- Sustainable unit economics (LTV > CAC)

**Key Insights**
- PMF is table stakes, not a guarantee
- The real question: can you acquire users profitably and keep them engaged?
- Retention is the biggest post-PMF failure mode
- Users can love a product but only need it occasionally (occasion-based retention)

**Application**
After PMF, the work changes:
- Optimize acquisition costs
- Improve retention
- Build growth loops
- Expand to adjacent users

**Famous Quote:** "People treat PMF like a finish line. It's the starting line."

---

### 3. Growth Loops > Funnels

**The Framework**
Funnels are linear: spend → acquire → maybe retain. Funnels don't compound.

Loops are circular: output becomes input. Every user acquired helps acquire more users.

**Three Types**
- **Viral Loops:** Users bring users (invites, sharing)
- **Content Loops:** Users create content → content attracts users → cycle
- **Paid Loops:** Revenue → ads → users → revenue

**Key Insights**
- Loops compound, funnels don't
- Multiple loops compound on each other
- Viral is rare; don't force it
- Content loops take time but build assets
- Paid loops are valid if unit economics work

**Application**
- Map your growth loop
- Design for compounding
- Measure viral coefficient or content production rate
- Build systems, not tactics

**Famous Quote:** "Funnels don't compound. You have to keep spending to keep growing."

---

### 4. The First Week Is Everything

**The Framework**
Users who become long-term active users behave differently in their first week than those who churn.

The first week is when users decide: Is this for me? Is this useful? Should I come back?

**Key Insights**
- Day 2 return rate predicts long-term retention
- Users need an "aha moment"—the point where they understand the value
- Onboarding should be designed around that aha moment
- Follow up within 24 hours

**Application**
- Identify your aha moment (what action predicts retention?)
- Design onboarding to get there fast
- Remove friction
- Use email/push to bring them back

**Famous Quote:** "The first week is when users decide: Is this for me?"

---

### 5. Retention Is All You Need

**The Framework**
Most companies over-optimize for acquisition while ignoring retention—the leaky bucket problem.

Retention determines:
- Unit economics (LTV)
- Growth potential (compounding user base)
- Market size
- Valuation

**Types of Retention**
- **Occasion-based:** Specific moments, low frequency
- **Value-based:** Ongoing utility, medium frequency
- **Habit-based:** Daily routine, automatic (best)

**Key Insights**
- Retention curves tell the truth—do they flatten?
- A 10% retention improvement > 10% acquisition improvement
- Plug leaks before pouring water

**Application**
- Balance acquisition and retention
- Optimize retention first
- Measure cohort retention curves
- Aim for habit formation

**Famous Quote:** "Retention is all you need."

---

### 6. Product Strategy for Growth

**The Framework**
Good product strategy includes: Who are we serving? What problem? How are we different? **How will we grow?**

The four growth levers:
- **Acquisition:** How do people find us?
- **Activation:** How quickly do they experience value?
- **Retention:** Do they keep coming back?
- **Monetization:** Can we make money?

**Key Insights**
- Growth isn't separate from product
- Product owns all four levers
- Trade-offs matter: depth vs breadth, engagement vs acquisition
- Build systems, not tactics

**Application**
- Make growth part of product strategy
- Map your four levers
- Identify bottlenecks
- Build loops, not hacks

---

### 7. SEO as a Growth Engine

**The Framework**
SEO isn't magic. It's a system:

Content → Indexing → Ranking → Clicks → Users → Content

Each step requires work. Most companies fail at one or more steps.

**Key Insights**
- Technical SEO first (crawlability, speed)
- Content quality > quantity
- User-generated content scales infinitely
- SEO takes years—most companies quit early
- Content loops create SEO compounding

**Application**
- Map the SEO flywheel
- Fix technical basics first
- Build content at scale (preferably UGC)
- Be patient

---

### 8. Growth Is Barrier Removal

**The Framework**
Growth work is removing barriers that prevent people from using your product.

Barriers:
- **Awareness:** Don't know it exists
- **Access:** Can't get it (geography, platform, price)
- **Language/Cultural:** Doesn't feel like "for them"
- **Capability:** Too hard to use
- **Trust:** Don't trust it yet

**Key Insights**
- Most products don't need more features
- They need barriers removed
- Product > Productivity (experience matters more than features)
- Change how people experience the product, not the product itself

**Application**
- Identify barriers through user research
- Categorize barrier types
- Remove one at a time
- Measure if adjacent users start converting

**Famous Quote:** "Growth work is barrier removal."

## Key Mental Models

1. **Adjacent User Theory** — Identify and remove barriers for the next user type
2. **Growth Loops** — Build compounding systems, not linear funnels
3. **The First Week** — First-week experience determines long-term retention
4. **Retention Curves** — Curves that flatten indicate sustainable growth
5. **Barrier Removal** — Growth is removing blocks, not adding features
6. **Four Levers** — Acquisition, activation, retention, monetization
7. **PMF Is Starting Line** — Real growth starts after PMF
8. **SEO Flywheel** — Content → indexing → ranking → clicks → users → content
