# Casey Winters - Retention Is All You Need

**Date:** 2020  
**Source:** Reforge Essay

---

## About This Essay

Casey's argument that retention is the North Star metric that actually determines growth success—and why most companies over-optimize for acquisition while ignoring retention.

---

## The Essay

### The Leaky Bucket

**Casey Winters:** I see it constantly: companies pour money into acquisition while ignoring retention.

This is the leaky bucket problem. You fill the bucket (acquire users), water leaks out (users churn), you pour more in.

If you don't fix the leaks, you never fill the bucket.

### Why Retention Matters

**Winters:** Retention determines:

**1. Unit Economics**
- If users churn quickly, LTV is low
- Can't spend much on acquisition
- Unit economics don't work

**2. Growth Potential**
- High retention = compounding user base
- Low retention = linear growth

**3. Market Size**
- With low retention, you quickly churn through the market
- With high retention, your addressable market grows

**4. Valuation**
- Investors value retention
- SaaS companies valued on LTV/CAC
- Retention drives LTV

### Pinterest's Retention Story

**Winters:** When I joined Pinterest, we focused heavily on retention. Here's why:

**Before:**
- Lots of users signing up
- Many never returning
- Retention curves didn't flatten

**After:**
- Fewer signups (we reduced acquisition spend)
- Much higher return rate
- Retention curves flattened
- Growth accelerated

We plugged the leak, then poured water in.

### Types of Retention

**Winters:** Not all retention is equal:

**1. Occasion-Based**
- Users need you at specific moments
- Low frequency but high intent
- Examples: Wedding planning, tax software

**2. Habit-Based**
- Part of daily/weekly routine
- High frequency, automatic usage
- Examples: Social media, email, news

**3. Value-Based**
- Users get ongoing value
- Return when they need more value
- Examples: Dropbox, Spotify, SaaS tools

**Habit-based is best.** But it's hardest to achieve.

### Retention Curves

**Winters:** Retention curves tell you everything:

**Good retention:**
- Starts at day 1
- Drops quickly at first
- Then flattens
- Plateaus at some %

**Bad retention:**
- Continues dropping
- Never flattens
- Approaches zero

### The Retention Formula

**Winters:** Retention is:

**Retention = Activation × Engagement × Habit Formation**

**Activation:**
- Do they experience value?
- The aha moment

**Engagement:**
- Do they keep using it?
- Frequency, depth

**Habit Formation:**
- Does it become automatic?
- Triggers, reinforcement

### Improving Retention

**Winters:** Tactics for each stage:

**Activation:**
- Better onboarding
- Faster time to value
- Clear aha moment

**Engagement:**
- Core product improvements
- Notifications (useful, not spammy)
- Personalized content

**Habit Formation:**
- Triggers (time-based, action-based)
- Variable rewards
- Social components
- Streaks/gamification

### The 30-60-90 Rule

**Winters:** Retention benchmarks:

**Consumer apps:**
- 30-day: 20-30%
- 60-day: 15-25%
- 90-day: 10-20%

**SaaS:**
- 30-day: 60-80%
- 60-day: 50-70%
- 90-day: 40-60%

Below these? You have a retention problem.

### Measuring Retention

**Winters:** Key retention metrics:

**1. Cohort Retention**
- % of users from [date] still active

**2. Day N Retention**
- % returning on day 1, 7, 30, etc.

**3. Rolling Retention**
- % who used in last 30 days

**4. LTV**
- Lifetime value

**5. Payback Period**
- Time to recover CAC

### Retention > Acquisition

**Winters:** If you're optimizing one, optimize retention:

- Acquisition gets you users
- Retention keeps them
- Retention improves unit economics
- Retention compounds

A 10% improvement in retention is often worth more than 10% more acquisition.

### 4. Retention > Acquisition
Optimize retention first, then acquire.
