# Casey Winters - Interview with Lenny Rachitsky

**Date:** 2020  
**Source:** Lenny's Newsletter  
**Interviewer:** Lenny Rachitsky

---

## About This Interview

A deep dive into Casey's frameworks for growth, his time at Pinterest, and lessons from scaling multiple companies.

---

## The Interview

### On Starting at Pinterest

**Lenny Rachitsky:** Tell me about joining Pinterest. What was the situation?

**Casey Winters:** I joined in 2012. Pinterest was growing fast but hitting a ceiling. We had this core user base—mostly women, mostly doing crafts and recipes—but we couldn't break out.

The growth team was basically throwing spaghetti at the wall. Lots of optimization, no strategy.

**Rachitsky:** What changed?

**Winters:** We developed the "Adjacent User Theory"—though we didn't call it that yet. We realized we hadn't captured all women who might use Pinterest. We'd just captured the easiest ones.

### The Growth Ceiling

**Rachitsky:** You wrote about products hitting ceilings. What happens?

**Winters:** Every product eventually plateaus. You've captured all the users who are already inclined to try you.

The mistake is thinking you need a new product or feature. Usually, you just need to make your existing product work for people who are close to becoming users.

**Rachitsky:** Give me an example.

**Winters:** At Pinterest, we had lots of women visit, create accounts, then never come back. Why?

They'd sign up, see an empty feed, and think "I don't know what to do."

The fix wasn't changing the product. It was changing the onboarding. We gave them pre-populated feeds. Showed them what Pinterest was.

### The Retention Problem

**Rachitsky:** Why do good products struggle with retention?

**Winters:** Most products have "occasion-based" retention. You need the user to want your product at a specific moment.

The best products create habits. They fit into the user's daily routine. Think Instagram, TikTok, email.

Pinterest was somewhere in between. People loved it but only needed it when they were planning specific things—weddings, home decor, recipes.

**Rachitsky:** How did you improve it?

**Winters:** Email and push notifications. Not spammy—genuinely useful recommendations. We personalized them based on what you'd saved.

"You liked modern kitchens? Here's more modern kitchens."

But we had to be careful. Too many emails and people unsubscribe. Too few and they forget about you.

### On Growth Teams

**Rachitsky:** You've grown growth teams. What's your approach?

**Winters:** Three principles:

**1. Own the full funnel**
Don't just optimize one part. Own acquisition, activation, retention, monetization.

**2. Systems over tactics**
Don't chase tactics. Build compounding systems—loops, personalization, automation.

**3. Data + qual**
Numbers tell you what. Qualitative research tells you why. You need both.

### The Adjacent User

**Rachitsky:** Can you explain the Adjacent User Theory simply?

**Winters:** Sure. Your product grows until you've captured everyone who's already willing to try it.

Then it stalls.

The "adjacent user" is the person who knows about your product, might find it useful, but has something blocking them.

They're "adjacent" to being active users. You just need to identify and remove the barrier.

Every time you unlock an adjacent user type, you unlock a new growth wave.

**Rachitsky:** How do you find them?

**Winters:** Look at people who:
- Visit but don't sign up
- Sign up but don't activate
- Activate but don't retain

Survey them. Interview them. Figure out what's blocking them.

### International Growth

**Rachitsky:** You did a lot of international work at Pinterest.

**Winters:** Yeah, international was our biggest opportunity. When I joined, Pinterest was almost entirely US.

By the time I left, over 50% of users were international.

The playbook:
1. Localize the product (language, cultural references)
2. Localize the content (local content creators)
3. Localize growth channels (local SEO, local partnerships)
4. Hire local teams who understand the market

**Rachitsky:** Was product-market fit consistent globally?

**Winters:** Generally, yes. Pinterest's core concept—visual discovery—was universal.

But the content was different. Brazilian users wanted Brazilian content. German users wanted German content.

We had to build local content ecosystems.

### On Moving to Eventbrite

**Rachitsky:** Why Eventbrite? Pinterest was huge.

**Winters:** I wanted a new challenge. Pinterest was mature. Eventbrite was earlier stage, more complexity.

Events are hard. Every event is different. The organizers, the attendees, the use cases. Lots of variability.

Plus, COVID hit right after I joined. We had to pivot hard to virtual events.

**Rachitsky:** What did you learn from that?

**Winters:** How fast you can move when you have to.

We went from in-person to virtual in weeks. Built new products, new discovery, new ticketing.

Crisis creates clarity. Everyone aligned on the same goal.

### Advice for Growth PMs

**Rachitsky:** What advice would you give someone starting in product growth?

**Winters:** Three things:

**1. Learn the basics**
- Metrics and experimentation
- User research
- Basic statistics

**2. Understand your users**
- Spend time with them
- Read their support tickets
- Watch them use your product

**3. Think in systems**
- Don't just optimize tactics
- Build growth loops
- Create compounding effects

And one more: Be patient. Growth takes time. Everyone wants overnight success. Real growth is built over years.

## Key Insights

### 1. Retention Is Occasion-Based
Products retain users when they fit into specific moments or create habits.

### 2. Growth Is Not Just Optimization
It's about unlocking adjacent users and removing barriers.

### 3. International Requires Local Content
Localization isn't just translation. It's content ecosystems.

### 4. Data + Qualitative
Numbers tell you what. People tell you why.

### 5. Build Systems, Not Tactics
Compounding growth comes from loops, not one-off wins.
