# Casey Winters - The Adjacent User Theory

**Date:** 2020  
**Source:** Reforge Essay & Talk  
**Topic:** Product Growth Strategy

---

## About This Framework

Casey Winters' "Adjacent User Theory" explains why products grow until they hit a ceiling—and how to break through. The theory suggests that growth stalls when you've saturated your current user type, and you need to identify and build for the "adjacent user" to continue growing.

---

## The Theory

### The Core Insight

**Casey Winters:** Products grow until they don't. When growth slows, people assume it's product-market fit issues or market saturation. But usually, it's simpler: you've captured all the users who are already willing to try your product.

To grow further, you don't need to change the product. You need to make the product work for the next user type—the "adjacent user."

### Who Is the Adjacent User?

**Winters:** The adjacent user is the person who knows about your product, is aware it might be useful, isn't actively using it, and has a barrier preventing them from doing so.

They're close to being active users, but something is stopping them.

### Examples

**Pinterest:**
- **Initial users:** American women interested in crafts, recipes, home decor
- **Adjacent user 1:** International users (different languages, different content)
- **Adjacent user 2:** Men (perceived as "not for me")
- **Adjacent user 3:** Younger users (Gen Z)

Each required different strategies:
- International: Localization, local content
- Men: Different content verticals, onboarding messaging
- Gen Z: Mobile-first, video content

**Uber:**
- **Initial:** Tech-savvy urbanites who value convenience
- **Adjacent user 1:** Suburban users (need different coverage model)
- **Adjacent user 2:** Price-sensitive users (UberPool)
- **Adjacent user 3:** Non-tech-savvy (need easier app, phone booking)

## The Framework

### Step 1: Identify Your Current User
**Winters:** Before you can find the adjacent user, you need to understand who you're currently serving. Look at:
- Demographics
- Behaviors
- Acquisition channels
- Engagement patterns

### Step 2: Find the Adjacent User
**Winters:** The adjacent user is usually:
- Already aware of your product
- Not using it
- Has a barrier (perceived or real)

Common barriers:
- **Awareness barrier:** Don't know it exists or what it does
- **Access barrier:** Can't get it (geography, platform, price)
- **Language/cultural barrier:** Doesn't feel like "for them"
- **Capability barrier:** Too hard to use
- **Trust barrier:** Don't trust it yet

### Step 3: Remove Barriers
**Winters:** Growth work is removing barriers. You have two options:
- **Change the product** (rarely needed)
- **Change how people experience the product** (usually the answer)

**Examples of barrier removal:**
- Localization (remove language barrier)
- Price tiering (remove price barrier)
- Simplified onboarding (remove complexity barrier)
- Referral incentives (remove trust barrier)
- Better empty states (remove "don't know what to do" barrier)

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Growth Stalls Are User-Ceiling Problems
Growth slows not because the product is bad, but because you've captured everyone who currently fits your ideal user profile.

### 2. The Adjacent User Is Close
They're not a completely different market. They're your current users' friends, family, coworkers. They're just blocked by something you can fix.

### 3. One Adjacent User at a Time
Don't try to serve everyone. Pick one adjacent user type, solve for them, then move to the next.

### 4. Productivity > Product
Usually, the product is fine. The issue is how people discover it, sign up, onboard, or understand its value.

### 5. Growth Is Sequential
You grow by unlocking adjacent users one after another, not by trying to serve everyone at once.

## Application

### When to Use This
- Growth is slowing or plateauing
- You're trying to expand to new markets
- Feature usage is concentrated in one user type

### How to Apply
1. Map your current user (who's using it now?)
2. Survey/ interview non-users (what's stopping you?)
3. Categorize barriers (awareness, access, language, capability, trust)
4. Prioritize one adjacent user type
5. Remove their barrier
6. Measure if they start converting
7. Repeat

---

## Impact

The Adjacent User Theory has become a foundational framework for product growth teams. Companies like Pinterest, Uber, and Eventbrite have used it to systematically expand beyond initial user bases.

Unlike "growth hacking" tactics, this is a strategic framework for sustainable expansion.
