# Paul Graham - Interview with Kevin Rose (Foundation)

**Date:** 2015  
**Source:** Foundation (Kevin Rose's interview series)  
**Interviewer:** Kevin Rose

---

## About This Interview

A wide-ranging conversation covering Paul's early days, the founding of Y Combinator, his writing process, and advice for founders.

---

## The Interview

### On Early Programming

**Kevin Rose:** When did you start programming?

**Paul Graham:** I was in high school. This was the early 1980s. My dad bought me a computer and I just started tinkering. Back then you had to really want to program — there was no internet, no Stack Overflow. You figured things out yourself.

**Rose:** What drew you to Lisp?

**Graham:** Lisp is beautiful. It's the most elegant programming language ever created. It has this property where the language itself can be extended by the programmer. That power is incredible.

### On Viaweb

**Rose:** Tell me about Viaweb. That's where you made your money, right?

**Graham:** Viaweb was an online store builder. We started it in 1995. The idea was: let people create stores without knowing HTML.

**Rose:** You sold it to Yahoo for a lot of money.

**Graham:** $49 million. Which seemed like a crazy amount at the time. We were three people.

**Rose:** What did you learn from Viaweb?

**Graham:** Two big things. One: startups need to grow fast or die. We were growing 10% a week. Two: the technology matters less than the problem you're solving. We built Viaweb in Lisp, but that wasn't why we succeeded.

### On Starting Y Combinator

**Rose:** How did Y Combinator happen?

**Graham:** After Viaweb, I started writing essays. People liked them. I realized I liked giving startup advice more than most things.

In 2005, my friend Robert Morris and I decided to fund some startups. The idea was: give smart people a small amount of money and see what they could build in the summer. That became YC.

**Rose:** Did you think it would become this big?

**Graham:** Absolutely not. The first batch was 8 companies. Airbnb was in the second batch. Dropbox was in the third. It just grew from there.

### On Writing

**Rose:** You've written so many influential essays. How do you write?

**Graham:** I think about things for a long time. Then when I'm ready, I write fast. The thinking is 90% of it. The writing is just getting it down.

**Rose:** Where do the ideas come from?

**Graham:** From observing startups. YC gave me a window into hundreds of companies. Patterns emerge. You see what works and what doesn't.

### On Good Founders

**Rose:** What makes a good founder?

**Graham:** Determination and flexibility. That sounds contradictory, but it's not. You have to be determined to succeed, but flexible about how.

Also: good founders are relentlessly resourceful. They just find ways to make things happen.

**Rose:** What do bad founders do?

**Graham:** They give up too easily. Or they're too rigid — they fall in love with their original idea and can't pivot.

### On Silicon Valley

**Rose:** Why is Silicon Valley so special?

**Graham:** It's a place where failure is okay. That's rare. In most places, if you start a company and it fails, you're damaged goods. In the Valley, people say "What did you learn?"

Also: it's a dense network. Everyone knows everyone. Information flows fast.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Technology is Not the Point
Viaweb succeeded because it solved a problem, not because of Lisp.

### 2. Writing is Thinking
Good essays come from deep thinking, not just writing skill.

### 3. Determination + Flexibility
The best founders persist but adapt.

### 4. Failure is Acceptable
Silicon Valley's acceptance of failure enables risk-taking.

### 5. Networks Matter
Dense networks accelerate information and ideas.
