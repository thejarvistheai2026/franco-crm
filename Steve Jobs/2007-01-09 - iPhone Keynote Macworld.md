# Steve Jobs - iPhone Keynote (Macworld 2007)

**Date:** January 9, 2007  
**Source:** Macworld Conference & Expo, San Francisco  
**Location:** Moscone Center, San Francisco, California  
**Presenter:** Steve Jobs (Apple CEO)

---

## About This Keynote

Steve Jobs' iPhone introduction at Macworld 2007 is widely considered one of the greatest product launches in history. The presentation revealed a device that combined three products — a widescreen iPod, a revolutionary mobile phone, and an internet communicator — into one breakthrough device.

The iPhone would go on to fundamentally reshape not just the smartphone industry, but multiple industries including telecommunications, software, media, and commerce.

---

## The Presentation

### Opening: "Three Products"

**Steve Jobs:** This is a day I've been looking forward to for two and a half years. Every once in a while, a revolutionary product comes along that changes everything. And Apple has been — well, first of all, one's very fortunate if you get to work on just one of these in your career. Apple's been very fortunate. It's been able to introduce a few of these into the world.

In 1984, we introduced the Macintosh. It didn't just change Apple, it changed the whole computer industry.

In 2001, we introduced the first iPod, and it didn't just change the way we all listen to music, it changed the entire music industry.

Well, today, we're introducing THREE revolutionary products of this class.

The first one: is a widescreen iPod with touch controls.

The second: is a revolutionary mobile phone.

And the third is a breakthrough Internet communications device.

So, three things: a widescreen iPod with touch controls; a revolutionary mobile phone; and a breakthrough Internet communications device. An iPod, a phone, and an Internet communicator. An iPod, a phone... Are you getting it?

These are not three separate devices. This is ONE device. And we are calling it iPhone.

Today, Apple is going to reinvent the phone. And here it is.

[Shows iPhone on screen]

No, actually here it is. But we're going to leave it there for now.

### The Problem with Existing Phones

**Steve:** Why do we need a revolutionary user interface? Here's four smartphones, right? Motorola Q, the BlackBerry, Palm Treo, Nokia E62 — the usual suspects. And what's wrong with their user interfaces? Well, the problem with them is really sort of in the bottom 40 there. It's this stuff right here.

[Points to physical keyboards on screen]

They all have these keyboards that are there whether you need them or not to be there. And they all have these control buttons that are fixed in plastic and are the same for every application. Well, every application wants a slightly different user interface, a slightly optimized set of buttons, just for it.

And what happens if you think of a great idea six months from now? You can't run around and add a button to these things. They're already shipped.

So what are we going to do?

### The Solution: Multi-Touch

**Steve:** What we're going to do is get rid of all these buttons and just make a giant screen. A giant screen.

Now, how are we going to communicate this? We don't want to carry around a mouse, right? So what are we going to do? Oh, a stylus, right? We're going to use a stylus. No.

[Audience laughs]

No. Who wants a stylus? You have to get 'em and put 'em away, and you lose 'em. Yuck. Nobody wants a stylus. So let's not use a stylus.

We're going to use the best pointing device in the world. We're going to use a pointing device that we're all born with — we're born with ten of them. We'll use our fingers. We're going to touch this with our fingers. And we have invented a new technology called multi-touch, which is phenomenal. It works like magic.

You don't need a stylus. It's far more accurate than any touch display that's ever been shipped. It ignores unintended touches, it's super-smart. You can do multi-finger gestures on it. And boy, have we patented it.

So we have been very lucky to have brought a few revolutionary user interfaces to the market in our time. First was the mouse. The second was the click wheel. And now, we're going to bring multi-touch to the market.

[Demonstrates iPhone features live on stage]

### Revolutionary Features Demo

**iPod Functionality:**
- Cover flow browsing
- Touch scrolling
- Widescreen video

**Phone Functionality:**
- Visual voicemail — "Random access voicemail!"
- Smart dialing with touch
- SMS with threaded conversations
- Photos assigned to contacts

**Internet Communicator:**
- Full Safari web browser
- Real web pages, not "baby web"
- Multi-touch zoom with pinch
- Google Maps with satellite view
- YouTube

### Design and Hardware

**Steve:** So this is what iPhone looks like from the front. It is really thin. It is thinner than any smartphone out there. At 11.6 millimeters, it's thinner than the Moto Q, the BlackJack, the Treo, the Nokia E61. All of them.

It has a 3.5 inch display on the front. It has a 2 megapixel camera. And it's really small. It's 4.5 by 2.4 by 0.46 inches.

It has three sensors on it. It's got a proximity sensor that knows when it's up to your ear and turns off the display and saves power. It's got an ambient light sensor that adjusts the brightness to save power. And it's got a accelerometer that knows when you switch from portrait to landscape.

### Software Keyboard

**Steve:** Now, how are we going to do this? We want a keyboard. The question is: How are we going to do it? Well, let's take the best keyboard in the world — the ones we're shipping on our laptops — and let's shrink it down. And let's get rid of all the keys.

[Demonstrates software keyboard appearing on screen]

There's no keys! The keys are only there when you need them. And they come up with intelligent software to help you type.

[Demonstrates typing]

It takes a little getting used to, but it's really fast. The software helps you. And if you hit a wrong key, it figures out what you meant. It's really smart.

### Partnerships

**Steve:** We've got a great partnership with Google. We've got Google Maps. We've got satellite view. You can zoom in. You can see where you are. You can get directions.

And we're also adding in YouTube. This is the first time you can see YouTube on a phone.

### The Internet in Your Pocket

**Steve:** This is the REAL internet. Not some kind of mobile web. This is the real deal. You can see CNN. You can see the New York Times. And you can see it with this incredible ease of use.

So, iPhone is like having your internet in your pocket.

### Revolutionary and Magical

**Steve:** Now, I've got something to tell you. Today, Apple is going to reinvent the phone.

We are calling it iPhone.

Now, we're going to be shipping these in June of this year in the US. We're going to be partnering with Cingular. And in Europe in the fourth quarter. And in Asia in 2008.

So, iPhone is a revolutionary and magical product that is literally five years ahead of any other mobile phone.

And we are so excited about this. We are so excited because, you know, we make computers. We make the best computers in the world. We make the best operating system in the world. And we make the best MP3 players in the world. And now we're making the best phone in the world.

## Key Moments

### 1. "Three Products"
The famous rhetorical device: iPod, phone, internet communicator — building suspense before the reveal that it's one device.

### 2. "Who Wants a Stylus?"
Jobs' contempt for the stylus became iconic. The rejection of existing paradigms was central to Apple's design philosophy.

### 3. "Touch Your Music"
The cover flow demo showed the magic of multi-touch. Flicking through album covers felt futuristic.

### 4. "Random Access Voicemail"
Visual voicemail seems small now, but in 2007, it was revolutionary. No more "press 7 to delete."

### 5. "The Real Internet"
Jobs emphasized that iPhone ran real Safari, not a dumbed-down "mobile web." This was crucial to the product's appeal.

### 6. "Five Years Ahead"
The claim that iPhone was "literally five years ahead" of competition seemed audacious. History proved it was conservative.

## Why This Keynote Matters

1. **Product launch as theater** — Jobs turned a product announcement into a masterclass in storytelling
2. **Category creation** — iPhone didn't just improve smartphones; it created the modern smartphone category
3. **Multi-touch as magic** — The gesture interface felt like science fiction come to life
4. **Ecosystem thinking** — iPhone wasn't just hardware; it was iTunes, Safari, Google Maps, YouTube
5. **The post-PC era** — This keynote signaled that phones would become primary computing devices
6. **Design as differentiator** — Thin, beautiful, intuitive — iPhone made other phones look obsolete

---

## Impact

- **June 29, 2007:** iPhone launched to lines around the block
- **First weekend:** 270,000 units sold
- **2007 total:** 1.4 million units
- **App Store (2008):** Unlocked the platform's potential
- **By 2010:** 100 million iPhones sold
- **Legacy:** Established the template for all modern smartphones

Jobs' claim that iPhone was "five years ahead" was proven accurate. Competitors took years to catch up, and some (BlackBerry, Palm) never did.
