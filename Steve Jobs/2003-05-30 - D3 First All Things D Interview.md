# Steve Jobs - First All Things D Interview (D3, 2003)

**Date:** May 30, 2003  
**Source:** All Things D (D3 Conference)  
**Location:** Half Moon Bay, California  
**Interviewer:** Walt Mossberg and Kara Swisher  
**Guest:** Steve Jobs (Apple CEO)

---

## About This Interview

Steve Jobs' first appearance at All Things D (D3) in 2003 was a pivotal moment. Apple had just launched the iTunes Music Store and was beginning its transformation from a niche computer company to a consumer electronics giant. The iPod was gaining traction, but the iPhone was still four years away.

This was the beginning of what would become an iconic series of interviews between Jobs and Mossberg at the D conferences.

---

## Topics Covered

### iTunes Music Store

**Walt Mossberg:** You just launched the iTunes Music Store. Why did you go into the music business?

**Steve Jobs:** Music is a part of everyone's life. Everyone. And it hasn't been changed much in 20 years. We looked at the music industry and saw that people were downloading music for free from Kazaa and other services. They wanted digital music. But they were stealing it because there was no legal alternative that was as easy.

We thought we could create something better. Something easy, legal, and fair-priced. 99 cents a song. No subscription. Just buy what you want.

**Kara Swisher:** The record labels must have been skeptical.

**Steve:** They were terrified. They had seen Napster destroy their business model. They were looking for a lifeline. We came to them with a complete solution — the iTunes Music Store, the iPod, and FairPlay DRM that was reasonable.

**Walt:** Some people say 99 cents is too much for a single song.

**Steve:** If you buy an album, you're paying about $10-12 for 10-12 songs. That's about a dollar a song. We're just letting you buy one song instead of the whole album. That's more choice, not less value.

### The iPod

**Kara:** The iPod has become a phenomenon. Did you expect this?

**Steve:** We knew it was a great product. We didn't know it would become a cultural phenomenon. The iPod let people carry their entire music library in their pocket. 1,000 songs in your pocket. That was the pitch, and it resonated.

**Walt:** It's more expensive than competitors. Why does it work?

**Steve:** Because it's better. The scroll wheel, the integration with iTunes, the design. People will pay for something that's significantly better. We're not interested in making cheap, mediocre products.

### Apple's Turnaround

**Walt:** When you came back to Apple in 1997, the company was in bad shape. What's changed?

**Steve:** We focused. We had too many products, too many directions. We cut 70% of our product line. We said no to a thousand good ideas so we could say yes to a few great ones.

The key was making great products. The iMac. The PowerBook. Then the iPod. We built things we wanted to use ourselves. That's always been the Apple way.

**Kara:** Were you worried about Microsoft? Windows dominates.

**Steve:** We don't think about beating Microsoft. We think about making great products. If we do that, some people will choose us. We don't need everyone. We just need enough people who care about what we care about — great design, great user experience, attention to detail.

### Innovation and Product Philosophy

**Walt:** Apple is often criticized for being a closed system. What do you say to that?

**Steve:** We care about the user experience from end to end. When you buy an Apple product, we want the whole thing to work beautifully — hardware, software, services. That requires integration. It's not about being closed; it's about being complete.

**Kara:** Where do you see Apple in 5 years?

**Steve:** Doing what we do. Making great products that people love. I don't know exactly what they'll be. But they'll be things we want to use ourselves. That's our compass.

### On Being CEO

**Walt:** You're both CEO of Apple and head of Pixar. How do you balance that?

**Steve:** They feed each other. At Pixar, I learned about storytelling, about technology serving creative goals. At Apple, I apply those lessons. And what I learn at Apple about consumer technology helps at Pixar.

**Kara:** Is it exhausting?

**Steve:** It's energizing. I'm doing what I love. Both companies are making things that matter. That's what keeps me going.

### On the Digital Hub Strategy

**Walt:** You've talked about the Mac as a digital hub. What does that mean?

**Steve:** The Mac is the center of your digital life. Your music, your photos, your movies, your documents — all flowing through your Mac. The iPod is an extension. Future devices will be too. The Mac orchestrates everything.

**Kara:** So you're not giving up on the Mac?

**Steve:** Absolutely not. The Mac is our foundation. Everything else we do builds on it. The iPod needs a Mac. iTunes runs on a Mac. The Mac is essential.

### Retail Stores

**Walt:** You've opened Apple retail stores. Many thought that was crazy. How are they doing?

**Steve:** Great. Better than we expected. People want to experience our products. They want to touch them, play with them, ask questions. Our stores let them do that in a beautiful environment. We're not just selling; we're teaching and supporting.

**Kara:** Are they profitable?

**Steve:** Yes. And they're a great way to control the customer experience. From the moment someone walks in to when they buy, everything is Apple. That matters.

## Key Insights from D3

### 1. The iTunes Vision
Jobs saw digital music as broken — stealing was too easy, legal options were too hard. iTunes was about fixing that equation.

### 2. Product Over Market Share
"We don't think about beating Microsoft. We think about making great products." This philosophy would define Apple's comeback.

### 3. The Power of "No"
"We said no to a thousand good ideas so we could say yes to a few great ones." Apple's turnaround came from focus, not expansion.

### 4. End-to-End Control
Jobs' defense of Apple's "closed" system: "It's not about being closed; it's about being complete."

### 5. Building What You Want
"We build things we want to use ourselves. That's always been the Apple way." This became Apple's guiding principle.

### 6. Retail as Experience
Apple Stores weren't just about selling — they were about letting people experience products in a controlled, beautiful environment.

### 7. The Digital Hub
The Mac as "center of your digital life" — a strategy that would eventually lead to iPhone and iPad.

### 8. Culture Over Price
"People will pay for something that's significantly better. We're not interested in making cheap, mediocre products."

---

## Context and Impact

This 2003 interview captured Apple at a crucial inflection point:
- iPod was growing but not yet ubiquitous
- iTunes Music Store had just launched (April 2003)
- The iPhone was unimaginable to most
- Apple's retail stores were new and successful
- The company was profitable but still small vs. competitors

Jobs' confidence in this interview was prescient. Within 4 years, the iPhone would launch. Within 7 years, Apple would surpass Microsoft in market cap. Everything Jobs described in this interview — focus, great products, user experience — would pay off spectacularly.
