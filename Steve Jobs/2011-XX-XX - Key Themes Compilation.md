## Core Ideas That Repeat Across Interviews

### 1. Build What You Want to Use

**First appearance:** D5 Interview (2007), recounted by Bill Gates  
**Most detailed:** Lost Interview (1995), D3 (2003)

> "We build the products that we want to use ourselves." — Bill Gates quoting Jobs at D5

**Why this matters:**
- Apple's entire product philosophy flows from this
- Creates authenticity in product decisions
- Explains why Apple didn't do focus groups — they were the focus group
- Products reflect genuine user needs, not market research

**See also:**
- `2007-05-30 - D5 Interview with Bill Gates.md`
- `1995-XX-XX - The Lost Interview.md`
- `2003-05-30 - D3 First All Things D Interview.md`

### 2. Focus Is About Saying No

**First appearance:** D3 (2003)  
**Most powerful expression:** Stanford Commencement (2005)

> "We said no to a thousand good ideas so we could say yes to a few great ones." — D3, 2003

> "I'm as proud of what we don't do as what we do." — Various interviews

**Key points:**
- Apple's turnaround came from eliminating 70% of product lines
- Every product requires focus and resources
- Saying "no" protects quality
- The discipline of exclusion

**See also:**
- `2003-05-30 - D3 First All Things D Interview.md`
- `2005-06-12 - Stanford Commencement Address.md`

### 3. Taste Matters

**First appearance:** Lost Interview (1995)  
**Most detailed:** Lost Interview, D5

> "The difference between the average and the best in most things is 20% to 30%. In software the difference between average and the best is 50 to 1. Maybe 100 to 1." — Lost Interview

> "He pursued that with incredible taste and elegance." — Bill Gates at D5

**Key insights:**
- Taste is not teachable — you have it or you don't
- The impact of excellence is exponential in software
- Good taste creates emotional connection to products
- Design is not just how it looks, but how it works

**See also:**
- `1995-XX-XX - The Lost Interview.md`

### 4. Death as Life's Change Agent

**First appearance:** Stanford Commencement (2005)  
**Most powerful:** Stanford Commencement

> "Death is very likely the single best invention of Life. It is Life's change agent. It clears out the old to make way for the new." — Stanford, 2005

> "If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I am about to do today?" — Stanford, 2005

**Framework:**
- Memento mori (remember you will die) as motivation
- Fear of death clears away external expectations
- "You are already naked" — nothing to lose by following your heart
- Remembering death prevents living someone else's life

**See also:**
- `2005-06-12 - Stanford Commencement Address.md`

### 5. Connecting the Dots

**First appearance:** Stanford Commencement (2005)

> "You can't connect the dots looking forward; you can only connect them looking backward. So you have to trust that the dots will somehow connect in your future." — Stanford, 2005

**Key insight:**
- Trust in the process, even when the path isn't clear
- Dropping out led to calligraphy led to Mac typography
- Curiosity without immediate purpose pays off
- Faith in the ultimate coherence of your path

**See also:**
- `2005-06-12 - Stanford Commencement Address.md`

### 6. The Blue Box Mindset

**First appearance:** Lost Interview (1995)

> "What we learned was that we could build something ourselves that could control billions of dollars' worth of infrastructure in the world. That was what we learned. We didn't know much. We could build a little thing that could control a giant thing. And that was an incredible lesson. I don't think there would have ever been an Apple computer had there not been blue boxing." — Lost Interview

**Core insight:**
- Small things can control big things
- Smart, clever products can have outsized impact
- This mindset led to Mac, iPod, iPhone
- Empowerment through technology

**See also:**
- `1995-XX-XX - The Lost Interview.md`

### 7. Product vs. Profit

**First appearance:** Lost Interview (1995)  
**Throughout all interviews**

> "I don't think about the money. I never have. I think about the product. If you build a great product, the profits will follow." — Lost Interview paraphrase

**Key distinction:**
- Start with great product, not business model
- Profit is a lagging indicator of product quality
- "We're just trying to make great products" — repeated at D8
- Money follows excellence

**See also:**
- All interviews — this is consistent across 20 years

### 8. The Importance of Great People

**First appearance:** Lost Interview (1995)  
**Most explicit:** Lost Interview

> "I have built a lot of my success off finding these truly gifted people." — Lost Interview

> "The difference between average and the best is 50 to 1 in software." — Lost Interview

**Hiring philosophy:**
- A small team of A+ players beats a large team of B players
- Great people create leverage
- Mediocrity is toxic to product quality
- Recruitment is the CEO's most important job

**See also:**
- `1995-XX-XX - The Lost Interview.md`

### 9. Craftsmanship Over Ideas

**First appearance:** Lost Interview (1995)

> "There's a tremendous amount of craftsmanship in between a great idea and a great product." — Lost Interview

> "Designing a product is keeping five thousand things in your brain — these concepts — and fitting them all together in new and different ways to get what you want." — Lost Interview

**Product development insight:**
- Ideas are 10%; execution is 90%
- The "magic" is in the iteration
- Thousands of tradeoffs in every product
- Details matter enormously

**See also:**
- `1995-XX-XX - The Lost Interview.md`

### 10. Defiance and Confidence

**Throughout all interviews, but especially:**
- D8 (2010): "My sex life is great, how's yours?"
- iPhone keynote: "Who wants a stylus? Yuck."
- All keynotes: Dismissing competitors

**Pattern:**
- Confidence bordering on arrogance
- Defiance of conventional wisdom
- Willingness to bet against the market
- Contempt for mediocre products

**See also:**
- `2010-06-01 - D8 Interview with Walt Mossberg.md`
- `2007-01-09 - iPhone Keynote Macworld.md`

### 11. The Post-PC Era

**First appearance:** D8 (2010)

> "PCs are going to be like trucks. They're still going to be around, they're still going to have a lot of value, but they're going to be used by one out of X people." — D8, 2010

**Vision:**
- Tablets as cars, PCs as trucks
- Most people don't need the full power of a PC
- Specialized tools for specialists; simple tools for everyone
- The future is mobile, intuitive, appliance-like

**See also:**
- `2010-06-01 - D8 Interview with Walt Mossberg.md`

### 12. Control the Experience

**First appearance:** D3 (2003)  
**Most detailed:** D8 (2010)

> "It's not about being closed; it's about being complete." — D3, 2003

> "We care about the user experience from end to end." — D3, 2003

**Philosophy:**
- Hardware + software + services = integrated experience
- End-to-end control enables quality
- "Complete" beats "open"
- Users don't want to manage complexity

**See also:**
- `2003-05-30 - D3 First All Things D Interview.md`

## Jobs' Evolution Across Time

### 1995 (Lost Interview): The Craftsman
- Focus on product development process
- Emphasis on taste and excellence
- Reflection on being fired from Apple
- Contemplative, less polished

### 2003 (D3): The Turnaround Artist
- Justifying the Apple strategy
- Explaining iTunes and iPod
- Confidence growing but not yet dominant
- Still explaining Apple to skeptics

### 2005 (Stanford): The Philosopher
- Most personal and reflective
- Death and meaning
- Connecting dots backward
- Stay hungry, stay foolish

### 2007 (D5, iPhone): The Revolutionary
- Launching the iPhone
- Historic appearance with Gates
- Confidence at peak
- "Reinventing the phone"

### 2010 (D8): The Defiant Visionary
- Unapologetic about Flash
- Dismissing competition
- Peak "Steve mode" — combative, confident
- Last major interview appearance

---

## Consistent Patterns

**What never changed:**
1. Product obsession
2. Contempt for mediocrity
3. Belief in small teams of great people
4. End-to-end control philosophy
5. Building what he wanted to use
6. Defiance of conventional wisdom

**What evolved:**
1. From explaining Apple to defining the industry
2. From underdog to dominant player
3. From explaining decisions to asserting them
4. From collaborative (with Gates) to combative (with Google/Adobe)
